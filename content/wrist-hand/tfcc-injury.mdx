# TFCC Injury (Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex)

## Overview & Pathophysiology

The **Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex (TFCC)** is the primary stabiliser of the **distal radioulnar joint (DRUJ)** and the **ulnocarpal joint**, and acts as a load-transmitter distributing approximately **20% of axial load** across the ulnar side of the wrist (Palmer & Werner, 1984). Injury is a common cause of ulnar-sided wrist pain.

**Anatomical components:**
- **Articular disc (fibrocartilage):** Central avascular zone; periphery is vascular
- **Dorsal and volar radioulnar ligaments:** Deep fibres insert at fovea (primary DRUJ stabilisers); superficial fibres insert at ulnar styloid
- **Ulnotriquetral (UT) ligament**
- **Ulnolunate (UL) ligament**
- **Extensor carpi ulnaris (ECU) subsheath**
- **Meniscus homologue**

**Vascular anatomy (surgical relevance):**
- Central disc = avascular; poor healing potential; debridement is treatment of choice
- Peripheral (1–2 mm) = vascular; repair is feasible
- Deep fibres at fovea = critical stabilisers; foveal detachment = DRUJ instability

**Mechanism of injury:**
- **Traumatic (Palmer Type I):** Fall on outstretched hand; forceful forearm rotation; axial loading with ulnar deviation; DRUJ dislocation; distal radius fracture (associated)
- **Degenerative (Palmer Type II):** Repetitive loading; ulnar impaction syndrome; abutment of ulnar head against TFCC/lunate/triquetrum
- **Ulnar variance:** Positive ulnar variance increases load through TFCC; associated with degenerative tears

**Epidemiology:**
- Common cause of ulnar wrist pain; exact incidence unclear
- **Wrist arthroscopy series:** TFCC tears found in ~35% of arthroscopies for wrist pain; 53% in distal radius fractures
- Degenerative (Type II) tears increasingly common >40 years; frequent incidental finding

## Special Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| **Fovea sign** (deep palpation between FCU tendon and ulnar styloid in anatomical snuffbox equivalent; positive if reproduces deep ulnar wrist pain) | **0.95** | **0.87** | 7.3 | 0.06 | Moderate (Tay et al., 2007) |
| **Press test** (patient presses up from chair arms using wrists; positive if ulnar wrist pain reproduced) | **1.00** | **0.64** | 2.8 | 0.00 | Moderate (Lester et al., 1995) — caution: high sensitivity, poor specificity |
| **Ulnar grind test** (axial compression + rotation of wrist in ulnar deviation; positive if pain reproduced at DRUJ/ulnocarpal) | ~0.54 | ~0.67 | 1.6 | 0.69 | Low (Nakamura et al.) |
| **DRUJ ballottement (piano key test)** (dorsopalmar stress of ulnar head vs radius; positive if laxity, pain, or crepitus vs contralateral) | ~0.66 | ~0.64 | 1.8 | 0.53 | Low-moderate |
| **Ulnocarpal stress test** (maximal ulnar deviation + axial compression + forearm rotation; positive = ulnar pain) | ~0.64 | ~0.65 | 1.8 | 0.55 | Low |
| **ECU synergy test** (resisted index finger abduction while palpating ECU; positive if ECU pain/weakness) | ~0.82 | ~0.81 | 4.3 | 0.22 | Low-moderate (differentiate ECU) |

> **Clinical interpretation:** The **fovea sign** has the best combined sensitivity/specificity and should be the primary provocative test. **Press test** is highly sensitive (rules out if negative) but non-specific. Use a cluster: fovea sign + press test + piano key (for DRUJ stability) + active forearm rotation assessment.

> **DRUJ instability assessment:** Compare ballottement laxity bilaterally in neutral, pronation, and supination. Laxity in all positions = physiological; laxity in only one = pathological.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Urgent onward referral if:

- **Acute DRUJ dislocation** (inability to pronate/supinate; prominent ulnar head; post-trauma) → Emergency Department / Orthopaedics same day
- **Galeazzi fracture-dislocation** (distal radius fracture + DRUJ disruption) → Orthopaedic emergency
- **Essex-Lopresti injury** (radial head fracture + interosseous membrane disruption + DRUJ injury) → Orthopaedic emergency
- **Vascular compromise** (absent ulnar pulse; Allen's test positive for ulnar artery) → Vascular surgery
- **Acute ulnar nerve deficit** → Urgent neurological assessment
- **Suspected carpal instability** (perilunate dislocation; scapholunate dissociation on plain X-ray) → Urgent orthopaedic referral

## Clinical Frameworks

### Palmer Classification

**Type I — Traumatic:**

| Subtype | Lesion |
|---------|--------|
| **IA** | Central perforation (articular disc) — avascular; debridement |
| **IB** | Ulnar avulsion ± ulnar styloid fracture — peripheral; can repair; foveal detachment = DRUJ instability |
| **IC** | Volar avulsion (ulnocarpal ligaments) |
| **ID** | Radial avulsion ± sigmoid notch fracture |

**Type II — Degenerative (Ulnar Impaction Syndrome):**

| Subtype | Lesion |
|---------|--------|
| **IIA** | TFCC wear — no perforation |
| **IIB** | TFCC wear + lunate/ulnar chondromalacia |
| **IIC** | TFCC perforation + chondromalacia |
| **IID** | TFCC perforation + chondromalacia + LT ligament tear |
| **IIE** | TFCC perforation + chondromalacia + LT tear + ulnocarpal arthritis |

> **Palmer classification guides arthroscopic management** — Type IA: debridement; Type IB: repair; Type IIE: ulnar shortening osteotomy may be required.

### Diagnostic Algorithm
1. Ulnar-sided wrist pain + mechanism + provocative tests
2. Plain X-ray (bilateral neutral PA): assess ulnar variance; exclude fracture
3. **If diagnosis unclear:** MRI wrist ± arthrography
4. **If surgical management planned:** MR arthrography (gold standard non-invasive); wrist arthroscopy (definitive)

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Plain Radiography
- **Bilateral neutral PA wrists:** Assess ulnar variance (positive >2 mm associated with degenerative TFCC)
- Assess DRUJ congruence; carpal alignment (Gilula's arcs)
- Pronated-grip PA view increases positive variance by ~1–2 mm

### MRI Wrist
- **Standard MRI:** Sensitivity 0.71–0.86, specificity 0.67–0.89 for TFCC tears (meta-analysis, Saupe et al.)
- 3T superior to 1.5T; dedicated wrist coil essential
- Identifies peripheral vs central tears, associated pathology (LT ligament, ulnocarpal)

### MR Arthrography (MRA)
- **MRA:** Sensitivity ~0.84–0.93, specificity ~0.93–0.95 for full-thickness TFCC tears
- **Gold standard non-invasive investigation** — preferred pre-arthroscopy
- Intra-articular gadolinium (radiocarpal injection); contrast leak into DRUJ confirms perforation
- Differentiates partial from full-thickness; better assessment of foveal detachment

### CT / CT Arthrography
- DRUJ geometry assessment pre-operative; instability evaluation
- CT arthrography: comparable sensitivity to MRA; useful when MRI contraindicated

### Wrist Arthroscopy (Definitive)
- Gold standard for diagnosis and treatment
- Allows direct visualisation of all TFCC components; dynamic assessment; treatment in same setting
- Trampoline test (probe bounce on articular disc) evaluates tension; abnormal if disc lax

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| **ECU tendinopathy/subluxation** | ECU synergy test; pain dorsal ulnar; ECU groove tenderness; USS |
| **Lunotriquetral (LT) ligament tear** | Shuck test (LT ballottement); ulnar dorsal pain; MRA |
| **Ulnocarpal impaction syndrome** | Positive ulnar variance; degenerative; X-ray; axial loading pain |
| **Pisotriquetral arthritis** | Pisiform tenderness; pain on radial deviation + compression; X-ray |
| **Ulnar styloid non-union** | Post-fracture; X-ray; tenderness at styloid |
| **Hypothenar hammer syndrome** | Vascular; repetitive hypothenar trauma; abnormal Allen's test; duplex USS |
| **Guyon's canal syndrome** | Ulnar nerve compression; sensory loss 4th/5th digits; Tinel's |
| **De Quervain's tenosynovitis** | Radial-sided pain; Finkelstein positive |
| **Distal radius malunion** | Post-fracture; radiological |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Hand Therapy Pathway

> **Primary care / Physiotherapy → MSK imaging → Specialist Hand Surgery referral for surgical cases**

**Step 1 — Initial Assessment:**
- Confirm ulnar-sided wrist pain; mechanism; provocative tests (fovea sign, press test, piano key)
- Palmer classification (clinical working diagnosis)
- Baseline PRWE + QuickDASH + grip strength + forearm rotation goniometry
- Plain X-ray (bilateral PA wrists — request if not done)

**Step 2 — Conservative management (6–8 weeks; appropriate for most Type I uncomplicated, Type II non-surgical):**
- DRUJ immobilisation (Munster cast or rigid DRUJ orthosis 6–8 weeks for acute peripheral tears)
- Activity modification: avoid forearm rotation under load; gripping
- Graded hand therapy after immobilisation

**Step 3 — Physiotherapy programme (post-immobilisation or degenerative):**
- DRUJ stabilisation exercises; ECU strengthening
- Forearm rotation restoration; proprioception training
- Theraband graded resisted pronation/supination
- Typical programme: 6–12 weeks physiotherapy post-immobilisation

**Step 4 — MRI/MRA (if not improving at 8–12 weeks conservative, or acute high-energy mechanism):**
- Request via MSK interface / T&O outpatient
- Guide surgical decision-making

**Step 5 — Surgical Referral (DRUJ instability; failed conservative; acute Type IB foveal detachment):**
- HSC NI Hand Surgery / Trauma & Orthopaedics
- Include ulnar variance, Palmer type (clinical), MRI findings in referral

---

### Conservative Management

**Immobilisation:**
- **Acute peripheral tears (Type IB):** Below-elbow Munster cast or custom rigid DRUJ orthosis in neutral rotation × 6–8 weeks
- Evidence: Minimal RCT data; case series demonstrate 60–75% satisfactory outcomes with conservative management of peripheral tears
- **Type IA (central):** Conservative has lower success; shorter immobilisation (2–4 weeks) then physiotherapy; arthroscopic debridement if persistent

**Physiotherapy:**
- ECU subsheath repair / DRUJ stabilisation via dynamic muscle exercise
- Evidence limited (case series); consensus recommends graded loading programme
- Duration: 6–12 weeks post-immobilisation

**Corticosteroid Injection:**
- USS-guided injection into radiocarpal joint ± DRUJ
- Limited specific evidence; may reduce pain in degenerative Type II
- Refer to qualified injection physiotherapist / GP-with-SI

---

### Surgical Options

| Procedure | Indication | Outcomes | Evidence Level |
|-----------|------------|----------|----------------|
| **Arthroscopic debridement** | Type IA (central tear) | ~78–88% good/excellent at 2 years | Moderate (case series) |
| **Arthroscopic peripheral repair** | Type IB (peripheral tear; DRUJ stable) | ~80–85% good/excellent; return to sport/work | Moderate (case series) |
| **Foveal reattachment (open or arthroscopic)** | Type IB with foveal detachment + DRUJ instability | ~80–90% DRUJ stability restored | Moderate |
| **Ulnar shortening osteotomy (USO)** | Type II degenerative + positive ulnar variance | ~75–90% pain improvement | Moderate |
| **DRUJ reconstruction** | Chronic instability; failed repair; salvage | Variable | Low |

### Onward Referral Criteria

| Trigger | Referral Destination |
|---------|---------------------|
| Acute DRUJ dislocation / Galeazzi / Essex-Lopresti | Emergency Department — same day |
| DRUJ instability confirmed (clinical) | HSC NI Hand Surgery — urgent (2 weeks) |
| Failed conservative ≥8–12 weeks | MSK Radiology (MRA) → Hand Surgery |
| Positive ulnar variance + degenerative symptoms | Hand Surgery / MSK interface discussion |
| Vascular compromise | Vascular Surgery — urgent |
