# Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

## Overview & Pathophysiology

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) is the most common peripheral entrapment neuropathy, affecting 3–6% of the general population and up to 15% of specific occupational groups. It results from compression of the median nerve as it passes through the carpal tunnel — a rigid fibro-osseous canal bounded by the carpal bones posteriorly and the flexor retinaculum anteriorly.

**Pathophysiological mechanisms:**
- Elevated intracarpal tunnel pressure (normal ~7 mmHg; symptomatic CTS typically >30 mmHg)
- Ischaemia of the median nerve epineurium → demyelination → axonal degeneration in severe cases
- Mechanical deformation of axons with repetitive wrist flexion/extension
- Inflammatory oedema of tenosynovium (especially in secondary CTS)

**Risk factors:**
- Female sex (3:1 female:male ratio); peak incidence 40–60 years
- Pregnancy (transient, resolves postpartum in ~50%)
- Obesity (BMI >30 doubles risk)
- Diabetes mellitus (metabolic neuropathy compounds compression)
- Hypothyroidism, rheumatoid arthritis, acromegaly
- Repetitive hand/wrist use (vibrating tools, keyboard work — moderate evidence)
- Previous distal radius fracture (Colles' fracture)
- Haemodialysis (amyloid deposition)

**Anatomical variants:** Bifid median nerve and persistent median artery increase compression risk and complicate surgical release.

## Special Tests

> All values referenced against nerve conduction studies (NCS) as gold standard unless otherwise stated.

| Test | How to Perform | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| **Phalen's Test** | Passive wrist flexion ≥60° for 60 sec; positive = symptom reproduction | 0.68 | 0.73 | 2.52 | 0.44 | High (meta-analysis, Wilder 2010) |
| **Tinel's Sign** | Percussion over carpal tunnel at wrist crease; positive = paraesthesia in median distribution | 0.50 | 0.77 | 2.17 | 0.65 | High (meta-analysis, Wilder 2010) |
| **Durkan's Carpal Compression Test** | Direct thumb pressure over carpal tunnel for 30 sec; positive = symptom reproduction | 0.87 | 0.90 | 8.70 | 0.14 | High (Durkan 1991; replicated Gonzalez 2002) |
| **Hand Elevation Test** | Both hands elevated above head for 2 min; positive = symptom reproduction | 0.75 | 0.98 | 37.5 | 0.26 | Moderate (Ahn 2001) |
| **Flick Sign** | Patient reports relief of nocturnal symptoms by flicking/shaking the hand | 0.93 | 0.96 | 23.3 | 0.07 | Moderate (Pryse-Phillips 1984) |
| **Two-Point Discrimination** | Static 2PD >6 mm on volar fingertip (median distribution) | 0.33 | 0.99 | 33.0 | 0.68 | Moderate (severe/advanced CTS) |
| **Thenar Wasting** | Visual/palpable atrophy of APB | 0.19 | 0.99 | 19.0 | 0.82 | Low (late sign — indicates axonal loss) |

**Clinical note:** Durkan's compression test has the best combination of sensitivity and specificity among physical examination tests. The Hand Elevation Test has exceptional specificity (0.98) — a positive result is strongly predictive. The Flick Sign, while poorly studied, has high reported accuracy and is highly patient-specific.

**Sensory distribution to confirm:** Thumb, index, middle finger, radial half of ring finger (palmar surface). Dorsal surface of distal phalanges. Sparing of thenar eminence (palmar cutaneous branch exits proximal to carpal tunnel).

## Red Flags

> Immediate escalation required if any present

- **Thenar muscle wasting** — indicates chronic axonal loss; urgent NCS + surgical referral
- **Complete median nerve motor loss** (APB weakness, inability to oppose) — urgent referral
- **Bilateral acute onset** — consider cervical myelopathy, systemic neuropathy
- **Night pain with constitutional symptoms** (weight loss, fever) — exclude malignancy
- **Rapidly progressive neurological deficit** — acute compression (e.g., post-fracture haematoma); surgical emergency
- **Vascular compromise** — pallor, pulselessness — exclude thoracic outlet syndrome or vascular pathology
- **Signs inconsistent with CTS** — consider double crush, brachial neuritis, MS

## Clinical Frameworks

### CTS-6 Clinical Prediction Rule
*(Graham et al., 2006 — validated diagnostic prediction tool)*

Score 1 point for each present:

| Item | Points |
|------|--------|
| Nocturnal numbness | 1 |
| Shaking relieves symptoms (Flick sign) | 1 |
| Thenar atrophy | 1 |
| Positive Phalen's test | 1 |
| Loss of 2-point discrimination (>6mm) | 1 |
| Wrist ratio >0.67 (AP:ML diameter) | 1 |

**Interpretation:**
- ≥4 points: CTS highly likely (LR+ = 18.3) — NCS may be deferred; treat clinically
- 3 points: Uncertain — NCS recommended
- ≤2 points: CTS unlikely (LR– = 0.09) — consider alternative diagnosis

### NCS/EMG Diagnostic Criteria
*(American Association of Neuromuscular & Electrodiagnostic Medicine — AANEM)*

**Diagnostic threshold for abnormal NCS:**
- Median sensory distal latency >3.5 ms (14 cm distance)
- Median motor distal latency >4.2 ms (to APB)
- Median-ulnar sensory latency difference >0.4 ms (ring finger comparison)
- Median-radial sensory latency difference >0.5 ms (thumb comparison)

**Severity grading (Stevens 1997):**

| Grade | NCS Findings |
|-------|-------------|
| Mild | Sensory latency prolonged only |
| Moderate | Both sensory and motor latency prolonged |
| Severe | Absent sensory response ± reduced motor amplitude |
| Extreme | Absent sensory and motor responses; denervation on EMG |

**HSC NI NCS Referral:** NCS arranged via neurology/neurophysiology; await results before surgical referral in non-urgent cases. Urgent track for progressive motor loss.

### USS Criteria
- Cross-sectional area (CSA) of median nerve at pisiform level ≥10 mm² = abnormal (sensitivity 0.87, specificity 0.83)
- CSA ≥12 mm² has higher specificity (0.94) — useful in equivocal cases
- USS preferred in pregnancy (avoids NCS); also useful for anatomical variants pre-surgery

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**Diagnostic algorithm (adapted from NICE NG54 and AANEM guidelines):**

1. **Clinical diagnosis first** — history + physical examination sufficient in classic presentation
   - Nocturnal paraesthesia, flick sign, median nerve distribution symptoms → high pre-test probability
   
2. **Apply CTS-6** — score ≥4: treat clinically; score 3: NCS; score ≤2: reconsider diagnosis

3. **NCS indicated when:**
   - Diagnosis uncertain after clinical assessment
   - Atypical features (e.g., whole-hand numbness, proximal symptoms)
   - Surgical planning (baseline severity grading)
   - Medicolegal / occupational health documentation
   - Failed conservative treatment (reassess severity)

4. **USS as adjunct** when:
   - NCS unavailable or inconclusive
   - Pregnancy
   - Anatomical variant suspected
   - Space-occupying lesion possible (lipoma, ganglion, amyloid)

**Diagnostic accuracy of clinical assessment alone:**
- Experienced clinician with classic history: sensitivity 0.85, specificity 0.80 (Fowler 2002)
- Combined test battery (≥2 positive tests) improves specificity substantially

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features | Investigation |
|-----------|----------------------------|---------------|
| **Cervical radiculopathy (C6)** | Neck pain, shoulder/arm radiation, positive Spurling's, symptoms in thumb/index but also dorsal | MRI cervical spine; NCS (normal distal latencies) |
| **Pronator teres syndrome** | Proximal forearm pain, reproduction with resisted pronation, positive Tinel's at pronator, palmar cutaneous branch involved | NCS (abnormal proximal); USS forearm |
| **Thoracic outlet syndrome** | Bilateral, positional, Adson's/ROOS test, vascular component | Vascular duplex; chest X-ray (cervical rib) |
| **Ulnar neuropathy (cubital tunnel)** | Little/ring finger numbness, intrinsic weakness, Froment's sign, Tinel's at elbow | NCS (ulnar motor/sensory) |
| **De Quervain's tenosynovitis** | Radial-sided wrist pain, Finkelstein's positive, no sensory change | Clinical; USS |
| **Raynaud's phenomenon** | Colour change (white → blue → red), cold trigger, bilateral, no neurological deficit | Nailfold capillaroscopy; ANA if secondary suspected |
| **Diabetic peripheral neuropathy** | Stocking-glove distribution, bilateral, distal symmetrical | HbA1c; NCS |
| **Double crush syndrome** | CTS + proximal nerve compression (C6 radiculopathy); amplified symptoms | Full NCS; MRI spine |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Pathway

```
Clinical diagnosis of CTS
         │
         ▼
Mild–Moderate (no motor loss, <12 months) ──► Conservative (6 weeks)
         │                                          │
         │                                    Review at 6 weeks
         │                                    ├── Resolved → Discharge
Moderate–Severe or failed conservative      └── Persistent → NCS + consider injection
         │
         ▼
NCS confirmed ──► Surgical referral (hand surgery)
         │
Progressive motor loss / thenar wasting ──► URGENT referral (2-week track)
```

### Conservative Management

**Splinting (strong evidence — Cochrane 2012):**
- Wrist splint in neutral position (0°) worn nocturnally
- 3–6 months trial recommended
- Full-time wear more effective than night-only in early stages
- Custom thermoplastic vs prefabricated: equivalent outcomes at 1 year (Brininger 2007)
- NNT for symptom relief: ~3 at 3 months

**Exercise (emerging evidence):**
- Nerve gliding exercises: equivocal evidence; may reduce need for surgery in mild CTS
- Tendon gliding: reduces tendon adhesion, improves flexor tendon excursion
- Programme: 5 × daily, 10 repetitions each exercise

**Activity modification:**
- Avoid sustained wrist flexion/extension >30°
- Vibrating tool exposure reduction
- Ergonomic keyboard/mouse assessment (HSC OT referral if occupational)

**LLLT/Ultrasound therapy:**
- Therapeutic ultrasound: some evidence for short-term symptom relief (Ebenbichler 1998); effect modest
- LLLT: insufficient evidence; not routinely recommended

### Pharmacological Management

**Corticosteroid injection (strong evidence):**
- Methylprednisolone 20–40 mg + 1% lidocaine, palmar approach at carpal tunnel
- NNT for symptom relief at 1 month: 2.0 (Armstrong 2004)
- Benefit wanes at 3 months; 50% recurrence by 6 months
- Maximum 2 injections recommended (risk of median nerve damage, tendon rupture)
- Diagnostic role: short-term response predicts good surgical outcome
- **Technique:** Needle insertion at proximal wrist crease, ulnar to palmaris longus, directed 30° distally; USS-guided preferred to avoid median nerve injury

**Oral corticosteroids:**
- Short-term relief (prednisone 20 mg/day × 2 weeks): evidence limited
- Not recommended for long-term management; significant systemic side effects

**NSAIDs:** No significant evidence for benefit over placebo in CTS; may address coexisting tendinopathy

**Diuretics (in pregnancy):** Not recommended; limited evidence

**Vitamin B6:** Insufficient evidence; not recommended

### Surgical Management (Referral Criteria)

**Indications for surgical referral:**
- Failed ≥6 weeks conservative management (splinting ± injection)
- Moderate–severe NCS findings
- Thenar wasting or progressive motor deficit (urgent)
- Patient preference after informed discussion of risks/benefits

**Surgical options:**
- Open carpal tunnel release (OCTR): gold standard; recurrence <5% at 5 years
- Endoscopic carpal tunnel release (ECTR): equivalent outcomes; faster return to work; higher risk of transient nerve injury
- Mini-open: intermediate approach

**Post-operative physiotherapy:**
- Scar desensitisation, grip strengthening, oedema management
- Return to light work: 2–4 weeks; heavy manual work: 6–12 weeks

### NICE Pathway (NG54 / CKS)
- Diagnose clinically; NCS if uncertainty or surgical planning
- Offer splinting and/or steroid injection first-line
- Refer for surgery if conservative measures fail after 3 months or if motor involvement
- Refer urgently if thenar wasting or severe NCS
