# Trigger Finger (Stenosing Tenosynovitis)

## Overview & Pathophysiology

Trigger finger (stenosing tenosynovitis) results from a **size mismatch between the flexor tendon and the A1 pulley** at the level of the MCP joint. Repetitive microtrauma causes fibrocartilaginous metaplasia and hypertrophy of the A1 pulley, while the flexor tendon develops a fusiform nodule proximal to the pulley. This produces the characteristic catching, triggering, or locking of the digit in flexion.

**Anatomy:**
- **A1 pulley** overlies the MCP joint; most commonly affected
- Flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS) and profundus (FDP) pass through the fibro-osseous canal
- Tendon nodule forms proximal to A1 pulley; catches on pulley margins during gliding

**Epidemiology:**
- General population incidence: 28 per 100,000 person-years
- Peak age: 55–60 years; females > males (2:1)
- **Ring finger** most commonly affected, followed by thumb (trigger thumb)
- Strong association with:
  - Diabetes mellitus (10× increased risk; 10–20% of diabetics affected)
  - Rheumatoid arthritis
  - Hypothyroidism
  - Carpal tunnel syndrome (co-occurrence ~10–20%)
  - Repetitive gripping activities

**Trigger thumb** (A1 pulley of thumb flexor) behaves similarly; note distinction from paediatric trigger thumb (congenital form with Notta's node).

## Special Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| **Triggering/locking on active flexion/extension** | ~0.92 | ~0.95 | 18.4 | 0.08 | Moderate |
| **Palpable nodule at A1 pulley (MCP palmar crease)** | ~0.75–0.80 | ~0.90 | 7.5 | 0.25 | Low-moderate |
| **Tenderness over A1 pulley** | ~0.90 | 0.65 | 2.6 | 0.15 | Low |
| **Crepitus on tendon gliding** | ~0.50 | ~0.80 | 2.5 | 0.63 | Low |

> **Diagnosis is predominantly clinical** — active triggering or locking of the digit during flexion/extension, combined with palmar MCP tenderness and a palpable nodule, is sufficient for diagnosis. Imaging is not routinely required.

> **USS** can confirm nodule size, pulley thickening, and tendon morphology — useful pre-injection or when diagnosis is uncertain. Characteristic USS finding: hypoechoic nodule proximal to A1 pulley with restricted tendon glide on dynamic assessment.

## Green Classification (Severity)

| Grade | Description | Clinical Features |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Grade I** | Pre-triggering | Pain; history of catching but no demonstrable triggering on examination; tenderness over A1 pulley |
| **Grade II** | Active triggering | Demonstrable triggering/snapping on active flexion-extension; patient can actively extend digit |
| **Grade III** | Locked | Digit locked in flexion; patient cannot actively extend; requires passive correction (**IIIa**) or cannot be corrected passively (**IIIb**) |
| **Grade IV** | Contracture | Fixed flexion contracture at PIP joint in addition to locking |

> **Clinical relevance:** Grade I–IIIa respond well to corticosteroid injection. Grade IIIb–IV typically require surgical intervention. Grade IV may require additional PIP joint contracture release.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Urgent/priority onward referral if:

- **Acute locked digit unable to extend passively** + fever/erythema → **Septic flexor tenosynovitis** — surgical emergency (Kanavel's cardinal signs: fusiform swelling, flexed posture at rest, pain on passive extension, tenderness over tendon sheath)
- **Traumatic mechanism** with locked digit → exclude volar plate injury, fracture, or dislocation
- **Rapidly progressive, bilateral triggering** with joint swelling → inflammatory arthropathy (RA)
- **Paediatric locked thumb** → congenital trigger thumb — paediatric orthopaedic referral
- **Firm, fixed nodule** not consistent with tendon — consider giant cell tumour of tendon sheath (GCT); USS ± referral

## Clinical Frameworks

### Diagnostic Framework
1. **History:** Catching, triggering, locking; worse in morning; pain at palm/MCP
2. **Examination:** Active triggering; palpable nodule; A1 pulley tenderness; assess for locked digit
3. **Green classification** to guide management
4. **Comorbidity screen:** Diabetes, RA, hypothyroidism
5. **Imaging:** USS if diagnosis uncertain or pre-procedural planning

### Special Considerations
- **Diabetic patients:** Lower injection success rates (50–60% vs ~75% in non-diabetics); higher recurrence; may require earlier surgical referral
- **Multiple digits:** USS of all affected digits; injection of all at one visit appropriate
- **Paediatric trigger thumb:** Different pathology (Notta's node at A1 pulley); spontaneous resolution possible up to age 3; refer paediatric orthopaedics

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### USS Findings (if performed)
- Hypoechoic nodule in flexor tendon proximal to A1 pulley
- A1 pulley thickening (normal: <1 mm; abnormal: >2 mm)
- Restricted tendon glide on dynamic USS with active flexion/extension
- Power Doppler: peritendinous hyperaemia in active inflammation
- USS-guided injection improves accuracy and may improve outcomes

### Laboratory
- No routine bloods required for diagnosis
- **If inflammatory arthropathy suspected:** FBC, ESR, CRP, RF, anti-CCP
- **Diabetes screen** if not known: HbA1c / fasting glucose

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| **Septic flexor tenosynovitis** | Kanavel's signs; fever; systemic illness; surgical emergency |
| **Volar plate injury / PIP dislocation** | Trauma; localised PIP tenderness; X-ray |
| **Dupuytren's contracture** | Progressive painless contracture; palmar cord; no triggering |
| **Giant cell tumour of tendon sheath** | Firm, fixed, non-tender nodule; USS — lobulated, vascular |
| **Flexor tendon rupture (RA)** | Inability to flex; RA history; Mannerfelt syndrome |
| **Locked MCP joint** | Sesamoid entrapment; X-ray shows sesamoid between metacarpal heads |
| **Camptodactyly** | Congenital or juvenile; painless PIP flexion deformity; no triggering |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Hand Therapy Pathway

> **Primary care → Physiotherapy/Hand Therapy → Injection → Surgical referral**

**Step 1 — Initial Assessment:**
- Confirm diagnosis + Green grade
- Screen for comorbidities (diabetes, RA)
- Baseline QuickDASH + grip/pinch strength

**Step 2 — Conservative (Grade I–II; trial 4–6 weeks):**
- Night extension splinting of MCP joint (PIP/DIP free) — reduces morning locking
- Activity modification; avoid sustained gripping/vibration
- Gentle tendon-gliding exercises
- Refer to hand therapist for splint fabrication and exercise programme

**Step 3 — Corticosteroid Injection (Grade I–IIIa; preferred first-line for active triggering):**
- USS-guided into tendon sheath at A1 pulley level
- Refer to qualified physiotherapist (injection) / GP-with-SI / MSK interface clinic
- Second injection if inadequate response at 6–8 weeks

**Step 4 — Surgical Referral (Grade IIIb–IV; failed 2 injections; diabetic recurrence):**
- Percutaneous A1 pulley release or open surgical release
- HSC NI Hand Surgery / T&O referral

---

### Conservative Management

**Night Extension Splinting:**
- MCP joint in 0–15° extension; PIP and DIP joints free
- Reduces triggering by preventing full flexion at rest
- Evidence: Patel & Bassini (1992) — 66% resolution with splinting alone (grade I–II)
- Thermoplastic custom splint via hand therapy or off-the-shelf MCP extension splint

**Tendon Gliding Exercises:**
- Hook fist, straight fist, composite fist positions
- Purpose: maintain tendon excursion, reduce adhesion formation
- Limited high-quality RCT evidence; clinical consensus supports use

---

### Pharmacological

**Corticosteroid Injection (CSI):**
- **Cochrane (Peters-Veluthamaningal et al., 2009):** CSI produces short-term resolution in up to **93%** of cases at 4 weeks; effect diminishes at 6 months (~57% sustained); recurrence rate ~50% at 1 year
- **Best evidence:** Triamcinolone acetonide 10–20 mg (or methylprednisolone 20–40 mg) + 1 mL lidocaine into tendon sheath
- USS-guided injection: higher accuracy, similar outcomes to landmark-guided (RCT evidence limited)
- **Diabetic patients:** ~50–60% short-term success; earlier recurrence; monitor glucose post-injection
- **2nd injection:** Additional 50–60% resolution in those not responding to first
- **3rd injection:** Diminishing returns; proceed to surgical referral

**NSAIDs:** Topical or oral for pain; limited specific evidence in trigger finger; adjunct only

---

### Surgical Options

| Procedure | Description | Recurrence Rate | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| **Percutaneous A1 pulley release** | Needle or blade via palmar approach; outpatient | <3–5% | Avoid in thumb (radial digital nerve proximity); USS-guided improves safety |
| **Open A1 pulley release** | Direct visualisation; definitive | <2–3% | Gold standard; day case; general/regional anaesthesia |
| **Endoscopic release** | Less common in UK practice | Similar to open | — |

> **Recurrence post-surgery** is rare (<5%); if recurrence occurs, consider incomplete pulley release or alternative diagnosis.

### Onward Referral Criteria

| Trigger | Referral Destination |
|---------|---------------------|
| Septic tenosynovitis (suspected) | Emergency Department — same day |
| Active triggering (Grade II–IIIa) | Injection service (physio with injection competency / GP-with-SI) |
| Grade IIIb–IV at presentation | HSC NI Hand Surgery (direct) |
| Failed 2 injections | HSC NI Hand Surgery / T&O |
| Inflammatory arthropathy suspected | Rheumatology |
| Paediatric trigger thumb | Paediatric Orthopaedics |
