# De Quervain's Tenosynovitis

## Overview & Pathophysiology

De Quervain's tenosynovitis is a stenosing tenosynovitis of the **first dorsal compartment** of the wrist, affecting the tendons of **abductor pollicis longus (APL)** and **extensor pollicis brevis (EPB)**. The fibro-osseous tunnel at the radial styloid becomes thickened and constrictive, producing friction, pain, and swelling with thumb and wrist movement.

**Pathological mechanism:**
- Repetitive radial/ulnar deviation with thumb use → mechanical irritation of the tendon sheath
- Fibrosis and thickening of the extensor retinaculum
- Oedema and mucoid degeneration within the tendon sheath
- **Anatomical variant:** EPB subcompartment (present in ~33–50% of patients) — missed subcompartment is the most common cause of treatment failure

**Epidemiology:**
- Incidence: ~0.5 per 1,000 person-years
- Female:male ratio approximately 6–10:1
- Peak age: 30–50 years
- Strong association with new mothers (infant lifting), repetitive manual work, and racket sports
- Prevalence in postpartum women estimated at 17–30%

## Special Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| **Finkelstein's test** (original: ulnar deviation with thumb adducted in fist) | 0.89 | 0.14 | 1.03 | 0.79 | Moderate (Kutsumi et al., 2005) |
| **Eichhoff's test** (patient makes fist around thumb, examiner deviates ulnarly) | ~0.89 | ~0.14 | — | — | Low-moderate (commonly conflated with Finkelstein) |
| **Modified Finkelstein / Wrist Stretch Test (WST)** (examiner-administered: passive thumb flexion + ulnar deviation) | Higher specificity reported | — | — | — | Limited (Goubau et al., 2014) |
| **Tenderness over radial styloid** | ~0.85 | 0.55 | 1.89 | 0.27 | Low |
| **Palpable crepitus** | 0.40 | 0.90 | 4.0 | 0.67 | Low |

> **Clinical note:** Finkelstein's and Eichhoff's tests are frequently conflated in the literature. The *true Finkelstein* (1930) involves the examiner grasping the thumb and sharply deviating the wrist ulnarly — this is the higher-sensitivity version. The *Eichhoff* (patient-performed) has similar sensitivity but poorer specificity. Both are positive if pain is reproduced over the first dorsal compartment. **A positive test in isolation has poor specificity; clinical context is essential.**

> **Modified Finkelstein/WST (Goubau et al., 2014):** Passive thumb CMC and MCP joint flexion combined with wrist ulnar deviation — may improve specificity compared to standard Eichhoff. Useful when standard tests produce equivocal results or when differentiating from intersection syndrome.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Urgent onward referral if any of the following are present:

- **Rapidly progressive swelling** with systemic features (fever, erythema tracking, rigors) → **Septic tenosynovitis** — surgical emergency
- **Inability to straighten/bend fingers** + fever → septic flexor tenosynovitis (Kanavel's signs)
- **Traumatic mechanism** with bony tenderness → scaphoid fracture / radial styloid fracture
- **Suspected inflammatory arthropathy** (bilateral, symmetrical, morning stiffness >1 hour, raised ESR/CRP)
- **Rapidly enlarging mass** at radial wrist → consider ganglion, lipoma, or rarely tumour
- **Neurovascular compromise** (paraesthesia, colour change, absent pulse)

## Clinical Frameworks

### Anatomical Variants — Surgical Relevance
- **EPB subcompartment** (33–50%): failure to release this separate compartment = most common surgical failure
- **Multiple APL slips** (up to 6 reported): associated with higher recurrence
- Ultrasound can identify subcompartmentalisation pre-procedure

### Intersection Syndrome (Differential)
- Pain 4–6 cm proximal to Lister's tubercle at crossover of APL/EPB and ECRB/ECRL
- Palpable/audible crepitus at crossover point
- Often seen in rowers, weightlifters

### Diagnostic Framework
1. Clinical diagnosis (history + special tests) sufficient for initial management
2. USS confirms if clinical uncertainty or prior to corticosteroid injection
3. MRI reserved for complex/refractory cases or suspected concurrent pathology

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Ultrasound (USS) Findings
- **First-line imaging** when diagnosis uncertain
- Findings: thickened tendon sheath (>3 mm), hyperaemia on Doppler, peritendinous fluid, EPB subcompartment identification
- Sensitivity ~0.87, specificity ~0.89 for confirming de Quervain's (Klauser et al., 2002)
- USS-guided injection significantly improves corticosteroid placement accuracy vs anatomical landmark injection

### MRI
- Not routinely indicated
- Demonstrates tendon sheath thickening, fluid, and tendon changes
- Useful if concurrent intra-articular pathology suspected (SLAC, STT OA)

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| **Intersection syndrome** | Pain 4–6 cm proximal; crepitus at crossover point; common in rowers |
| **Thumb CMC osteoarthritis** | Grind test positive; bony enlargement at base of thumb; Eaton-Littler staging |
| **Scaphoid fracture** | Trauma history; anatomical snuffbox tenderness; X-ray ± MRI |
| **Wartenberg's syndrome** | Radial sensory nerve entrapment; burning/paraesthesia dorsal thumb; Tinel's over radial forearm |
| **FCR tendinopathy** | Pain at volar wrist; tenderness over FCR tendon |
| **Trigger thumb (A1 pulley)** | Triggering/locking; palmar MCP tenderness |
| **Extensor tendon tear/subluxation** | Sudden deformity; localised swelling; positive subluxation test |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Hand Therapy Pathway

> **Primary care → Physiotherapy/Hand Therapy → Surgical/Injection if required**

**Step 1 — Initial assessment (physiotherapy/hand therapy):**
- Confirm diagnosis; screen red flags
- Baseline QuickDASH/PRWE + grip strength
- Advise activity modification; avoid provocative loading

**Step 2 — Conservative management (first-line; 4–6 weeks):**
- Thumb spica splint (see below)
- Activity modification and ergonomic advice
- Hand therapy graded loading programme when acute phase resolves

**Step 3 — Corticosteroid injection (if inadequate response at 4–6 weeks, or severe at presentation):**
- USS-guided injection strongly preferred
- Refer to physiotherapist with injection qualification or GP/MSK interface clinic
- Document EPB subcompartment injection if USS confirms separate compartment

**Step 4 — Surgical referral (if 2 injections fail, or persistent >6 months):**
- HSC NI Trauma & Orthopaedics / Hand Surgery waiting list
- Include EPB subcompartment status in referral

---

### Conservative Management

**Thumb Spica Splinting:**
- **Cochrane (Peters-Veluthamaningal et al., 2009):** Low-quality evidence; splinting reduces symptoms in short-term but no RCT demonstrates superiority over other conservative treatments
- Thermoplastic thumb spica immobilising wrist in slight extension and CMC in palmar abduction; IP joint free
- Wear: 24 hours for acute phase; wean to activity-only as symptoms improve
- Duration: 4–6 weeks; reassess at 2–4 weekly intervals

**Exercise therapy:**
- Limited high-quality evidence; tendon-gliding exercises, graded loading
- Eccentric loading of APL/EPB not well-studied; clinical consensus supports gentle tendon mobilisation post-acute phase

**Acupuncture/dry needling:** Insufficient evidence; not routinely recommended in HSC NI pathway

---

### Pharmacological

**Corticosteroid Injection (CSI):**
- **Cochrane (Peters-Veluthamaningal et al., 2009):** One small RCT (20 participants) — CSI superior to splinting alone; combination of CSI + splint may not be superior to CSI alone
- **Evidence base (non-Cochrane):** Pooled data suggests ~62–83% success rate with single CSI; second injection adds limited additional benefit
- Methylprednisolone acetate 20–40 mg with 1 mL lidocaine; USS-guided
- **Complication awareness:** Skin depigmentation, fat atrophy, and tendon rupture risk (rare); counsel patient

**NSAIDs:** Topical (diclofenac gel) or oral for acute symptom management; short-course only; follow NICE/HSC NI prescribing guidance
