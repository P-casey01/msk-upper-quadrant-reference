# Thumb CMC Osteoarthritis (Trapeziometacarpal / First CMC OA)

## Overview & Pathophysiology

Thumb carpometacarpal (CMC) osteoarthritis — also termed **trapeziometacarpal (TMC) or basal joint OA** — is the **second most common site of symptomatic OA in the hand** after the DIP joint. It results from progressive articular cartilage degeneration at the trapeziometacarpal joint, with secondary involvement of the scaphotrapezial joint in advanced cases.

**Anatomy & Biomechanics:**
- The trapeziometacarpal joint is a **biconcave-convex saddle joint** with inherent bony incongruity
- Primary stabiliser: **anterior oblique ligament (beak ligament / volar AOL)** — deep fibres critical
- Instability of the beak ligament allows dorsoradial subluxation of the 1st metacarpal — characteristic finding in Eaton Stage II+
- **Pinch loads** generate joint reactive forces of 12× applied force at the CMC joint — explains vulnerability with repetitive fine grip

**Pathological sequence:**
1. Volar AOL laxity → dorsoradial subluxation of 1st MC
2. Progressive cartilage loss → subchondral sclerosis, osteophyte formation
3. Adduction contracture of 1st web space (adaptive shortening of adductor pollicis)
4. Compensatory MCP hyperextension ("shoulder sign" or "Z-deformity")
5. Advanced: scaphotrapezial involvement; STT OA

**Epidemiology:**
- Radiographic prevalence: ~25–35% women and ~10–15% men >50 years (Haara et al., 2004)
- Symptomatic OA: ~7–15% in adults >40 years
- **Female predominance** (5–10:1 vs males)
- Strong correlation with age, female sex, post-menopausal status, prior thumb injury, hypermobility, occupational pinching

## Special Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| **Grind test** (axial compression + circumduction of 1st MC at CMC; positive = pain ± crepitus) | **0.53** | **0.80** | 2.65 | 0.59 | Moderate (Merritt et al., 2010; Kalichman & Hernández-Molina, 2011) |
| **Distraction test** (axial distraction of 1st MC; positive = pain relief or joint laxity) | ~0.70 | ~0.55 | 1.6 | 0.55 | Low |
| **Shoulder sign (Z-deformity)** (MCP hyperextension + CMC flexion on lateral X-ray/inspection) | — | — | — | — | Diagnostic sign; not a provocation test |
| **CMC grind + crepitus** combined | ~0.65 | ~0.78 | 2.95 | 0.45 | Moderate |
| **Pinch grip pain** (key pinch or 3-jaw chuck; positive if basal pain reproduced) | ~0.80 | ~0.50 | 1.6 | 0.40 | Low |
| **Radial ABduction stress** (passive radial abduction stress; positive = pain at CMC) | ~0.70 | ~0.60 | 1.75 | 0.50 | Low |

> **Clinical note:** No single test has high sensitivity **and** specificity. **Grind test** has best specificity (80%) — positive result is more useful than negative for ruling in OA. Combine with **plain radiography** for diagnostic confirmation; X-ray is essential for Eaton-Littler staging and surgical planning.

> **Grind test technique matters:** The examiner applies firm axial compression to the 1st MCP joint while simultaneously circumducting the metacarpal. Reproducing the patient's typical pain at the CMC base is a positive result. Crepitus alone without pain is less significant (degenerative crepitus common in asymptomatic elderly).

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Expedited onward referral if:

- **Rapidly progressive joint destruction** in young patient → seronegative or psoriatic arthropathy; refer Rheumatology
- **Hot, swollen, erythematous CMC** → gout (podagra pattern may affect hand); septic arthritis (rare at CMC); aspirate and send for MCS/crystals
- **Suspected scaphoid fracture** (anatomical snuffbox tenderness + trauma) → X-ray ± MRI; do not conflate with CMC OA
- **Median nerve compromise** (carpal tunnel syndrome often co-exists; BCTQ screening) → neurophysiology ± CTS pathway
- **Inflammatory arthropathy** (bilateral symmetric; MCP/PIP swelling; morning stiffness >1 hour) → Rheumatology
- **Bone pain at night / unremitting** → exclude malignancy (uncommon; rare at carpus)

## Clinical Frameworks

### Eaton-Littler Classification (Radiographic Staging)

| Stage | Radiographic Findings | Joint Space | Clinical Correlation |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Stage I** | Normal or widened joint space; no osteophytes; possible laxity/subluxation | Normal | Ligamentous laxity; synovitis; normal/near-normal X-ray |
| **Stage II** | Joint space narrowing; osteophyte <2 mm; ≤1/3 MC subluxation | Narrowed | Early OA; responds to conservative/injection |
| **Stage III** | Significant joint space narrowing/obliteration; osteophyte >2 mm; >1/3 MC subluxation | Markedly narrowed | Established OA; surgical discussion; STT usually spared |
| **Stage IV** | Pantrapezial OA (CMC + STT joint); sclerosis; cysts | Obliterated | Advanced; trapeziectomy + STT involvement; arthroplasty |

> **Staging requires X-ray** — bilateral stress views (Robert view / AP with thumb extended) are standard. Eaton-Littler Stage guides surgical planning: Stage I–II → conservative/injection; Stage III → injection/surgery; Stage IV → surgery (trapeziectomy ± arthroplasty).

### Z-Deformity / Shoulder Sign
- MCP joint hyperextension compensates for CMC adduction contracture
- Visible on clinical inspection and lateral X-ray
- **Functionally significant:** MCP hyperextension >30° requires concomitant MCP fusion/capsulodesis at time of trapeziectomy to prevent post-operative swan-neck deformity

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Plain Radiography
- **Essential for diagnosis and staging** — AP (Roberts/stress view), lateral, and oblique views
- Bilateral comparison recommended
- Roberts view (true AP of thumb CMC): hyperpronate hand on cassette with beam perpendicular — best assessment of CMC joint

### USS
- Not primary diagnostic tool for CMC OA
- Useful: synovitis assessment, USS-guided injection (improves accuracy and reproducibility)
- Demonstrates joint effusion, osteophytes, synovial thickening, Doppler signal

### MRI
- Not routine for CMC OA
- Indications: suspected concurrent pathology (scaphoid pathology, adjacent ligament injury, avascular necrosis of trapezoid/scaphoid); diagnostic uncertainty

### Laboratory
- Not required for uncomplicated CMC OA
- **If inflammatory arthropathy suspected:** FBC, ESR, CRP, RF, anti-CCP, uric acid

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| **De Quervain's tenosynovitis** | First dorsal compartment; Finkelstein/Eichhoff positive; no bony change on X-ray |
| **Scaphoid fracture** | Trauma; anatomical snuffbox tenderness; X-ray ± MRI |
| **Scaphotrapezial (STT) OA** | Radial wrist pain; X-ray shows STT involvement; often co-existent Stage IV |
| **Flexor carpi radialis (FCR) tendinopathy** | Volar wrist pain; FCR tendon tenderness; USS confirms |
| **Wartenberg's syndrome** | Radial sensory nerve; burning; Tinel's; no X-ray changes |
| **Carpal tunnel syndrome** | Median nerve territory; Phalen/Tinel; BCTQ; nerve conduction |
| **Trigger thumb** | Triggering/locking; A1 pulley; palmar MCP crease |
| **Gout/pseudogout at CMC** | Acute hot swollen joint; raised uric acid; crystals on aspiration |
| **Inflammatory arthropathy** | Bilateral; MCP/PIP; morning stiffness; raised inflammatory markers |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Hand Therapy Pathway

> **Primary care → Hand therapy (conservative first-line) → Injection → Surgical referral**

**Step 1 — Initial Assessment:**
- Eaton-Littler stage (X-ray essential — request if not available)
- Baseline DASH/QuickDASH + AUSCAN + pinch/grip strength
- Assess MCP hyperextension (Z-deformity)
- Screen for carpal tunnel syndrome (BCTQ)

**Step 2 — Conservative management (all stages first-line; ongoing in Stage I–II):**
- Hand therapy referral for splinting + exercise programme
- Activity modification; ergonomic advice; adaptive equipment

**Step 3 — Corticosteroid injection (Stage I–III; failed conservative 6–8 weeks; acute flare):**
- USS-guided intra-articular injection CMC joint
- Refer injection physiotherapist / GP-with-SI / MSK interface clinic

**Step 4 — Surgical referral (Stage III–IV; failed conservative + 2 injections; significant functional limitation):**
- HSC NI Hand Surgery / Plastic Surgery / T&O
- Include Eaton-Littler stage, MCP hyperextension status, patient functional demands

---

### Conservative Management

**Splinting:**
- **Cochrane (Bertozzi et al., 2015; Moe et al., 2021):** Evidence supports hand orthoses for pain relief and function improvement; short opponens/CMC stabilisation splint superior to no splinting for pain (low-moderate quality evidence)
- **Custom thermoplastic CMC stabilisation orthosis:** Most evidence; immobilises CMC in functional position; MCP and IP joints free; worn during activity and at night
- **Soft neoprene splint:** Less immobilisation; improves compliance; suitable for milder cases
- **Duration:** Minimum 3 months; review at 4–6 weeks
- **Evidence:** Berggren et al. (2001) — splinting equivalent to corticosteroid injection at 6 months; combination not superior

**Exercise therapy:**
- **Cochrane (Moe et al., 2021):** Exercise + splinting superior to splinting alone for pain and function at short-term; exercises should target thenar muscle strengthening and CMC stability
- Exercises: opposition, abduction/adduction against resistance; web space stretching
- Graded theraband / putty; avoid painful pinching initially
- Duration: 6–12 weeks supervised; home programme

**Thermal modalities / Manual therapy:**
- Weak evidence; adjunct only; paraffin wax, heat, joint mobilisation (CMC traction/mobilisation)
- **Joint mobilisation (Maitland/accessory):** Small RCTs suggest short-term pain benefit; insufficient for standalone recommendation

---

### Pharmacological

**Corticosteroid Injection (CSI):**
- **Evidence:** Joshi et al. (2019) meta-analysis — CSI provides significant short-term pain relief (4–8 weeks); effect wanes at 6 months; no strong evidence for structural modification
- USS-guided recommended (small joint; improves accuracy vs landmark)
- Methylprednisolone 20–40 mg or triamcinolone 20 mg + 1 mL lidocaine
- **Frequency:** Maximum 2–3 per year; evidence supports benefit of repeated injections; structural caution with frequent use
- **Hyaluronic acid (viscosupplementation):** Multiple small RCTs — comparable to CSI at 6–12 months; not NICE-recommended as standard; available at some centres

**Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel):**
- NICE-recommended first-line pharmacological treatment for hand OA
- Evidence: Hunter et al.; Cochrane (Derry et al., 2017) — significant pain reduction vs placebo; fewer systemic effects than oral NSAIDs

**Oral NSAIDs / Analgesics:**
- Short-term use for acute flares; follow NICE/MHRA guidance; consider GI/CV/renal risk
- Paracetamol: limited efficacy in OA (Machado et al., 2015 meta-analysis); may still provide modest benefit

### Surgical Options

| Procedure | Indication | Outcomes | Recurrence / Notes |
|-----------|------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Trapeziectomy (simple)** | Stage III–IV; failed conservative | 80–90% good/excellent at 5 years; thumb shortening | Gold standard; strong evidence base |
| **Trapeziectomy + LRTI (ligament reconstruction tendon interposition)** | Stage III–IV | No evidence of superiority over simple trapeziectomy (Cochrane, Wajon et al., 2015) | Longer procedure; similar outcomes |
| **CMC arthroplasty (prosthetic)** | Stage II–III; younger, higher demand patients | Good short-term; concern for long-term implant survival | Limited UK uptake; pyrocarbon available |
| **CMC arthrodesis** | Young active patients; Stage II–III | Eliminates pain; reduces motion; useful in manual workers | Motion sacrifice |
| **Metacarpal extension osteotomy** | Stage I–II; young; delayed surgery | Modest evidence; alters load distribution | Niche; not widely used |

**Trapeziectomy (CMC arthroplasty criteria for HSC NI):**
- Eaton-Littler Stage III–IV
- Failed conservative management ≥6 months
- Failed ≥2 corticosteroid injections
- Significant functional limitation (QuickDASH >40 or equivalent)
- MCP hyperextension >30° — note for surgeon (may need concurrent MCP procedure)

**Cochrane evidence (Wajon et al., 2015):** No significant difference in outcomes between trapeziectomy alone vs trapeziectomy + LRTI vs trapeziectomy + K-wire stabilisation. **Simple trapeziectomy** is therefore the evidence-based standard, avoiding the morbidity of tendon harvest.
