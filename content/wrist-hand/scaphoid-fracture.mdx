## Overview & Pathophysiology

Scaphoid fractures are the most common carpal bone fractures, accounting for approximately 60–70% of all carpal fractures and 2–7% of all fractures. They typically result from a fall on an outstretched hand (FOOSH) with the wrist in dorsiflexion and radial deviation, transmitting compressive force through the thenar eminence to the scaphoid waist.

**Anatomical Vulnerability**
The scaphoid has a retrograde blood supply predominantly from the radial artery entering distally through the dorsal ridge. The proximal pole receives no direct blood supply, making proximal pole fractures particularly vulnerable to avascular necrosis (AVN). AVN occurs in up to 30–40% of untreated or delayed-diagnosis fractures of the proximal pole.

**Fracture Classification (Herbert & Fisher)**
- **Type A:** Stable acute fractures (A1: tubercle fracture; A2: incomplete waist fracture)
- **Type B:** Unstable acute fractures (B1: distal oblique; B2: complete waist; B3: proximal pole; B4: trans-scaphoid perilunate fracture-dislocation)
- **Type C:** Delayed union
- **Type D:** Established non-union (D1: fibrous; D2: pseudarthrosis)

**Epidemiology**
- Peak incidence: males aged 20–30 years
- Annual incidence: ~5 per 10,000 population (UK)
- Up to 20% of scaphoid fractures are occult on initial plain X-ray
- Delayed diagnosis is associated with non-union (up to 12% waist fractures if missed) and AVN

## Special Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| Anatomical snuffbox tenderness | 0.90 | 0.40 | 1.50 | 0.25 | Moderate (Parvizi et al. 1998; Duckworth et al. 2012) |
| Scaphoid compression test (longitudinal compression of thumb) | 0.80 | 0.48 | 1.54 | 0.42 | Moderate (Parvizi et al. 1998) |
| Scaphoid tubercle tenderness | 0.82 | 0.57 | 1.91 | 0.32 | Moderate (Grover 1996) |
| Pain on supination against resistance | 0.74 | 0.76 | 3.08 | 0.34 | Low–Moderate (Freeland 1989) |
| Combined 3-test rule (snuffbox + compression + tubercle) | 0.98 | 0.40 | 1.63 | 0.05 | Moderate (Unsworth et al. 2020) |

**Clinical Notes on Tests**

*Anatomical Snuffbox Tenderness:* High sensitivity (0.90) makes it an excellent screening test — a negative test is clinically useful (−LR 0.25). However, poor specificity (0.40) means many false positives; should not be used in isolation to confirm fracture.

*Scaphoid Compression Test:* Apply longitudinal compression along the axis of the thumb toward the scaphoid. Positive = pain at the anatomical snuffbox or radial wrist. Lower sensitivity than snuffbox tenderness; adds specificity when combined.

*Combined 3-Test Rule:* If all three tests are negative, fracture is unlikely (−LR 0.05, post-test probability < 2% in low-prevalence settings). If any test is positive, imaging is warranted.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ **Urgent / Do Not Miss**

- **Perilunate dislocation / trans-scaphoid perilunate fracture-dislocation** — Carpal malalignment on X-ray; median nerve compromise; requires emergency orthopaedic referral
- **Open fracture** — Requires same-day surgical review
- **Median nerve symptoms** (acute carpal tunnel syndrome post-injury) — Sensory loss in median nerve distribution, thenar weakness; urgent decompression may be required
- **Vascular compromise** — Skin changes, absence of digital pulses post-injury
- **Pathological fracture** — Low-energy mechanism; consider bone metastasis, primary bone tumour, or metabolic bone disease (osteoporosis workup in older patients)
- **Child/adolescent** — Growth plate involvement; paediatric orthopaedic referral

**Non-union risk factors (refer urgently):**
- Proximal pole fracture
- Displaced fracture (>1 mm gap or >10° humpback deformity)
- Delayed presentation >4 weeks
- Previous non-union

## Clinical Frameworks

### Ottawa Wrist Rules (Derived Criteria — Scaphoid Application)

The original Ottawa Wrist Rules were developed for wrist fracture detection post-FOOSH. Validated sensitivity for any wrist fracture: ~98%. For scaphoid specifically:

**Image if ANY of the following:**
1. Point tenderness over anatomical snuffbox
2. Point tenderness over scaphoid tubercle
3. Painful supination against resistance
4. Age <20 or >65 years with mechanism of FOOSH
5. Neurovascular deficit

**Clinical Prediction Rule (Duckworth et al. 2012 — BSSH guidance):**
- Negative snuffbox + negative compression + negative tubercle → fracture extremely unlikely (NPV ~98–100%)
- Any positive → proceed to imaging regardless of plain X-ray result

### Injury Grading — DISI Deformity
A scapholunate angle >60° on lateral wrist X-ray suggests DISI (Dorsal Intercalated Segment Instability) associated with ligamentous instability — requires specialist review.

**PRWE** is recommended as the primary outcome measure for scaphoid fractures in research and clinical audit settings (MacDermid et al. 1998).

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Imaging Pathway

```
FOOSH + Clinical Suspicion
         │
         ▼
Plain X-ray (PA, Lateral, Scaphoid views)
         │
    ┌────┴────┐
Fracture   No fracture visible
visible         │
    │      ┌───┴───┐
Confirmed  High    Low
fracture  clinical clinical
    │     suspicion suspicion
    │           │        │
    ▼           ▼        ▼
Manage      MRI within  Discharge
            72 hours    with safety
            (preferred) netting
                │
           ┌────┴────┐
        Fracture   No fracture
        on MRI         │
           │       CT if MRI
         Treat     unavailable
```

**MRI:**
- Gold standard for occult scaphoid fracture detection
- Sensitivity 0.88–1.00; Specificity 0.97–1.00 (Yin et al. 2010 — systematic review)
- Recommended within 72 hours if X-ray negative but clinical suspicion high
- NICE guidance (NG38, 2016): Offer MRI within 72 hours; if unavailable, CT acceptable; bone scintigraphy third-line

**CT:**
- Sensitivity 0.72–0.93; Specificity 0.99 (Brydie & Raby 2003)
- Superior to MRI for cortical detail and surgical planning
- Preferred for fracture characterisation once diagnosed (displacement, comminution, union monitoring)

**Bone Scintigraphy:**
- High sensitivity (~1.00) but low specificity (~0.60) — significant false-positive rate
- Reserved for when MRI and CT are both unavailable (NICE NG38)

**Plain X-ray Limitations:**
- Sensitivity for scaphoid fracture: 0.54–0.70 on initial films (Yin et al. 2010)
- Up to 20% missed on day 1; repeat X-ray at 10–14 days improves but does not replace MRI

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features | Investigation |
|-----------|----------------------------|---------------|
| Distal radius fracture | Tenderness over distal radius (not snuffbox); visible on X-ray | Plain X-ray |
| Scapholunate ligament injury | Scapholunate gap >3 mm on PA X-ray ("Terry Thomas sign"); stress views | X-ray stress views; MR arthrography |
| De Quervain's tenosynovitis | Positive Finkelstein's test; tenderness over 1st extensor compartment | Clinical; USS |
| Radial styloid fracture | Tenderness at radial styloid; fracture on X-ray | Plain X-ray |
| Trapezium fracture | Tenderness at CMC joint; rare | CT |
| Carpal tunnel syndrome (acute) | Median nerve symptoms; Tinel/Phalen positive | Clinical + nerve conduction |
| TFCC injury | Ulnar-sided pain; fovea sign positive; piano key test | MRI / MR arthrography |
| Wrist sprain | Diffuse tenderness; no specific bony point tenderness; normal imaging | Diagnosis of exclusion |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Pathway

**Emergency Department (ED):**
1. Clinical assessment + Ottawa Wrist Rules application
2. Plain X-ray (scaphoid series: PA, lateral, oblique, scaphoid view)
3. If fracture confirmed: immobilise in thumb spica plaster/splint; refer to fracture clinic (next available, within 5 working days)
4. If X-ray negative but clinical suspicion high:
   - Immobilise in thumb spica backslab
   - Refer urgently for MRI within 72 hours via fracture clinic or radiology direct access
   - Written safety-netting advice (return if symptoms worsen)
5. If perilunate dislocation suspected: **same-day orthopaedic review**

**Fracture Clinic (HSC NI Trust):**
- Review with MRI/CT results
- Classify fracture (Herbert & Fisher)
- Decision: conservative vs surgical management
- Physiotherapy referral: post-immobilisation rehabilitation

### Conservative Management (Evidence-Based)

**Indications:** Non-displaced (<1 mm) stable waist or distal pole fractures

**Immobilisation:**
- Thumb spica cast: below-elbow (forearm to IP joint of thumb)
- Duration: Waist fracture — 8–12 weeks; Distal pole — 6–8 weeks; Proximal pole — consider surgery
- Evidence: Randomised trials (Dias et al. 2008 — BMJ) show below-elbow cast non-inferior to above-elbow for non-displaced waist fractures
- Cast vs splint: Rigid fibreglass cast preferred during acute phase; thermoplastic splint acceptable for compliant patients in later stages

**Physiotherapy (Post-Immobilisation):**
- Commence at cast removal
- Phase 1 (0–4 weeks post-cast): Oedema management, scar mobilisation, wrist ROM exercises (active-assisted), tendon gliding
- Phase 2 (4–8 weeks): Progressive ROM, grip strengthening, proprioception
- Phase 3 (8+ weeks): Functional strengthening, return to work/sport
- Target: Grip strength ≥80% contralateral; QuickDASH ≤20; PRWE <15 at discharge

**Bone stimulation (LIPUS):**
- Low-intensity pulsed ultrasound (LIPUS) — some evidence for accelerating union in non-displaced fractures (Dias et al. 1999); not routinely commissioned in HSC NI; discuss with orthopaedic team

### Pharmacological

- Analgesia: Paracetamol 1g QDS (first-line); NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400mg TDS with food) if no contraindications — caution: NSAIDs may theoretically impair fracture healing; short course only
- Avoid opioids where possible; if required, liaise with GP
- Vitamin D: Check and supplement if deficient (NICE PH56)
- No evidence for routine use of calcium supplements post-fracture in non-osteoporotic patients

### Surgical Management

**Indications for fixation (refer to orthopaedics):**
- Displaced fracture (>1 mm step-off or >10° humpback deformity on CT)
- Proximal pole fracture (high AVN risk)
- Non-union at 12 weeks
- Athlete/manual worker preference for earlier return to activity
- Perilunate fracture-dislocation (emergency)

**Procedure:** Percutaneous or open cannulated headless compression screw fixation (e.g., Herbert screw, Acutrak)

**Post-surgical physio:** As conservative Phase 1–3, typically commencing 2 weeks post-op

### Onward Referral Criteria (Physiotherapy → Orthopaedics)

- Failure to progress with conservative management at 12 weeks
- Development of AVN (pain, restricted ROM, X-ray/MRI changes)
- Non-union on imaging
- Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) signs
