# Dupuytren's Contracture (Palmar Fibromatosis)

## Overview & Pathophysiology

Dupuytren's contracture is a **benign fibroproliferative disorder** of the palmar and digital fascia, characterised by progressive thickening and shortening of the palmar aponeurosis, resulting in **fixed flexion contractures** of the MCP and PIP joints. Named after Baron Guillaume Dupuytren (1831), though Plater described it earlier in 1614.

**Pathological mechanism:**
- Myofibroblast proliferation within palmar fascia (Skoog's pretendinous bands, spiral bands, natatory ligaments, Grayson's ligaments)
- Three histological phases: proliferative → involutional → residual
- **Pretendinous cord** → MCP contracture; **spiral cord** (Cleland's, Grayson's, pretendinous, lateral digital sheet) → PIP contracture + neurovascular bundle displacement
- Spiral cord displaces NVB medially, superficially, and proximally — critical surgical consideration

**Epidemiology:**
- Prevalence: ~2–30% in Western European populations; highest in Northern Europe (Scandinavia, Scotland, Ireland, Northern England)
- **Northern Ireland prevalence** estimated 10–15% in those >65 years — clinically significant for HSC NI
- Male:female ratio 6–10:1 (young); equalises with age
- Peak presentation: 50–60 years (males); 60–70 years (females)
- **Ring finger** most commonly affected (70–80%), followed by little finger

**Risk Factors (Established):**

| Risk Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|-------------|----------|-------|
| Northern European ancestry | Strong (genetic) | Autosomal dominant, incomplete penetrance |
| Age >50 years | Strong | Risk increases linearly with age |
| Male sex | Strong | — |
| Diabetes mellitus | Moderate (OR ~3–4) | Bilateral; radial digits; more aggressive |
| Epilepsy / anticonvulsant use | Moderate (phenobarbitone historically) | Possibly drug-mediated |
| Alcohol use disorder | Moderate | Liver fibrosis association |
| Smoking | Moderate | Microvascular ischaemia proposed |
| Manual labour / vibration | Weak-moderate | Contested; some evidence for repetitive hand trauma |
| HIV infection | Emerging | — |

**Diathesis (Hueston):** Aggressive phenotype — bilateral disease, ectopic involvement (Peyronie's, Ledderhose/plantar), radial digit involvement, strong family history, early onset. Higher recurrence post-treatment.

## Special Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| **Hueston tabletop test** (patient cannot lay palm flat on table) | ~0.95 | ~0.90 | ~9.5 | ~0.06 | Low-moderate (widely used; limited formal validation) |
| **Passive joint angle assessment (goniometry)** | Reference standard | — | — | — | Moderate |
| **Palpable cord in palm/digit** | ~0.90 | ~0.85 | 6.0 | 0.12 | Low |
| **Skin pitting/tethering** | ~0.70 | ~0.90 | 7.0 | 0.33 | Low |

> **Diagnosis is clinical** — history of progressive painless contracture + palpable cord + skin changes. Imaging not required for diagnosis.

> **Hueston tabletop test:** Positive when patient cannot simultaneously lay palm and fingers flat on a table surface. Indicates ≥30° combined MCP/PIP contracture and is commonly used as the **surgical referral threshold**.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Expedited onward referral if:

- **Rapidly progressive contracture** (weeks, not months/years) → consider rare desmoplastic fibroma or sarcoma; request USS ± MRI
- **Painful, inflamed cord** with systemic features → exclude infection or acute inflammatory condition
- **Neurovascular compromise** (digital paraesthesia, colour change, absent pulse) → urgent surgical assessment
- **Locked digit** in acute flexion with trauma history → exclude fracture/dislocation
- **Child/adolescent** with progressive hand contracture → consider knuckle pads alone vs juvenile Dupuytren; rare — paediatric orthopaedic referral

## Clinical Frameworks

### Tubiana Staging System

| Stage | Total Passive Extension Deficit (TPED) | Description |
|-------|---------------------------------------|-------------|
| **N** | 0° | Palmar nodule/cord only; no contracture |
| **Stage I** | 0–45° | Mild; MCP predominantly |
| **Stage II** | 45–90° | Moderate; MCP + PIP involvement |
| **Stage III** | 90–135° | Severe; significant PIP contracture |
| **Stage IV** | >135° | Very severe; fixed contracture; DIP may be involved |

> **TPED** = sum of extension deficit at MCP + PIP + DIP for the affected ray. Guides surgical planning and outcome benchmarking.

### Surgical Referral Threshold (HSC NI)
- **MCP contracture ≥30°** or **PIP contracture ≥15°** (any degree of PIP contracture is functionally significant)
- Positive tabletop test
- Progressive disease documented over 3–6 months

### Anatomorphological Types
- **Nodular:** Early; may be painful; proliferative phase
- **Cord:** Band-like; main driver of contracture
- **Diffuse:** Skin involvement; poor prognosis; higher recurrence

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Imaging
- **USS:** Not required for diagnosis; useful to identify cord anatomy, NVB position pre-procedure (needle aponeurotomy)
- **MRI:** Reserved for atypical cases or suspected malignancy (aggressive/rapidly progressive)
- No routine imaging indicated for established Dupuytren's

### Histopathology
- Rarely required; if diagnosis uncertain (e.g., excludes fibrosarcoma)
- Alpha-smooth muscle actin (α-SMA) positive myofibroblasts — hallmark

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| **Palmar ganglion** | Fluctuant; transilluminates; no contracture; USS confirms |
| **Giant cell tumour of tendon sheath** | Firm, lobulated nodule; USS — vascular; no cord |
| **Fibroma/fibrosarcoma** | Rapidly progressive; painful; USS ± MRI |
| **Trigger finger** | Active triggering/locking; A1 pulley tenderness; no palmar cord |
| **Ulnar nerve palsy** | Intrinsic minus hand; clawing 4th/5th digits; motor/sensory deficit |
| **Camptodactyly** | Congenital; painless PIP flexion; no palmar cord; young onset |
| **Knuckle pads** | Thickening over PIP/MCP dorsum; no contracture; Dupuytren diathesis association |
| **Ledderhose disease (plantar fibromatosis)** | Ectopic manifestation; plantar nodules; part of Dupuytren diathesis |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Hand Therapy Pathway

> **Monitoring → Hand therapy (conservative) → Procedural/Surgical (intervention when threshold met)**

**Step 1 — Baseline Assessment:**
- Tubiana staging + goniometry per joint/digit
- Hueston tabletop test (document pass/fail)
- QuickDASH + URAM scale
- Risk factor screening; document diathesis features

**Step 2 — Watchful waiting (Stage N–I; not functionally limiting):**
- Reassure re: benign nature; lifestyle advice (alcohol, smoking)
- Review at 6–12 months or if progression
- Educate: "not cured by treatment; recurrence is expected"

**Step 3 — Hand therapy (pre- and post-procedural):**
- Pre-procedural: Serial static night extension splinting NOT evidence-based as standalone; do not delay referral
- Post-procedural: Extension splinting + hand therapy exercise programme is **essential** for all interventions
- Post-collagenase/NA: Immediate hand therapy; night splinting 3–6 months

**Step 4 — Procedural Intervention (MCP ≥30° or PIP ≥15°, or functionally significant):**
- Discuss all options; patient choice and comorbidities guide selection
- Refer HSC NI Hand Surgery / Plastic Surgery

---

### Conservative Management

**Splinting (alone, non-post-procedural):**
- No high-quality RCT evidence that splinting alone alters disease progression or prevents contracture recurrence
- Night extension splinting is appropriate post-procedure; not as primary disease-modifying treatment
- **Radiotherapy** (low-dose, <30 Gy): May slow progression at nodular stage (Stage N–I); limited UK availability; experimental in HSC NI

### Interventional Options — Comparative Evidence

| Intervention | Procedure | Initial Correction | Recurrence at 5 years | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Needle fasciotomy (NA/PNA)** | Percutaneous needle division of cord; outpatient; LA | 75–80% MCP; 50–60% PIP | ~50–65% (MCP); higher PIP | Rapid recovery; lowest cost; office procedure; suitable elderly/comorbid |
| **Collagenase injection (Xiapex / CCH)** | Clostridium histolyticum collagenase; 2-visit: inject Day 1, manipulate Day 2 | ~75–90% MCP; ~40–60% PIP | ~47–66% at 5 years (CORDLESS study) | NICE-approved; withdrawn UK market 2020 (commercial); may return; discuss if available |
| **Limited fasciectomy (LF)** | Surgical excision of diseased fascia; regional/GA; day case | ~85–95% MCP; ~70–80% PIP | ~20–40% at 5–10 years | Gold standard; higher complication rate (10–15%); nerve/vessel risk; longer recovery |
| **Dermofasciectomy** | Fasciectomy + skin graft (Z-plasties); recurrent/diathesis cases | Similar to LF | ~8–10% at 5 years | Lowest recurrence; highest morbidity; reserved for recurrence or severe diathesis |

**Key comparative evidence:**
- **CORD-I & CORD-II RCTs (Hurst et al., 2009; Gilpin et al., 2011):** Collagenase (CCH) vs placebo — CCH significantly superior; 64% achieved full correction MCP
- **CORDLESS study (Peimer et al., 2013):** Long-term follow-up — 47% recurrence (≥20° contracture) at 5 years post-CCH
- **Cochrane (Zhao et al., 2022):** Needle fasciotomy vs limited fasciectomy — LF achieves superior correction; NA has shorter recovery and fewer complications; recurrence higher with NA
- **van Rijssen et al. (2012):** RCT — NA vs LF; at 5 years, recurrence 85% (NA) vs 21% (LF) for MCP joints

### Onward Referral Criteria

| Trigger | Referral Destination |
|---------|---------------------|
| MCP ≥30° contracture | HSC NI Hand Surgery / Plastic Surgery |
| Any PIP contracture affecting function | HSC NI Hand Surgery / Plastic Surgery |
| Positive tabletop test | HSC NI Hand Surgery / Plastic Surgery |
| Diathesis features + progressive disease | HSC NI Hand Surgery / Plastic Surgery (early discussion) |
