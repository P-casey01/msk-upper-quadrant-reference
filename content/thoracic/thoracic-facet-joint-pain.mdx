## Overview & Pathophysiology

Thoracic facet joint pain (also termed thoracic zygapophyseal joint pain) refers to pain arising from the synovial facet (zygapophyseal) joints of the thoracic spine. It is a recognised but frequently underdiagnosed cause of mid-back pain in the adult population.

### Prevalence
- Estimated prevalence of thoracic facet joint pain in patients with thoracic back pain: **34–48%** based on controlled diagnostic medial branch blocks (Manchikanti L et al., 2007)
- Cervical facet joint pain is better characterised; thoracic data extrapolated from cervical/lumbar literature
- Can occur at any thoracic level; slightly more common at lower thoracic levels
- Affects adults of all ages; increased prevalence with degenerative change in middle-older age

### Relevant Anatomy
- **Thoracic facet joints (zygapophyseal joints)** are paired synovial joints between the superior and inferior articular processes of adjacent vertebrae
- In the thoracic spine, the facet joints are oriented **coronally (frontal plane)** — approximately 60° from the horizontal — permitting rotation and lateral flexion, limiting flexion/extension
- Each facet joint is innervated by the **medial branch of the dorsal ramus** from its own level and the level above (dual innervation — basis for medial branch block diagnostic technique)
- Additionally, the thoracic spine has **costovertebral joints** (articulations between rib head and vertebral body) and **costotransverse joints** (between rib tubercle and transverse process) — these may also contribute to pain syndromes clinically overlapping with facet pain

### Mechanism & Aetiology
- **Degenerative:** Articular cartilage loss, osteophyte formation, subchondral sclerosis — most common in middle–older age
- **Postural:** Prolonged thoracic flexion (kyphotic postures, desk work) loads posterior elements; increased facet joint compressive force in hyperkyphosis
- **Traumatic:** Acute hyperextension or rotation injury (whiplash-type mechanism), direct trauma
- **Inflammatory:** Rheumatoid arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis — facet joint synovitis
- **Acute locked facet (meniscoid entrapment):** Sudden onset acute thoracic pain following rotation — proposed entrapment of fibroadipose meniscoid structure within the joint

## Special Tests

> **Critical note:** No single clinical test has been validated for thoracic facet joint pain with adequate sensitivity/specificity data. The **gold standard for diagnosis** is **controlled comparative medial branch blocks** (two anaesthetics with different durations producing concordant relief). Clinical tests assist in identifying likely facet-mediated pain prior to diagnostic blocks.

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **Passive Accessory Intervertebral Movement (PAIVM) — PA Unilateral** | Not formally validated | Not formally validated | — | — | Expert consensus | Unilateral PA on articular pillar reproducing concordant pain; primary clinical assessment tool |
| **Passive Physiological Intervertebral Movement (PPIVM) — Rotation** | Not formally validated | Not formally validated | — | — | Expert consensus | Restricted painful rotation in direction of affected facet; compare bilaterally |
| **Kemp's Test (Thoracic Extension-Rotation)** | Limited data | Limited data | — | — | Expert opinion | Extension combined with ipsilateral rotation loading facet joint; reproduces back ± referred pain |
| **Thoracic Extension Test** | Limited data | Limited data | — | — | Expert opinion | Isolated extension reproducing posterior thoracic pain; suggests posterior element involvement |
| **Medial Branch Block (diagnostic)** | 0.92–0.96 (for concordant ≥80% relief) | 0.72–0.82 (controlled double block) | 3.3–5.3 | 0.05–0.10 | High (reference standard) | Diagnostic procedure; not a clinical test but the reference standard |

### Maitland Assessment Findings Consistent with Facet Pain
- Unilateral paraspinal tenderness at facet joint level
- Restricted and painful thoracic rotation (ipsilateral side)
- Positive PA unilateral accessory movement reproducing concordant pain
- Often absent neurological signs

**References:** Manchikanti L et al. (2007). *Pain Physician*; Bogduk N. (2011). *Practice Guidelines for Spinal Diagnostic and Treatment Procedures*.

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Thoracic pain + fever + elevated inflammatory markers | Septic arthritis of facet joint / discitis | Urgent MRI + microbiology; admit |
| Constant severe thoracic pain, worse lying | Malignancy / metastatic disease | Urgent MRI ± bone scan; bloods |
| Age >50 + thoracic pain + history of malignancy | Metastatic spinal disease | Urgent oncology referral per NICE NG23 |
| Thoracic pain + bilateral neurological signs | Cord compression / myelopathy | Emergency MRI |
| Thoracic pain + bowel/bladder dysfunction | Cord compression | Emergency neurosurgical referral |
| Sudden onset thoracic pain + haemodynamic compromise | Aortic dissection / aneurysm | **999 — Emergency** |
| Thoracic pain + weight loss + night sweats | Malignancy / lymphoma | Urgent GP referral / investigations |
| Thoracic pain in patient with osteoporosis + acute onset | Compression fracture | X-ray / MRI |
| Thoracic pain + morning stiffness >1 hour + bilateral | Ankylosing spondylitis / axial SpA | Rheumatology referral; HLAB27, MRI sacroiliac joints |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Framework
Thoracic facet joint pain is highly amenable to the Maitland approach:

**Assessment:**
- **PAIVMs:** Central PA (spinous process) and unilateral PA (articular pillar, 2–3cm lateral to spinous process) — assess resistance, end-feel, and pain response. Grade I–IV on quality, resistance, and pain
- **PPIVMs:** Rotation (primary thoracic motion), lateral flexion — assess range, end-feel, symmetry
- **Comparable sign:** Identify the movement/pressure that most closely reproduces the patient's concordant pain

**Treatment Grading:**
- Grade I–II: Pain dominant; oscillatory movements at start/middle of range (acute/irritable presentation)
- Grade III–IV: Stiffness dominant; end-range mobilisation (subacute/chronic presentation)
- Grade V (manipulation): High-velocity low-amplitude thrust — for non-irritable stiff thoracic facet restriction; contraindications: osteoporosis, cord compression, instability, anticoagulation

**Reassessment:** Always reassess comparable sign after each intervention.

### McKenzie/MDT Framework
- Thoracic extension typically the directional preference in posterior element pain
- Extension exercises: prone press-up, seated thoracic extension, foam roller extension
- Correct flexion postures that load thoracic facets in extension gap
- Education on postural modification — sustained flexion perpetuates facet joint stress
- May classify as: dysfunction syndrome (repeated extension exercises for restricted posterior elements) or postural syndrome (posture correction)

### Bogduk's Approach (Evidence-Based Diagnostic Framework)
- History-based clinical features alone have poor diagnostic accuracy for facet joint pain (Bogduk N, 2011)
- Controlled medial branch blocks remain the only validated diagnostic method
- Clinical examination used to localise level(s) for diagnostic block
- Identify: unilateral vs bilateral, acute vs chronic, suspected levels

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Prevalence Studies
- Manchikanti L et al. (2007): Using controlled double diagnostic medial branch blocks, found **34–48% prevalence** of thoracic facet joint pain in patients presenting with chronic thoracic pain. *Pain Physician*, 10(4), 521–532.
- Atluri S et al. (2011): Systematic review — thoracic medial branch blocks have good diagnostic accuracy when controlled double-block paradigm used (Atluri S et al., *Pain Physician*, 14(5), E241–E264)

### Clinical Prediction Rules
- **No validated clinical prediction rule** exists specifically for thoracic facet joint pain
- Cervical and lumbar facet prediction rules exist but have not been validated at thoracic levels
- Schwarzer AC et al. (1994) demonstrated that clinical features cannot reliably identify lumbar facet pain — likely applicable to thoracic spine

### Diagnostic Imaging
- **Plain X-ray:** May show degenerative facet changes, spondylosis, kyphosis — **not diagnostic** for pain source
- **CT scan:** Better visualisation of facet joint morphology, osteophytes — cannot confirm pain source
- **MRI:** Superior soft tissue assessment; may show facet joint synovitis (joint effusion, synovial thickening on T2) — poor correlation with pain in isolation
- **Bone scan (SPECT):** Increased uptake at facet joints in active degeneration; moderate diagnostic value — sensitivity ~70%, specificity ~65% (Pneumaticos SG et al., 2006)
- **Diagnostic medial branch blocks:** Gold standard; controlled comparative blocks with short-acting (lidocaine) and long-acting (bupivacaine) anaesthetic; ≥80% pain relief with concordant duration = positive diagnosis

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Thoracic disc herniation | Central or paracentral pain, may have neurological signs; MRI confirms |
| Thoracic radiculopathy | Dermatomal band-like pain, neurological deficit; MRI ± NCS |
| Costochondritis | Anterior chest pain, costo-sternal junction tenderness |
| Costovertebral joint pain | Very lateral paraspinal pain, reproduced by rib mobilisation rather than PA facet pressure |
| Scheuermann's disease | Kyphotic deformity, wedge vertebrae on X-ray; younger patients |
| Osteoporotic compression fracture | Acute onset, female >60, DEXA confirms osteoporosis; MRI shows fracture |
| Metastatic spinal disease | Constant pain, night pain, systemic symptoms, bone destruction on imaging |
| Ankylosing spondylitis | Bilateral, morning stiffness, sacroiliitis, elevated CRP/ESR, HLA-B27 |
| Rheumatoid arthritis | Symmetrical joint involvement, rheumatoid factor/anti-CCP, systemic features |
| Intercostal neuralgia | Band-like dermatomal pain; post-herpetic; no movement reproduction |
| Cardiac/visceral referred pain | Not reproducible with spinal palpation/movement; associated systemic symptoms |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Community Physiotherapy (First-line):**
- GP referral or self-referral to community MSK physiotherapy
- Appropriate for straightforward mechanical thoracic facet joint pain with no red flags
- Adequate trial: 6–8 sessions over 8–12 weeks

**Secondary Care:**
- Pain management referral if no improvement after 8–12 weeks conservative management
- Medial branch blocks / radiofrequency denervation — referred to pain clinic (Belfast City Hospital Pain Management Centre; Causeway/Altnagelvin for western HSC Trusts)
- Rheumatology if inflammatory arthropathy suspected

**Occupational Health:** Ergonomic assessment for work-related thoracic pain (especially sedentary workers with prolonged thoracic flexion)

### Conservative Management

**Physiotherapy — Evidence Grade B–C:**

1. **Manual Therapy (Grade B — moderate evidence):**
   - Thoracic spine manipulation / high-velocity thrust (HVLAT): moderate evidence for short-term pain reduction in thoracic pain (Briggs AM et al., 2009)
   - Maitland Grade III–IV mobilisation at affected levels: well-supported in clinical practice; Grade I–II for irritable presentations
   - Rib mobilisation for costovertebral component
   - Contraindications to manipulation: osteoporosis, instability, cord signs, anticoagulation, inflammatory arthritis

2. **Exercise Therapy (Grade B):**
   - Thoracic extension strengthening: prone cobra, scapular retraction, rowing exercises
   - Flexibility: thoracic rotation stretches, foam roller extension, lateral flexion stretches
   - Core stability: deep thoracic/lumbar stabiliser programme
   - Postural strengthening: lower trapezius, rhomboids, serratus anterior

3. **Electrotherapy:**
   - TENS: short-term pain relief, Grade C evidence (NICE supports for chronic pain)
   - Therapeutic ultrasound: insufficient evidence for recommendation

4. **Education and Self-Management:**
   - Ergonomics: workstation assessment, avoid prolonged thoracic flexion, screen height, chair lumbar support
   - Activity modification: pace activities, graded return to exercise
   - Reassurance: often resolves with appropriate management

### Pharmacological (NICE-Aligned)

- **Topical NSAIDs (first-line):** Diclofenac 1% gel TDS at painful area (NICE NG59 — preferred for localised MSK pain)
- **Oral NSAIDs:** Ibuprofen 400mg TDS or naproxen 500mg BD + PPI; 2–4 weeks (NICE NG59)
- **Paracetamol:** Adjunct; limited evidence for back pain per NICE
- **Low-dose tricyclic antidepressants:** Amitriptyline 10–25mg nocte for chronic thoracic pain — off-label but supported by NICE NG193 for chronic primary pain
- **Avoid:** Prolonged opioid use, benzodiazepines (NICE NG59)

**Interventional (Pain Clinic):**
- **Medial branch blocks:** Diagnostic and short-term therapeutic; methylprednisolone 20–40mg + local anaesthetic at thoracic medial branches (image-guided)
- **Radiofrequency denervation (RFD) / thermocoagulation:** For confirmed facet joint pain (≥80% relief from dual medial branch blocks) — evidence: Manchikanti L et al. (2012) — significant pain/function improvement at 1–2 years vs sham (Level II evidence, *Pain Physician*)
- **Intraarticular steroid injection:** Moderate short-term benefit; less evidence than RFD for long-term relief

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Urgent:**
- Any red flag identified (see above)
- Progressive neurological deficit → emergency

**To Pain Management (Elective):**
- Failure of ≥8–12 weeks physiotherapy
- High pain/disability burden suitable for diagnostic medial branch blocks
- For consideration of RFD if blocks confirm diagnosis

**To Rheumatology:**
- Suspected inflammatory arthropathy (bilateral, morning stiffness, elevated inflammatory markers)

**Surgical:**
- Rarely indicated for isolated facet joint pain
- Occasionally: spinal decompression if co-existing stenosis or spondylolisthesis
