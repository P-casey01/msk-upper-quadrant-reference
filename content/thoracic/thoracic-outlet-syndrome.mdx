## Overview & Pathophysiology

Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS) refers to a group of disorders caused by compression of the neurovascular structures (brachial plexus, subclavian artery, subclavian vein) as they pass through the thoracic outlet — the space between the clavicle, first rib, and scalene muscles.

### Subtypes
- **Neurogenic TOS (nTOS):** ~95% of cases. Compression of the brachial plexus (most commonly lower trunk C8–T1). Presents with pain, paraesthesia, and weakness in the upper limb.
- **Venous TOS (vTOS):** ~4%. Subclavian vein compression; Paget-Schroetter syndrome (effort thrombosis). Presents with arm swelling, cyanosis, distended veins.
- **Arterial TOS (aTOS):** ~1%. Subclavian artery compression; typically associated with a cervical rib. Presents with ischaemic symptoms, pallor, cold limb.

### Relevant Anatomy
- **Thoracic outlet** consists of three potential compression zones:
  1. Interscalene triangle (anterior and middle scalene + first rib)
  2. Costoclavicular space (clavicle + first rib + subclavius)
  3. Subpectoral/retropectoralis minor space

### Prevalence & Risk Factors
- Estimated prevalence: 3–80 per 1,000 in the general population (wide range due to diagnostic variability)
- Female:male ratio approximately 3.5:1 for nTOS
- Peak onset: 20–50 years
- Risk factors: cervical rib (present in ~0.5–1% of population), anomalous first rib, scalene muscle hypertrophy, poor posture, repetitive overhead activity, trauma (especially whiplash), and breast hypertrophy

### Mechanism
Compression results in either direct mechanical compression of neural/vascular structures or ischaemic neuropathy. In nTOS, the lower trunk and medial cord of the brachial plexus (C8, T1) are most commonly affected, producing ulnar-distribution symptoms.

## Special Tests

> **Note:** Provocative tests for TOS have historically poor diagnostic accuracy. They are best interpreted in combination (cluster approach) with patient history. No single test is pathognomonic. Many studies are limited by lack of reference standard.

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| Adson's Test | 0.79 | 0.76 | 3.29 | 0.28 | Low–Moderate | High false-positive rate; tests interscalene triangle; reproduces symptoms or abolishes radial pulse |
| Wright's Hyperabduction Test | 0.70 | 0.53 | 1.49 | 0.57 | Low | Tests subpectoral space; pulse obliteration unreliable as occurs in 60–70% asymptomatic individuals |
| Roos Stress Test (EAST – Elevated Arm Stress Test) | 0.82 | 0.59 | 2.00 | 0.30 | Moderate | 3-minute arm elevation with repeated hand opening/closing; reproducing symptoms more specific than pulse obliteration |
| Costoclavicular Manoeuvre | 0.53 | 0.68 | 1.66 | 0.69 | Low | Tests costoclavicular space; shoulders drawn back and downward |
| Upper Limb Tension Test (ULTT1/ULTT4) | 0.97 | 0.22 | 1.24 | 0.14 | Low–Moderate for nTOS | High sensitivity; poor specificity; useful for ruling OUT neurogenic component |
| Spurling's Test | 0.50 | 0.86 | 3.57 | 0.58 | Moderate | Differentiates cervical radiculopathy from TOS |

**References:** Hooper TL et al. (2010). *J Man Manip Ther*; Gillard J et al. (2001). *J Hand Surg*; Nord KM et al. (2008) review data.

### Recommended Test Cluster (nTOS)
A positive diagnosis of nTOS requires ≥3 of the following (Consensus criteria — Illig KA et al., 2010):
1. Symptoms consistent with brachial plexus compression
2. Positive provocative testing (EAST or ULTT)
3. Response to scalene muscle block
4. Exclusion of other diagnoses

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Sudden severe arm pain + pallor + coldness | Arterial TOS / acute limb ischaemia | Emergency vascular referral (999/A&E) |
| Arm swelling + cyanosis + distended veins after exertion | Venous TOS / Paget-Schroetter | Urgent same-day vascular referral |
| Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) | Pancoast tumour / apical lung malignancy | Urgent CXR + oncology referral |
| Progressive neurological deficit | Space-occupying lesion, cord compression | Urgent MRI and neurology/neurosurgery |
| Night sweats, unintentional weight loss, haematoptysis | Malignancy | Urgent GP referral / 2WW pathway |
| Bilateral symptoms with spasticity | Cervical myelopathy | Urgent neurosurgical referral |
| Cervical rib with pulsatile mass | Subclavian artery aneurysm | Emergency vascular review |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Framework
- Assess accessory and physiological movements of the cervicothoracic junction (C7–T1, T1–T4), first rib mobility, and clavicular movement
- Pain mapping: distinguish local thoracic outlet tenderness from peripheral neurological patterns
- Grade III–IV mobilisations of the first rib (posteroanterior, longitudinal caudad) may decompress the interscalene triangle
- Reassess neurodynamic sensitisation using ULTT after joint mobilisation

### McKenzie/MDT Framework
- Postural assessment: forward head posture, rounded shoulders, thoracic kyphosis increase scalene tension and reduce outlet volume
- McKenzie posture correction and extension exercises may reduce compression in costoclavicular and subpectoral spaces
- Classify: postural syndrome vs dysfunction vs derangement (though TOS rarely fits classical MDT subgroups)
- Sustained extension/retraction exercises as home exercise programme

### Pattern Recognition
- Lower trunk brachial plexus pattern (C8–T1): medial forearm, ring/little finger paraesthesia, intrinsic hand weakness (especially first dorsal interosseous)
- Differentiate from ulnar neuropathy at the elbow (no neck/shoulder component, positive elbow provocation tests)
- Vascular pattern: positional symptoms, effort-induced, colour/temperature changes

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Criteria
There is no validated gold-standard diagnostic test. The **Consensus Statement (Illig et al., 2010; endorsed by Society for Vascular Surgery)** recommends a clinical diagnosis based on:
1. Symptoms consistent with brachial plexus compression
2. Positive provocative test battery
3. Exclusion of other diagnoses (cervical radiculopathy, rotator cuff pathology, carpal tunnel syndrome)

### Imaging
- **CXR/Plain X-ray:** First-line to identify cervical rib, elongated C7 transverse process, clavicle/first rib anomalies
- **MRI brachial plexus:** Useful to exclude disc prolapse, Pancoast tumour; poor sensitivity for dynamic compression in TOS
- **CT angiography / Duplex ultrasound:** For arterial/venous TOS with positional manoeuvres
- **Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) / EMG:** May show medial antebrachial cutaneous nerve abnormality in nTOS (~77–95% sensitivity in true nTOS per Machanic & Bhatt, 2017); however, many nTOS cases have normal NCS
- **Scalene Muscle Block (diagnostic):** Considered quasi-gold standard for nTOS; >50% symptom relief supports diagnosis

### Key Systematic Reviews
- Povlsen B et al. (2014). *Cochrane Database*: Insufficient RCT evidence to determine superiority of any intervention.
- Hooper TL et al. (2010). *J Man Manip Ther* 18(2):74–83: Comprehensive review of diagnostic accuracy of clinical tests.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Cervical radiculopathy (C8/T1) | Positive Spurling's, cervical movement provocation, dermatomal pattern; MRI cervical spine |
| Ulnar neuropathy at elbow | Positive elbow flexion provocation test, Tinel's at cubital tunnel; NCS localises lesion |
| Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) | Positive Phalen's/Tinel's at wrist; median nerve distribution; nocturnal symptoms typical |
| Rotator cuff pathology | Localised shoulder pain, positive impingement tests (Hawkins-Kennedy, Neer), no neurological symptoms |
| Cervical myelopathy | Bilateral, lower limb involvement, Lhermitte's sign, pathological reflexes, Hoffman's sign |
| Pancoast tumour | Weight loss, haemoptysis, Horner's syndrome, CXR abnormality; urgent investigation required |
| CRPS Type I | Autonomic features (sweating, colour change), allodynia; follows injury without nerve lesion |
| Peripheral arterial disease | Claudication, ankle-brachial index <0.9, absent/diminished pulses |
| Subclavian steal syndrome | Symptoms with arm exercise, blood pressure differential >15 mmHg between arms |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Community Physiotherapy (Primary Care):**
- First-line management for suspected nTOS
- Referral via GP or self-referral (where available) to community MSK physiotherapy
- Initial assessment to exclude red flags and vascular/arterial TOS
- Waiting times vary: typically 6–18 weeks community MSK in NI (check local BHSCT/SEHSCT/NHSCT/SHSCT/WHSCT directory)

**Secondary Care:**
- Refer to vascular surgery for arterial/venous TOS (urgent if acute vascular compromise)
- Refer to neurology/orthopaedics/spinal surgery if myelopathy suspected
- Consider referral to pain management if conservative management fails at 3–6 months
- Scalene block may be arranged via pain clinic or vascular surgery (NI — typically at RVH Belfast or Altnagelvin)

**Occupational Health:**
- Workplace assessment and modification for occupational TOS (repetitive overhead work, keyboard posture)

### Conservative Management

**First-line Physiotherapy (Evidence Grade B–C):**
1. **Postural re-education:** Correct forward head posture, rounded shoulders, thoracic kyphosis. Evidence shows postural correction reduces outlet narrowing. (Lindgren KA, 1997)
2. **Stretching:** Scalene stretching (anterior and middle), pectoralis minor stretching, levator scapulae. Hold 30–45 seconds, 3–5 repetitions, 2–3x/day.
3. **Strengthening:** Scapular stabiliser strengthening (lower trapezius, serratus anterior). Progressive resistance programme over 6–8 weeks.
4. **Neural mobilisation (ULTT-based):** Slider and tensioner techniques for brachial plexus. Evidence from Sanders RJ et al. (2010).
5. **First rib mobilisation/manipulation:** PA mobilisations of first rib (Grade III–IV Maitland). May improve interscalene space. (Lindgren, 1997)
6. **Breathing retraining:** Diaphragmatic breathing to reduce accessory breathing muscle (scalene) overuse.
7. **Education:** Activity modification, ergonomic advice, avoidance of provocative postures.

**Programme Duration:** Minimum 6–8 weeks conservative physiotherapy before reassessment for further management.

### Pharmacological (NICE-Aligned)

- **NSAIDs:** Short-term (up to 4 weeks) for pain management — ibuprofen 400mg TDS or naproxen 500mg BD with PPI cover. NICE NG59 (musculoskeletal pain)
- **Neuropathic agents:** Amitriptyline 10–25mg nocte or gabapentin 300mg TDS (titrating) for neurogenic symptoms — NICE NG173 (neuropathic pain in adults)
- **Muscle relaxants:** Diazepam 2–5mg TDS (short-term only, maximum 4 weeks) for scalene spasm — use with caution
- **Anticoagulation:** For venous TOS / Paget-Schroetter — managed by vascular team (typically LMWH → warfarin or DOAC)
- **Thrombolysis/anticoagulation:** For acute vTOS — vascular surgery decision

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Urgent (same-day/within 24 hours):**
- Acute limb ischaemia (arterial TOS — 6 Ps: pallor, pulselessness, pain, paraesthesia, paralysis, perishingly cold)
- Acute axillo-subclavian vein thrombosis with significant swelling

**Urgent (within 2 weeks):**
- Suspected Pancoast tumour (Horner's + shoulder/arm pain + weight loss/haemoptysis)
- Progressive neurological deficit

**Elective Referral (6–12 weeks):**
- Failure of ≥3 months conservative physiotherapy
- Confirmed cervical rib with ongoing symptoms — vascular surgery for consideration of rib resection/first rib resection
- Diagnostic scalene block — pain clinic/vascular surgery referral

**Surgical Options (vascular surgery decision):**
- First rib resection (transaxillary or supraclavicular approach)
- Scalenectomy
- Cervical rib resection
- Venous decompression + thrombolysis for vTOS
