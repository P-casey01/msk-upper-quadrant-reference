## Overview & Pathophysiology

Thoracic radiculopathy refers to pain and/or neurological dysfunction attributable to irritation or compression of a thoracic nerve root as it exits the spinal canal through the intervertebral foramen. It is significantly less common than cervical or lumbar radiculopathy, partly because the thoracic spine has less mobility and is stabilised by the rib cage, resulting in less disc degeneration and foraminal narrowing.

### Prevalence
- Thoracic radiculopathy is **rare in isolation** — estimated at <1% of all spinal radiculopathy presentations
- More commonly seen as a component of a broader thoracic spine disorder (disc herniation, compression fracture, herpes zoster)
- No robust epidemiological incidence/prevalence data specific to thoracic radiculopathy (differentiated from myelopathy)

### Nerve Root Distribution (Key Dermatomal Map)
| Root | Dermatome |
|------|-----------|
| T1 | Inner upper arm, axilla |
| T2 | Inner upper arm, axilla, upper chest |
| T3–T6 | Chest wall (horizontal band) |
| T7–T9 | Upper abdomen |
| T10 | Umbilical level |
| T11 | Lower abdomen |
| T12 | Groin, upper thigh |

**Myotomal involvement:** Limited in thoracic region — intercostal muscles (T1–T12), abdominal muscles (T6–T12); testable but difficult to isolate clinically

### Relevant Anatomy
- **12 paired thoracic nerve roots** exit beneath each thoracic pedicle
- Each nerve root divides into:
  - **Dorsal ramus:** Supplies paravertebral muscles and overlying skin
  - **Ventral ramus:** Becomes the intercostal nerve (T1–T11) or subcostal nerve (T12)
- The intercostal nerve travels along the inferior border of the rib in the costal groove
- Thoracic nerve roots are relatively close to the pedicle and disc, with limited reserve space
- **Thoracic dorsal root ganglia** are located within the intervertebral foramen — vulnerable to foraminal stenosis

### Mechanisms / Aetiology
1. **Disc herniation:** Lateral or foraminal disc protrusion compressing nerve root (see Thoracic Disc Herniation)
2. **Foraminal stenosis:** Degenerative osteoarthritis, osteophytes narrowing foramen
3. **Vertebral fracture:** Compression fracture with foraminal involvement (osteoporotic or traumatic)
4. **Herpes zoster (shingles):** Most common cause of thoracic dermatomal pain — viral radiculitis; must be excluded early
5. **Metastatic disease:** Vertebral body metastasis causing foraminal invasion or nerve root compression
6. **Diabetic thoracic radiculopathy:** Rare presentation of diabetic radiculoneuropathy — abdominal pain/weakness; trunk muscles
7. **Primary tumour:** Schwannoma, meningioma of thoracic nerve root
8. **Spondylitis/discitis:** Infective involvement of vertebral body/disc with secondary nerve root compression

## Special Tests

> **Critical note:** There are **very limited validated clinical tests** specifically for thoracic radiculopathy. The thoracic spine lacks equivalent validated provocative tests to the SLR (lumbar) or Spurling's (cervical). Diagnosis is primarily clinical (dermatomal distribution + symptom pattern) supported by MRI and nerve conduction studies.

### Available Clinical Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **Thoracic Slump Test** | Limited data (one small study) | Limited data | — | — | Expert opinion | Neural tension test for thoracic nerve roots; overpressure in slump + thoracic extension/rotation reproducing dermatomal symptoms |
| **Kemp's Test (Thoracic)** | Not formally validated | Not formally validated | — | — | Expert opinion | Extension + ipsilateral rotation + compression at thoracic level; reproduces radicular pain |
| **Deep Tendon Reflexes (abdominal)** | Limited data | Limited data | — | — | Expert opinion | Upper abdominal (T7–T9), lower abdominal (T10–T12) reflexes — assessed by skin stimulation; loss suggests root pathology |
| **Dermatomal Sensation Testing** | Not validated as standalone | Not validated | — | — | Standard practice | Pinprick and light touch in thoracic dermatomal pattern; lateralisation and level identification |
| **Intercostal Rib Palpation (tender nerve in groove)** | Not validated | Not validated | — | — | Expert opinion | Palpation along inferior rib border reproducing radicular pain in dermatomal distribution |
| **Chest Expansion / Deep Breath Provocation** | Not validated | Not validated | — | — | Expert opinion | Exacerbation of band-like pain with deep inspiration suggests intercostal nerve/root irritation |

**Key diagnostic tool: MRI thoracic spine** — assesses nerve root compression, foraminal stenosis, disc herniation, cord signal change.

**Nerve conduction studies / EMG:** Limited utility for thoracic radiculopathy (intercostal muscles technically difficult to assess); however, paraspinal EMG may show denervation at affected level. NCS for associated limb pathology useful for differential diagnosis.

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Bilateral band-like thoracic pain + leg weakness + spasticity | Myelopathy (cord involvement, not purely radiculopathy) | **Emergency MRI + neurosurgical referral** |
| Bowel/bladder dysfunction | Cord or conus medullaris involvement | **Emergency neurosurgical referral** |
| Thoracic dermatomal pain + known malignancy | Metastatic nerve root compression | Same-day oncology/MSCC coordinator (NICE NG23) |
| Thoracic pain + fever + immunosuppression | Herpes zoster, epidural abscess, discitis | Urgent: antiviral if vesicular rash; MRI if no rash |
| Vesicular rash in thoracic dermatome | Herpes zoster — refer to GP for antiviral within 72 hours | GP same-day |
| Thoracic radiculopathy + unexplained weight loss, night sweats | Malignancy | 2WW GP referral + urgent imaging |
| Thoracic pain + postural hypotension + abdominal pain | Aortic aneurysm / dissection | **999** |
| Uncontrolled diabetes + thoracic pain + abdominal wall weakness | Diabetic thoracic radiculopathy | GP referral; exclude other causes first |
| Severe, rapidly progressive neurological deficit | Space-occupying lesion; vascular | Emergency neurosurgery |
| Thoracic pain in child/adolescent | Scheuermann's, tumour, infection | Paediatric orthopaedic referral |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Framework
- Assess thoracic spine segmentally: PAIVMs central and unilateral at suspected root level(s) and adjacent levels
- Identify comparable sign: movement or pressure reproducing concordant radicular symptoms
- Rib mobility: assess costovertebral/costotransverse joints — may contribute to foraminal narrowing via rib head position
- **Neural tension:** Thoracic Slump test — assess neural mechanosensitivity; distinguish peripheral sensitisation from local mechanical compression
- Treatment: Grade I–II oscillatory mobilisations to reduce pain and neural sensitivity; Grade III–IV for foraminal mobility (once irritability assessed)
- Caution: avoid mobilisations that peripheralise symptoms

### McKenzie/MDT Framework
- Perform systematic assessment of directional preference
- Thoracic extension commonly reduces discogenic radiculopathy (centralisation)
- Lateral shift correction if present (lateral disc herniation with nerve root displacement)
- If symptoms centralise with extension: progressive extension programme (McKenzie Protocol)
- If extension worsens/peripheralises: flexion or lateral bias assessment; may indicate foraminal pathology
- Education: avoidance of sustained flexion postures (increase disc/nerve pressure)

### Neurodynamic Framework (Butler/Shacklock)
- Thoracic radiculopathy produces mechanosensitivity of the thoracic nerve roots and intercostal nerves
- **Slump Sliders (grade I):** Gentle rhythmic neural mobilisation — alternating cervical flexion/extension with knee extension to create sliding motion of neural tissue
- **Tensioners:** Held-end-range for more established mechanical adherence (use with caution in acute phase)
- Combined with spinal mobilisation to address the joint component

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Approach
1. **History:** Band-like, dermatomal chest/abdominal pain; unilateral; aggravated by thoracic movement, deep inspiration, cough; may have history of disc herniation, fracture, herpes zoster, diabetes, or malignancy
2. **Neurological Examination:** Dermatomal sensory change (hypoaesthesia/hyperaesthesia), abdominal reflex loss, focal muscle wasting (rare)
3. **MRI Thoracic Spine:** Primary investigation — identifies structural cause (disc, osteophyte, fracture, tumour); cord signal change indicates myelopathy (change management)
4. **EMG/NCS:** Limited but paraspinal EMG can show denervation at affected levels; rules out more distal nerve pathology
5. **Serological:** HbA1c (diabetic radiculopathy), ESR/CRP (infection/inflammatory), PSA/protein electrophoresis (malignancy), VZV serology/PCR (herpes zoster)

### Herpes Zoster — Critical Consideration
- Herpes zoster (shingles) reactivation in thoracic ganglia is the **most common cause of acute thoracic dermatomal pain** in adults
- Pain typically precedes the vesicular rash by **3–7 days** — during this window, presentation is identical to mechanical thoracic radiculopathy
- **Always ask:** previous chickenpox, immunocompromised state, age >60
- **If suspected:** Same-day GP referral for antiviral prescription (aciclovir 800mg 5x/day or valaciclovir 1g TDS for 7 days) — most effective within 72 hours of rash onset (NICE CKS Shingles)
- **Post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN):** Persistent pain >3 months after rash resolution; gabapentin/amitriptyline first-line (NICE NG173)

### Diabetic Thoracic Radiculopathy (Diabetic Truncal Neuropathy)
- Rare; presents as unilateral or bilateral abdominal/thoracic pain and weakness
- Associated with abdominal wall bulge (muscle atrophy T6–T12)
- Often presents in middle-aged males with poorly controlled diabetes
- Diagnosis: clinical + EMG; MRI normal
- Management: glycaemic control; neuropathic pain agents; physiotherapy

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Herpes zoster (shingles) | Dermatomal burning pain ± vesicular rash; VZV serology; older/immunocompromised |
| Thoracic disc herniation | MRI confirms; may present with myelopathy (cord signs) |
| Intercostal neuralgia (post-herpetic) | Post-rash chronic pain; allodynia in dermatomal distribution |
| Costochondritis | Anterior chest wall, costo-sternal tenderness, no neurological signs |
| Cardiac ischaemia | Central, crushing; ECG/troponin changes; exertional |
| Pulmonary embolism | Pleuritic; dyspnoea; tachycardia; D-dimer/CTPA |
| Aortic dissection | Sudden severe tearing pain; BP differential; haemodynamic instability |
| Biliary colic / cholecystitis | Right upper quadrant, post-prandial; Murphy's sign; ultrasound |
| Peptic ulcer disease | Epigastric, food-related; H. pylori; endoscopy |
| Renal colic | Loin-to-groin pain; haematuria; non-positional; CT-KUB |
| Pleuritis / pneumonia | Fever, cough, reduced air entry; CXR abnormality |
| Thoracic facet joint pain | Paraspinal, localised; no dermatomal neurological signs; not true radiculopathy |
| Diabetic truncal neuropathy | Diabetes history; abdominal wall weakness; EMG confirms |
| Metastatic spinal disease | Night pain; weight loss; bone destruction; known primary |
| Multiple sclerosis | Lhermitte's; relapsing-remitting; MRI brain/spine plaques |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care / Community:**
- GP assessment to exclude herpes zoster, cardiac, and pulmonary causes — mandatory
- Herpes zoster → same-day antiviral prescription from GP
- Mechanical thoracic radiculopathy → community physiotherapy referral (self-referral or GP referral)
- Diabetic radiculopathy → GP/diabetology for glycaemic optimisation + physiotherapy
- Malignancy/MSCC → NICE NG23 pathway; contact MSCC coordinator (NI: Belfast City Hospital oncology)

**Secondary Care:**
- MRI ordering (GP or secondary care): consider for persistent radiculopathy >6 weeks unresponsive to conservative management
- Neurosurgical referral: for confirmed compressive pathology on MRI with significant neurological deficit or myelopathy
- Pain management: referral for thoracic epidural, nerve root injection, or management programme if conservative management fails at 3 months
- NI Pain Management: Belfast City Hospital, Altnagelvin, Daisy Hill

### Conservative Management

**Physiotherapy — Evidence Grade C (extrapolated from cervical/lumbar radiculopathy evidence):**

1. **Patient Education:**
   - Natural history: most mechanical radiculopathies improve within 6–12 weeks
   - Pain neuroscience education (PNE): address fear-avoidance, catastrophising
   - Activity modification: avoid sustained thoracic flexion, heavy lifting during acute phase
   - Positioning advice: pain-relieving positions, sleep posture

2. **Neural Mobilisation:**
   - Thoracic slump sliders — Grade I–II rhythmic neural mobilisation
   - Intercostal nerve mobilisation: side-lying ipsilateral lateral flexion away + rib spacing
   - Progress to tensioners when acute irritability settles
   - Butler DS (2000) — Mobilisation of the Nervous System: neural sliding techniques

3. **Manual Therapy:**
   - Grade I–II Maitland mobilisations at affected level (pain-dominant/acute phase)
   - Grade III–IV mobilisation for foraminal restriction (subacute/chronic phase)
   - Rib mobilisation: costovertebral joints may contribute to foraminal dynamics
   - Avoid Grade V manipulation in acute radiculopathy with neurological signs

4. **Exercise Therapy:**
   - McKenzie extension programme (if directional preference confirmed)
   - Thoracic stabiliser strengthening (progressive — avoid painful loading)
   - Postural correction: scapular retractors, thoracic extensors

5. **TENS / Electrotherapy:**
   - TENS for pain relief in acute neuropathic pain (Grade C)
   - Acupuncture: NICE NG193 supports for chronic primary pain

### Pharmacological (NICE-Aligned)

- **NSAIDs:** Short-term (2–4 weeks) — ibuprofen 400mg TDS or naproxen 500mg BD + PPI (NICE NG59)
- **Neuropathic Pain (first-line per NICE NG173):**
  - Amitriptyline 10mg nocte (titrating to 25–75mg) — evidence Grade B
  - Duloxetine 60mg daily (titrate from 30mg) — evidence Grade B
  - Gabapentin 300mg TDS (titrate) — evidence Grade B; note 2019 controlled drug scheduling (Schedule 3)
  - Pregabalin 75–300mg BD — evidence Grade B; also Schedule 3 controlled drug
- **Antiviral (Herpes Zoster):** Aciclovir 800mg 5x/day × 7 days OR valaciclovir 1g TDS × 7 days — most effective within 72 hours of rash; reduces PHN risk (NICE CKS)
- **Corticosteroids:** Oral prednisolone 40mg/day (5-day reducing course) — acute severe radiculopathy; limited evidence; off-label
- **Topical:** Lidocaine patches or capsaicin cream (0.025–0.075%) for localised neuropathic thoracic pain — NICE NG173 supports as adjunct

**Interventional (Pain Clinic):**
- **Thoracic nerve root injection (TNRI) / epidural:** Methylprednisolone 40–80mg + local anaesthetic — image-guided (fluoroscopy or CT); short-to-medium term pain reduction; limited RCT evidence at thoracic level; risk of cord trauma → high expertise required
- **Pulsed radiofrequency (PRF)** of dorsal root ganglion: Emerging evidence for refractory radiculopathy (NICE IPGS; specialist pain service decision)

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Emergency:**
- Myelopathy (bilateral signs, cord compression)
- Bowel/bladder dysfunction
- MSCC (per NICE NG23)

**Urgent (within 2 weeks):**
- Progressive neurological deficit
- Severe refractory pain with MRI-confirmed compressive lesion
- Suspected malignancy

**Elective (3–6 months):**
- Failure of conservative management with confirmed compressive pathology
- Pain management referral for nerve root injection / PRF consideration
- Post-herpetic neuralgia >3 months → pain management (NICE NG173)
