## Overview & Pathophysiology

Scheuermann's disease (Scheuermann's kyphosis; juvenile kyphosis) is a structural developmental disorder characterised by **anterior vertebral body wedging** producing a rigid thoracic hyperkyphosis. It is the most common cause of structural kyphosis in adolescents.

### Diagnostic Criteria (Sorensen Criteria — 1964, widely accepted)
- **≥3 consecutive thoracic vertebrae with ≥5° of anterior wedging** each
- Associated with irregular vertebral endplates
- Presence of Schmorl's nodes (nuclear herniations through endplates)
- Kyphosis typically rigid (does not correct fully with hyperextension)

### Prevalence & Demographics
- Prevalence: **1–8% of the general population** (Bradford DS, 1977; Lowe TG, 1990)
- Onset: typically **10–15 years of age** (adolescent growth spurt)
- Male:female ratio: approximately **2:1** (some studies suggest equal distribution)
- Peak deformity progression during adolescent growth spurt (Risser stage 0–3)
- Thoracic Scheuermann's most common (T7–T9 most frequently involved apex)
- **Lumbar Scheuermann's:** Also described; causes low back pain; less common; wedging of lumbar vertebrae

### Relevant Anatomy
- **Ring apophysis** of vertebral endplate is vulnerable during adolescent growth
- During rapid growth, the apophysis is cartilaginous and susceptible to mechanical loading
- **Proposed mechanism:** Disruption of endplate ring apophysis → irregular endplate → asymmetric loading → anterior growth retardation → vertebral wedging
- **Schmorl's nodes:** Herniation of nucleus pulposus through weakened vertebral endplate → reactive sclerosis
- Adjacent disc spaces often narrowed

### Aetiology (Multifactorial — exact cause unknown)
- **Genetic predisposition:** Autosomal dominant inheritance suggested (Halal F et al., 1978)
- **Mechanical loading:** Repetitive flexion loading during growth (agricultural work, physical labour — historical association)
- **Collagen/endplate abnormality:** Proposed defect in collagen matrix of endplate
- **Osteoporosis:** Some evidence of reduced bone density in affected vertebrae
- **Tight hamstrings and hip flexors:** Common associated finding; may contribute to compensatory thoracic kyphosis

### Natural History
- Adolescents with <60° kyphosis: most reach adulthood with minimal functional impairment
- **Long-term:** Murray PM et al. (1993) — 32-year follow-up showed patients with Scheuermann's had more back pain but similar function/employment to controls; self-esteem and psychological impact significant
- Curves >75° may cause cardiopulmonary compromise in severe cases
- Curve progression after skeletal maturity is generally slow (<1°/year for most)

## Special Tests

> **Note:** Scheuermann's disease is primarily a **clinical + radiological diagnosis**. There are no specific orthopaedic special tests with published sensitivity/specificity data for this condition. The following assessments are used clinically.

### Clinical Assessment Findings

| Assessment | Finding in Scheuermann's | Clinical Significance |
|------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **Adam's Forward Bend Test** | Kyphosis **does not correct** (rigid); scoliosis screen simultaneously | Differentiates structural from postural kyphosis; postural kyphosis corrects |
| **Prone Press-Up / Extension Test** | Kyphosis partially or fully **corrects in postural kyphosis**; remains in Scheuermann's | Key differentiating test from postural round back |
| **Wall Stand Test** | Cannot flatten thoracic spine against wall; persistent curve | Quantifies rigidity of kyphosis |
| **Hamstring Length Assessment (SLR)** | Frequently tight (<70° SLR); common associated finding | Tight hamstrings increase posterior pelvic tilt, accentuate kyphosis |
| **Hip Flexor Length (Thomas Test)** | Often tight; anterior pelvic tilt compensation | Assess as part of full kinematic chain |
| **Cobb Angle Measurement (X-ray)** | Primary diagnostic and monitoring measurement | Measured from superior endplate of upper vertebra to inferior endplate of lower vertebra of the kyphotic curve |
| **Respiratory Assessment** | Reduced chest expansion in severe kyphosis (>75°) | FVC/FEV1 if >80° kyphosis |

### Postural Kyphosis vs Scheuermann's: Key Differentiation

| Feature | Postural Kyphosis | Scheuermann's Disease |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Corrects with extension | Yes — fully | No — partially or not at all |
| Vertebral wedging (X-ray) | No | Yes (≥5° in ≥3 vertebrae) |
| Schmorl's nodes | Absent | Present |
| Endplate irregularities | Absent | Present |
| Pain | Variable | Typically thoracic/lumbar pain |
| Age of onset | Any | Adolescence (10–15 years) |

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Rapidly progressive kyphosis in skeletally immature | Aggressive structural deformity | Urgent orthopaedic/spinal surgery referral |
| Neurological symptoms (weakness, paraesthesia, gait change) | Cord compression from severe kyphosis | Urgent MRI + neurosurgical referral |
| Bowel/bladder dysfunction | Cord/conus compression | Emergency neurosurgical referral |
| Kyphosis with systemic features (weight loss, fever) | Malignancy / infection / inflammatory disease | Urgent investigation + specialist referral |
| Kyphosis in a child <10 years | Other pathology (congenital, metabolic, neuromuscular) | Paediatric orthopaedic referral |
| Severe curve (>80°) + exertional dyspnoea | Cardiopulmonary compromise | Respiratory assessment; refer cardiac/respiratory |
| Kyphosis + skin changes (café-au-lait spots, neurofibromas) | Neurofibromatosis | Paediatric genetics/orthopaedic referral |
| Kyphosis + restricted chest expansion + spinal inflammatory markers | Ankylosing spondylitis | Rheumatology referral |
| Severe thoracic pain at site of kyphosis | Acute Schmorl's node, fracture, disc herniation | MRI thoracic spine |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Framework
- Manual therapy has a role in Scheuermann's primarily for **pain management and mobility** (not deformity correction in skeletal maturity)
- Assess PAIVMs at each thoracic level for localised stiffness and pain
- Grade III–IV mobilisations at painful/restricted levels
- Rib mobilisation at costovertebral joints (often restricted secondary to kyphosis)
- Extension mobilisations: unilateral and central PA to promote extension
- Manipulation (Grade V): use with caution; deformity alters biomechanics

### McKenzie/MDT Framework
- **Primary approach in adolescent Scheuermann's:**
- Thoracic extension exercises as cornerstone of conservative management
- Prone lying → prone on elbows → prone press-up progression
- Seated extension over back of chair, foam roller extension
- Active thoracic rotation in flexion/extension to maintain segmental mobility
- Postural education and correction
- **Directional preference:** extension consistently beneficial (directional preference)
- McKenzie classification: mostly dysfunction syndrome (fixed kyphotic deformity) + postural syndrome overlay

### Bracing Framework (Orthopaedic)
- **Milwaukee brace** (cervico-thoraco-lumbar orthosis): traditional; poor compliance
- **Boston/TLSO brace**: thoraco-lumbar-sacral orthosis; used for apex below T7
- **Indication:** Cobb angle **45–70°** in skeletally immature patient (Risser 0–3)
- **Duration:** Full-time wear (~20–23 hours/day) until skeletal maturity; then weaning
- **Evidence:** Sachs B et al. (1987) — bracing effective in reducing progression; improvement of 40% in kyphotic angle achieved in some series
- Physiotherapy role during bracing: maintain core strength, exercise in and out of brace

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Radiographic Diagnosis
Standard lateral thoracic X-ray (standing, if possible) confirms diagnosis:
- **Cobb angle** of thoracic kyphosis (normal 20–40°)
- Vertebral wedging (anterior height < posterior height, ≥5° per Sorensen criteria)
- Endplate irregularities
- Schmorl's nodes
- Disc space narrowing at affected levels

### Key Evidence
- **Bradford DS (1977):** Classic description and classification; established prevalence data
- **Murray PM et al. (1993):** *JBJS* — 32-year follow-up of 67 patients with Scheuermann's compared to 34 controls; more back pain in Scheuermann's group but similar education, employment, quality of life; neurological deficits rare
- **Sachs B et al. (1987):** Bracing study — Milwaukee brace demonstrated 41% improvement in kyphosis in skeletally immature patients; adult patients did not benefit from bracing
- **Lowe TG (1990):** Review of Scheuermann's — comprehensive clinical update including surgical indications
- **Palazzo C et al. (2016):** Systematic review of exercise for Scheuermann's — limited RCT evidence; exercise associated with short-term pain reduction and improved posture
- **Weiss HR, Turnbull D (2012):** Review — physical therapy and bracing effective for mild-moderate curves; surgical outcomes good for severe deformity

### Imaging Guidance
- **Plain X-ray:** Diagnostic and monitoring — lateral thoracic standing X-ray
- **MRI:** Not routine; indicated if neurological symptoms, atypical presentation, or pre-surgical planning; assesses disc, cord, and neural foramina
- **CT:** Reserved for surgical planning; better osseous detail
- **DEXA:** Consider if atypical young presentation or suspected osteoporosis

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Postural round back (postural kyphosis) | Corrects fully with extension; no vertebral wedging on X-ray; no Schmorl's nodes |
| Congenital kyphosis | Present from birth/early childhood; vertebral segmentation/formation failure on X-ray/CT |
| Neuromuscular kyphosis | Underlying neurological condition (e.g., cerebral palsy, DMD); long C-shaped curve |
| Ankylosing spondylitis | Bamboo spine on X-ray; HLA-B27 positive; sacroiliitis; older onset |
| Vertebral compression fracture | Acute onset, history of trauma or osteoporosis; MRI shows fracture |
| Spinal tuberculosis (Pott's disease) | Fever, systemic illness, immunocompromised; vertebral destruction on imaging |
| Tumour (primary/metastatic) | Night pain, weight loss, constant pain; bone destruction |
| Idiopathic scoliosis | Coronal plane deformity; positive Adam's forward bend (rib hump); different radiographic pattern |
| Spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia | Short stature, multiple vertebral deformities; genetic diagnosis |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Community Physiotherapy (First-line for adolescent/adult):**
- Referral via GP (paediatric or adult GP) for exercise therapy and postural management
- Community MSK physiotherapy appropriate for mild-moderate kyphosis with pain
- Paediatric physiotherapy through HSC Trust children's services for <16 years

**Secondary Care — Paediatric Orthopaedics:**
- Refer to paediatric orthopaedics for adolescents with:
  - Cobb angle ≥50° in growing child (Risser 0–3) — bracing consideration
  - Rapid progression (>5°/year)
  - Neurological involvement
- NI referral: Royal Belfast Hospital for Sick Children (RBHSC) paediatric orthopaedics

**Secondary Care — Adult Orthopaedics/Spinal Surgery:**
- Refer adult patients with:
  - Severe kyphosis (≥75°) with pain/functional limitation
  - Neurological compromise
  - Cardiopulmonary compromise
- Surgical teams: Royal Victoria Hospital Belfast (spinal surgery)

### Conservative Management

**Exercise Therapy — Evidence Grade B:**

1. **Thoracic Extension Exercises (Primary intervention):**
   - Prone press-up (McKenzie extension)
   - Foam roller thoracic extension
   - Doorway/corner stretch (bilateral shoulder external rotation + extension)
   - Cat-camel mobilisation
   - Seated extension over chair back
   - Frequency: daily; 10–15 repetitions, 3 sets

2. **Flexibility:**
   - **Hamstring stretching:** Critical — address tight hamstrings to improve pelvic mechanics. Standing, supine, and neural-flossing techniques. Hold 30–45 seconds, 3–5 times/day
   - **Hip flexor stretching:** Thomas position, runner's lunge stretch
   - **Pectoral stretching:** Doorway stretch, foam roller bilateral arm extension

3. **Strengthening:**
   - **Thoracic extensor strengthening:** Prone cobras, back extensions (hyperextension bench), rows, pull-aparts
   - **Scapular retractors:** Rows (cable, band, dumbbell), face pulls, reverse flyes
   - **Core stabilisation:** Plank, bird-dog, dead bug — avoid excessive thoracic flexion loading
   - **Respiratory training:** Incentive spirometry, deep breathing — especially if chest expansion limited

4. **Bracing (Orthopaedic Prescription — Adolescents Only):**
   - Indicated for Cobb angle 45–70° in skeletally immature (Risser 0–3)
   - Milwaukee or TLSO brace prescribed and monitored by orthopaedics
   - Physiotherapy to support exercise compliance during bracing

5. **Postural Education:**
   - Ergonomics: desk height, monitor position, chair back support
   - Sleep position: no prone sleeping; supine with thoracic support
   - Self-monitoring of sitting posture

### Pharmacological (NICE-Aligned)

- **NSAIDs:** Ibuprofen 400mg TDS or naproxen 500mg BD + PPI for pain during acute flares (NICE NG59)
- **Topical NSAIDs:** Diclofenac gel for localised thoracic pain (NICE NG59 preferred for localised MSK)
- **Paracetamol:** Adjunct analgesia; limited evidence but commonly used
- **Vitamin D + Calcium:** If bone density concerns / adolescent with restricted diet (NICE NG187 — vitamin D supplementation)
- **Avoid:** Long-term NSAIDs in adolescents without gastroprotection; opioids not indicated

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Urgent:**
- Neurological deficit → MRI + neurosurgical referral
- Severe cardiopulmonary compromise → respiratory/cardiac referral

**Elective Orthopaedic/Spinal Surgery:**
- Cobb angle >75° with pain/functional disability unresponsive to conservative management
- Neurological compromise at any angle
- Cosmetic deformity with significant psychosocial impact after skeletal maturity
- Surgical technique: posterior spinal fusion (most common); occasionally combined anterior-posterior approach for rigid severe curves
- Success rate: >85% correction maintained at 10 years (Hosman AJF et al., 2002)
