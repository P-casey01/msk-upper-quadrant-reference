## Overview & Pathophysiology

Thoracic disc herniation (TDH) is the displacement of disc material (nucleus pulposus, annulus fibrosus, or both) beyond the intervertebral space in the thoracic spine (T1–T12). While less common than cervical or lumbar disc herniation, TDH carries significant clinical importance due to the proximity of the spinal cord and the risk of myelopathy.

### Prevalence
- Symptomatic TDH accounts for approximately **0.25–0.75% of all symptomatic disc herniations** (Wood KB et al., 1997)
- MRI studies report incidental TDH in **11–37% of asymptomatic individuals** (Wood KB et al., 1995)
- Peak age: 40–60 years; slight male predominance
- Most common levels: **T8–T12** (lower thoracic — 75% of symptomatic cases), particularly **T11–T12**

### Mechanism & Pathophysiology
- The thoracic spine is relatively immobile (rib cage stabilisation), placing lower mechanical stress compared to cervical/lumbar regions
- However, the thoracic spinal canal is narrow relative to cord diameter (cord:canal ratio ~0.4–0.7), meaning even small disc protrusions can cause significant myelopathy
- **Types:**
  - Soft disc herniation: acute, traumatic — nucleus pulposus extrusion
  - Hard disc/calcified herniation: chronic degeneration, calcification — more common in thoracic region (~70% of symptomatic TDH)
- **Direction:** Central and centrolateral protrusions most clinically significant (cord compression); lateral protrusions cause radiculopathy

### Relevant Anatomy
- Spinal cord occupies a larger proportion of the canal than in the lumbar spine
- Blood supply: anterior spinal artery (single vessel) — risk of cord ischaemia with anterior compression
- T1–T6: relatively fixed, rib articulations on both superior and inferior costovertebral joints
- T10–T12: floating ribs, relatively more mobile, therefore more susceptible to degenerative disc disease

## Special Tests

> **Critical note:** There are **no validated specific orthopaedic tests** for thoracic disc herniation comparable to lumbar provocative tests. Diagnosis is primarily clinical (symptoms + neurological examination) confirmed by MRI. The tests below assess for myelopathy or radiculopathy secondary to TDH.

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Bilateral lower limb weakness / paraparesis | Cord compression / myelopathy | **Emergency MRI + neurosurgical referral** |
| Bowel or bladder dysfunction (retention or incontinence) | Cord compression / cauda equina-equivalent | **Emergency neurosurgical referral (same day)** |
| Saddle anaesthesia | Conus medullaris compression | **Emergency referral** |
| Progressive gait disturbance / ataxia | Myelopathy | Urgent MRI within 24–48 hours |
| Night pain, constant pain, systemically unwell | Malignancy / metastatic disease | Urgent MRI + bloods (PSA, CEA, ESR, CRP, calcium, alkaline phosphatase) |
| Age >50, history of malignancy | Metastatic cord compression (MSCC) | Same-day oncological assessment; refer to MSCC coordinator (per NICE NG23) |
| Fever, IV drug use, immunosuppression + back pain | Discitis / epidural abscess | Urgent MRI + bloods (WCC, CRP, ESR, blood cultures); admit |
| Trauma + thoracic pain | Fracture / instability | X-ray ± CT; immobilise if instability suspected |
| Thoracic pain + hypotension + pulsatile mass | Aortic aneurysm/dissection | **999 — Emergency vascular** |
| Unexplained weight loss + thoracic pain | Malignancy | 2-week-wait GP referral |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Framework
- Passive accessory intervertebral movements (PAIVMs) at suspected level(s): posteroanterior (PA) central and unilateral pressures
- Passive physiological intervertebral movements (PPIVMs): flexion, extension, rotation, lateral flexion
- Note: with myelopathy, avoid forceful manipulations — Grade I–II mobilisations only pending MRI
- Establish comparable sign (symptom-reproducing movement) then reassess after intervention
- Maitland grades III–IV for pain-dominant non-myelopathic TDH

### McKenzie/MDT Framework
- Postural assessment: thoracic kyphosis assessment, correction of slouched sitting
- Directional preference: thoracic extension exercises may centralise or reduce symptoms in discogenic pain
- Extension bias: thoracic extension in prone (press-up), standing extension over foam roller
- Contraindicated if extension worsens/peripheralises symptoms — reassess direction
- Flexion bias less common in thoracic disc, but assess systematically
- Relevant for lower thoracic levels (T10–T12) with lumbar-type presentation

### Pattern Recognition Approach
- **Myelopathic pattern:** UMN signs below lesion level (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski), bilateral involvement, gait disturbance — refer urgently
- **Radiculopathic pattern:** Band-like chest/abdominal pain following dermatomal distribution, unilateral, neurological signs at level
- **Discogenic/mechanical pattern:** Central thoracic pain, worse with flexion/loading, improved with extension — conservative management appropriate

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis
- Diagnosis is made by **clinical examination + MRI**
- Physical examination alone is insufficient; MRI is the investigation of choice
- High incidence of asymptomatic MRI findings means clinical correlation is essential

### Imaging Guidelines
- **MRI thoracic spine:** Gold standard — assess disc morphology, cord signal change (T2 hyperintensity indicates myelopathy), degree of canal compromise
- **CT myelography:** Alternative when MRI contraindicated; better for calcified disc
- **Plain X-ray:** Limited utility; may show disc space narrowing, calcification, kyphosis — not diagnostic
- **CT without contrast:** Useful for identifying calcified disc (important for surgical planning — calcification changes surgical approach risk)

### Key Evidence
- Wood KB et al. (1995) demonstrated 37% prevalence of TDH on MRI in 90 asymptomatic volunteers — underscoring need for clinical correlation
- Stillerman CB et al. (1998): Retrospective review of 82 surgically treated TDH — 76% presented with myelopathy or mixed myeloradiculopathy; conservative management successful in mild cases
- Bouthors C et al. (2018): 10-year outcomes study — myelopathic TDH rarely improves without surgical decompression; 75% surgical success rate
- No Level 1 RCT evidence exists for conservative vs surgical management of TDH

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Thoracic facet joint pain | Localised paraspinal tenderness, no neurological signs, no imaging changes |
| Intercostal neuralgia | Band-like pain, dermatomal, often post-herpetic; no cord signs |
| Scheuermann's disease | Young patients, kyphotic deformity, wedge vertebrae on X-ray |
| Costochondritis | Anterior chest pain, point tenderness on rib/costal cartilage |
| Cardiac ischaemia / angina | Mid-sternal chest pain, exertional, ECG changes, troponin elevation |
| Aortic dissection | Sudden severe tearing pain, BP differential between arms, haemodynamic instability |
| Oesophageal pathology | Pain related to swallowing, GORD symptoms, relieved by antacids |
| Metastatic spinal disease | Known malignancy, constant night pain, bone destruction on imaging |
| Multiple sclerosis | Relapsing-remitting neurological symptoms, positive Lhermitte's, MRI plaques |
| Spinal cord tumour (ependymoma/astrocytoma) | Progressive myelopathy, MRI intrinsic cord lesion with enhancement |
| Osteoporotic fracture | Post-menopausal female, acute onset, deformity, DEXA scan osteoporosis |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care / Community Physiotherapy:**
- Suitable for non-myelopathic TDH with manageable pain
- GP referral to community MSK physiotherapy or direct access where available
- Red flag screen mandatory at first assessment
- If any UMN signs, bowel/bladder involvement → same-day emergency neurosurgical referral (Royal Victoria Hospital Belfast / Altnagelvin)

**Secondary Care:**
- Orthopaedic/neurosurgical referral for surgical consideration in:
  - Myelopathy (progressive or moderate-severe)
  - Failure of ≥6 weeks conservative management with severe radiculopathy
  - Significant canal compromise on MRI (>50% canal occlusion)
- Metastatic Spinal Cord Compression (MSCC) coordinator contact: per NICE NG23; NI MSCC pathway via Belfast City Hospital oncology

### Conservative Management

**Physiotherapy — Evidence Grade C (limited high-quality RCTs):**
1. **Education:** Natural history (majority of non-myelopathic TDH improves with time), activity modification, load management
2. **Posture & ergonomics:** Thoracic extension exercises, postural correction to reduce disc loading — especially lower thoracic levels
3. **Manual therapy:** Grade I–II Maitland mobilisations for pain relief; avoid Grade V manipulation without myelopathy exclusion. Soft tissue techniques to thoracic paraspinals, erector spinae
4. **Thoracic extension exercises:** McKenzie extension exercises, foam roller thoracic extension, seated thoracic rotation — for non-myelopathic cases
5. **Neural mobilisation:** Thoracic Slump sliders for radiculopathic presentation
6. **Core stability:** Graduated programme targeting deep thoracic/lumbar stabilisers — avoid high-load flexion exercises
7. **Hydrotherapy:** Buoyancy-assisted exercise — lower thoracic/mild cases

**Duration:** 6–12 weeks conservative management before surgical re-evaluation in non-myelopathic cases.

### Pharmacological (NICE-Aligned)

- **Analgesia (Step 1):** Paracetamol 1g QDS (limited evidence for back pain — NICE NG59)
- **NSAIDs (Step 2):** Ibuprofen 400mg TDS or naproxen 500mg BD with PPI (NICE NG59); caution in renal impairment, cardiovascular disease, elderly
- **Neuropathic agents:** Amitriptyline 10–75mg nocte or duloxetine 60–120mg daily for neuropathic/radicular pain (NICE NG173) — note: pregabalin/gabapentin NOT first-line per NICE 2021 update without specialist advice
- **Opioids:** Short-term weak opioids (codeine ± paracetamol) if NSAIDs insufficient — avoid long-term (NICE NG59)
- **Oral corticosteroids:** Short course dexamethasone 8mg daily (reducing) — may be used for acute myelopathic exacerbation pending neurosurgical review (off-label; neurosurgery decision)
- **Epidural steroid injection:** Thoracic epidural under image guidance — specialist pain management referral; limited evidence

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Emergency (same day):**
- Acute myelopathy with progressive paraparesis
- Bowel/bladder dysfunction — neurosurgical emergency
- Suspected MSCC (NICE NG23 — refer within 24 hours to MSCC coordinator)

**Urgent (within 2 weeks):**
- Moderate myelopathy without emergency features
- Failure of 4–6 weeks conservative management with significant disability

**Elective:**
- Persistent severe radiculopathy unresponsive to ≥12 weeks conservative management
- Surgical options: thoracoscopic discectomy (video-assisted), posterior laminectomy, transpedicular approach (selected by neurosurgeon based on level and calcification)
