## Overview & Pathophysiology

Costochondritis is inflammation of the costochondral or costo-sternal junctions causing anterior chest wall pain. It is one of the most common causes of chest pain presenting to both primary care and emergency departments, accounting for approximately **13–36% of chest pain presentations** (Proulx AM & Zryd TW, 2009).

### Distinction from Tietze Syndrome
- **Costochondritis:** Inflammation of the costochondral junctions — **no visible or palpable swelling**. Multiple levels often affected. More common.
- **Tietze Syndrome:** Localised non-suppurative inflammation with **visible/palpable swelling** at the costochondral/costo-sternal junction, typically affecting the 2nd or 3rd rib. Rarer; often younger patients.

### Prevalence & Demographics
- Accounts for 13–36% of musculoskeletal chest pain in primary care
- Peak age: 20–40 years for Tietze; broader age range for costochondritis
- Female:male ratio: approximately 3:1
- Often bilateral involvement; most commonly affects **ribs 2–5** at costo-sternal junctions

### Relevant Anatomy
- The costal cartilages connect the osseous ribs to the sternum at costo-sternal junctions
- Ribs 1–7 connect directly to the sternum (true ribs)
- Ribs 8–10 connect to costal cartilage of the rib above (false ribs)
- Ribs 11–12 are floating (no anterior attachment)
- The costo-sternal joint is a fibrous (rib 1) or synovial (ribs 2–7) joint — synovial joints may undergo inflammatory arthritis

### Mechanism & Aetiology
- Often **idiopathic**; proposed mechanisms include:
  - Repetitive microtrauma (heavy lifting, coughing, exercise)
  - Viral upper respiratory infection (preceding cough)
  - Direct chest trauma
  - Seronegative spondyloarthropathy (e.g., ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic arthritis)
  - Post-sternotomy (cardiac surgery)
  - Fibromyalgia (costochondritis as component)
  - Rarely: septic (IV drug use, immunocompromised)

## Special Tests

> **Key point:** Costochondritis is primarily a **clinical diagnosis** based on reproduction of pain with palpation of costochondral junctions. Sensitivity and specificity data specific to costochondritis is limited. The most important role of clinical examination is ruling out serious cardiac, pulmonary, and GI pathology.

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **Costochondral Palpation** (reproduction of concordant chest pain) | ~0.70–0.80 | ~0.90 | ~7.0–8.0 | ~0.22–0.33 | Low–Moderate | Key diagnostic manoeuvre; press firmly on costal cartilages 2–5 bilaterally |
| **Rib Spring Test** (lateral compression) | Limited data | Limited data | — | — | Expert opinion | Lateral compression of thorax reproducing anterior chest pain; differentiates from rib fracture |
| **Sternal Compression** | Limited data | Limited data | — | — | Expert opinion | Compression of sternum reproducing local pain |
| **Hooking Manoeuvre** | Limited data | Limited data | — | — | Expert opinion | For lower rib syndrome (ribs 8–10); hooking fingers under lower rib margin and pulling anteriorly |

### Key Diagnostic Manoeuvres (Expert Consensus)
1. **Systematic palpation of all costochondral junctions** — reproduce concordant pain at ≥1 junction
2. **Arm movements:** Pain reproduced or worsened with arm elevation, pectoral stretch
3. **Deep inspiration/cough:** Worsens costochondral pain — important history feature
4. **Response to local pressure:** Pain reproduced and correlates with patient's presenting complaint

**References:** Proulx AM & Zryd TW (2009). *American Family Physician*; Disla E et al. (1994). *Archives of Internal Medicine*.

## Red Flags

> This condition requires thorough cardiac, pulmonary, and GI exclusion before confirming a musculoskeletal diagnosis. Chest pain in any patient requires systematic red flag screening.

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Crushing/pressure central chest pain radiating to jaw/left arm | Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) | **999 — Emergency** |
| Chest pain + diaphoresis, nausea, vomiting | ACS / MI | **999 — Emergency** |
| Pleuritic chest pain + dyspnoea + haemoptysis | Pulmonary embolism | **999 — Emergency** |
| Sudden onset severe chest pain radiating to back | Aortic dissection | **999 — Emergency** |
| Chest pain + fever + productive cough + reduced air entry | Pneumonia / pleuritis | Same-day GP; chest X-ray |
| Chest pain + weight loss + haemoptysis in smoker | Lung malignancy | 2WW GP referral |
| Swelling/redness over costochondral junction in IVDU or immunocompromised | Septic costochondritis | Urgent A&E / bloods |
| Chest pain in patient with known malignancy | Metastatic disease to ribs/sternum | Urgent oncology review |
| ECG changes (ST elevation/depression, new LBBB) | ACS | **Emergency** |
| Chest pain + haemodynamic instability | Tension pneumothorax/PE/dissection | **999** |
| Costochondral tenderness + sacroiliitis/uveitis/IBD | Seronegative spondyloarthropathy | Rheumatology referral |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Framework
- Costochondritis does not directly fit Maitland's intervertebral movement assessment
- However, thoracic spine hypomobility at the costovertebral joints (T2–T7) may contribute to anterior chest wall pain
- Assess PA central and unilateral pressures at mid-thoracic spine
- Rib mobilisation (PA on rib angle posteriorly) may reduce anterior chest wall pain
- Evaluate costo-sternal joint mobility directly (cautious anterior pressure on costal cartilage)

### McKenzie/MDT Framework
- Thoracic kyphosis correction may reduce sternal/costo-sternal compression anteriorly
- Extension exercises (thoracic extension over foam roller, doorway pectoral stretches) open the anterior chest
- Useful when combined with poor posture (forward head, protracted shoulder girdle) as contributing factor
- Patient education: postural correction, activity modification (avoid provocative movements during acute phase)

### Pattern Recognition
- **Mechanical costochondritis:** Reproducible with palpation, positional, worse with specific movements/activities; better with rest; no cardiac risk factors
- **Inflammatory costochondritis (spondyloarthropathy):** Bilateral, insidious onset, morning stiffness, associated HLA-B27 features, elevated CRP/ESR
- **Post-infectious:** Following viral URTI/cough; diffuse, bilateral, settles over 4–12 weeks
- **Post-traumatic:** Direct blow or sternotomy; localised, appropriate history

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Criteria
There are **no validated formal diagnostic criteria** for costochondritis. Diagnosis is clinical:
1. Chest pain that is **reproducible with palpation** of costochondral/costo-sternal junctions
2. **No swelling** (swelling = Tietze syndrome)
3. Exclusion of cardiac, pulmonary, and GI causes
4. Pain reproducible with trunk movements or deep inspiration

### Systematic Review Evidence
- Disla E et al. (1994): Prospective study of 122 patients presenting to ED with chest pain — 30% had reproducible chest wall tenderness; however, 6% of those also had concurrent MI → **chest wall tenderness does NOT exclude ACS**
- Fruergaard P et al. (1996): 204 patients with acute chest pain — 13% diagnosed with costochondritis/musculoskeletal cause after cardiac exclusion
- Levy MJ (2021): Systematic review of MSK chest pain — costochondritis most common MSK diagnosis; resolution in most cases within 12 months

### Imaging
- **Generally not indicated** for straightforward presentation
- **CXR:** Required if pulmonary cause not excluded; normal in costochondritis
- **ECG:** Mandatory in any chest pain presentation — to exclude ACS
- **Cardiac troponin / D-dimer:** If clinical presentation raises ACS/PE concern (GP/ED decision)
- **Ultrasound:** May show costochondral joint hypoechogenicity in Tietze syndrome; limited for costochondritis
- **Bone scan / MRI:** If malignancy or stress fracture suspected; increased uptake in Tietze on bone scan

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Acute Coronary Syndrome | Central/crushing, diaphoresis, ECG changes, troponin rise; NOT position-dependent; not reproducible with palpation |
| Pericarditis | Pleuritic chest pain, worse lying flat, better leaning forward; pericardial rub; ECG changes (saddle ST) |
| Pulmonary Embolism | Pleuritic pain, dyspnoea, hypoxia, Wells score, D-dimer, CTPA |
| Pneumonia / Pleuritis | Fever, productive cough, reduced air entry; CXR consolidation |
| Aortic Dissection | Sudden severe tearing pain, BP differential, haemodynamic instability |
| Rib Fracture | History of trauma, bony tenderness, X-ray/CT confirmation |
| Tietze Syndrome | Visible/palpable swelling at costochondral junction; localised |
| Herpes Zoster (Shingles) | Dermatomal burning pain, vesicular rash (may precede rash by 3–7 days) |
| Seronegative Spondyloarthropathy | Bilateral chest wall pain, morning stiffness, sacroiliitis, enthesopathy, HLA-B27 |
| Fibromyalgia | Widespread pain, multiple tender points, fatigue, poor sleep |
| Gastro-oesophageal reflux | Burning, retrosternal, relation to meals, improved with antacids |
| Oesophageal spasm | Severe, episodic, can mimic cardiac; relieved by GTN (problematic for diagnosis) |
| Intercostal neuralgia | Band-like, dermatomal, often post-herpetic |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care / Community:**
- GP assessment mandatory to exclude cardiac/pulmonary causes (ECG, CXR, clinical assessment)
- Once excluded → community physiotherapy referral (self-referral or GP referral)
- Typically managed in community MSK physiotherapy across all HSC Trusts
- If seronegative spondyloarthropathy suspected → rheumatology referral

**Secondary Care:**
- Rarely required for simple costochondritis
- Rheumatology if inflammatory arthropathy suspected
- Pain management referral if >3 months, severe, and not responding to conservative management
- Orthopaedic/surgical referral not typically indicated

### Conservative Management

**Physiotherapy — Evidence Grade C (limited RCTs; expert consensus):**

1. **Patient Education (Grade A — expert consensus):**
   - Benign, self-limiting condition; most resolve within 12 months
   - Activity modification during acute phase — avoid provocative heavy lifting, pushing, pulling
   - Avoid positions that stretch costochondral junctions (e.g., reaching overhead during acute phase)
   - Reassurance is a key therapeutic intervention — reduces anxiety-driven symptom amplification

2. **Relative Rest then Graded Return to Activity:**
   - Acute phase (0–2 weeks): Avoid provocative activities
   - Subacute phase (2–6 weeks): Gradual reintroduction of upper limb/thoracic activities
   - Return to sport/work: Progressive loading programme

3. **Manual Therapy:**
   - Thoracic joint mobilisation (PA central/unilateral at mid-thoracic levels) — may reduce referred anterior chest pain
   - Rib mobilisation (posteroanterior on rib angles)
   - Soft tissue therapy: pectoralis minor, intercostal muscles, sternalis

4. **Exercise Therapy:**
   - Thoracic extension exercises (foam roller, doorway stretch)
   - Pectoral stretching — cautious in acute phase
   - Deep breathing exercises (diaphragmatic) to maintain thoracic cage mobility
   - Gradual strengthening: scapular stabilisers, thoracic extensors

5. **Physical Modalities:**
   - **TENS:** May provide short-term pain relief (Grade C)
   - **Ice/Heat:** Patient preference; ice for acute inflammation, heat for chronic
   - **Ultrasound:** Limited evidence; may consider for Tietze syndrome

6. **Acupuncture:** Limited evidence; NICE supports acupuncture for chronic primary pain (NICE NG193, 2021)

### Pharmacological (NICE-Aligned)

- **NSAIDs (first-line):** Ibuprofen 400mg TDS or naproxen 500mg BD with PPI cover — 2–4 weeks (NICE NG59)
- **Topical NSAIDs:** Diclofenac gel (3x/day) — NICE preferred for localised musculoskeletal pain (NICE NG59); fewer systemic side effects
- **Paracetamol:** Limited evidence for musculoskeletal pain (NICE NG59) but useful adjunct
- **Corticosteroid injection:** Local corticosteroid (methylprednisolone 20–40mg) + local anaesthetic injection at costo-sternal junction — under GP/pain clinic guidance; useful for refractory cases. Evidence from case series (Waldman SD, 2009)
- **Avoid long-term opioids** for costochondritis — not indicated per NICE NG59

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Back to GP (urgent):**
- Any new cardiac/pulmonary red flag identified during physiotherapy assessment
- Features suggesting inflammatory arthropathy (bilateral, morning stiffness, sacroiliitis)
- No improvement after 6–8 weeks conservative management — consider imaging and bloods

**Rheumatology:**
- Suspected seronegative spondyloarthropathy
- Bilateral costochondritis + elevated inflammatory markers

**Pain Management:**
- Persistent costochondritis >3 months unresponsive to conservative management
- For consideration of corticosteroid injection or pain management programme

**Surgical (rare):**
- Septic costochondritis — surgical debridement (infectious disease/orthopaedic/thoracic surgery)
