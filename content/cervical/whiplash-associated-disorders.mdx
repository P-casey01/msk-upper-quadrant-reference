## Overview & Pathophysiology

Whiplash Associated Disorders (WAD) is the term describing the constellation of symptoms following an acceleration-deceleration injury to the cervical spine, most commonly from motor vehicle collisions (MVCs). The Québec Task Force (QTF) on Whiplash-Associated Disorders (Spitzer et al., 1995) provides the foundational classification framework.

**Epidemiology:**
- Annual incidence: 235–300 per 100,000 in Western countries
- UK: ~250,000–300,000 per year; high rates linked to insurance claims (CEGA/ABI data)
- Most common MVC injury type; estimated annual cost to NHS and insurance >£2 billion
- Female:Male ratio ~1.4:1
- Peak incidence: 20–40 years

**Mechanism of Injury:**
- Rear-end collision most common: initial extension of lower cervical spine followed by rapid flexion of upper cervical spine (S-curve deformation)
- This S-shaped motion is different from simple extension — it causes:
  - Stretching of anterior longitudinal ligament
  - Compression of posterior elements (facet joints, nerve roots)
  - Differential motion between segments with relative translation
  - Strain of zygapophyseal joint capsules (most consistent pain source)
- Side-impact and frontal collisions also produce WAD with different biomechanics
- No single lesion visible on standard imaging in most cases (hence "soft tissue injury")

**Pathological pain sources:**
1. **Zygapophyseal (facet) joints** — most consistently identified pain source; Barnsley et al. (1995) demonstrated by diagnostic nerve blocks that facet joint pain present in ~54% of chronic WAD
2. **Intervertebral discs**: Annular tears, especially C5/6 and C6/7
3. **Musculature**: Multifidus, semispinalis, suboccipitals — MRI evidence of fatty infiltration and atrophy in chronic WAD (Elliott et al., 2006)
4. **Neural tissue**: Dorsal root ganglia sensitisation; central sensitisation
5. **Ligamentous injury**: Alar, transverse, C2–3 ligaments (rare but important in higher grades)
6. **Sympathetic nervous system involvement**: autonomic dysregulation; links to widespread hypersensitivity

**Neurobiological Mechanisms:**
- Peripheral sensitisation: nociceptors in joint capsule, disc sensitised by mechanical and chemical stimuli
- Central sensitisation: key driver of chronic WAD — explains widespread hypersensitivity, cold hyperalgesia, psychological distress seen in Grade III WAD
- Psychosocial factors amplify and perpetuate sensitisation

**Québec Task Force Classification:**

| Grade | Clinical Presentation |
|-------|-----------------------|
| WAD 0 | No neck complaints, no physical signs |
| WAD I | Neck complaint (pain, stiffness, tenderness), no physical signs |
| WAD II | Neck complaint + musculoskeletal signs (reduced ROM, point tenderness) |
| WAD III | Neck complaint + neurological signs (reflex changes, weakness, sensory deficit) |
| WAD IV | Neck complaint + fracture or dislocation |

WAD III is analogous to cervical radiculopathy following whiplash mechanism.

**Prognostic Risk Stratification — Prescreening:**
Use **WAD Prognostic Screening Tool (Sterling 2010)** or **Neck Disability Index ≥30% at 1 month** as predictor of chronic outcome.

**Prediction of Non-Recovery (Sterling et al., 2005, 2010):**
Elevated psychological distress + high pain intensity at 4 weeks + sensory hypersensitivity → high risk for non-recovery at 12 months

**Assessment priorities in WAD:**
1. **ROM measurement** (CROM device or goniometer): all planes; identify pain provocation and end-range quality
2. **Neurological screening** (WAD III): reflexes, motor, sensation
3. **Quantitative Sensory Testing (QST)**: pressure pain thresholds (PPT) with algometer if available — identifies central sensitisation
4. **Psychological screening**: Impact of Event Scale-Revised (IES-R), Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK), Örebro MSK Questionnaire (Short Form)
5. **Cervical artery dysfunction screening** (IFOMPT 2012): given mechanism, screen before manual therapy

## Red Flags

**Canadian C-Spine Rule (Stiell et al., 2001) — apply to ALL whiplash presentations:**

High-risk factors (X-ray/CT indicated regardless of other findings):
- Age ≥65
- Dangerous mechanism (fall from >1m height or 5 stairs; axial load to head; high-speed MVC >100 km/h; rollover; ejection; motorised recreational vehicle; bicycle collision)
- Paraesthesia in extremities

Low-risk factors (allow safe assessment — if any present AND no high-risk factors AND can rotate 45°, imaging not required):
- Simple rear-end MVC
- Sitting in ED
- Ambulatory at any time since collision
- Delayed onset of neck pain
- Absence of midline cervical tenderness

NEXUS Criteria (alternative — imaging NOT needed if ALL 5 met):
1. No midline cervical tenderness
2. No focal neurological deficit
3. Normal alertness
4. No intoxication
5. No distracting painful injury

**WAD IV (fracture/dislocation):**
- Do NOT mobilise cervical spine
- Refer to ED/trauma team immediately
- Cervical immobilisation until cleared

**Cervical Artery Dysfunction (post-trauma):**
- Traumatic vertebral artery dissection can present as WAD
- Symptoms: severe occipital/neck pain, unilateral facial numbness, Horner's syndrome, ataxia, brainstem signs
- 5 D's + 3 N's
- CTA/MRA required if suspected → Emergency

**Spinal cord injury indicators:**
- Bilateral paraesthesia, weakness
- Bladder/bowel dysfunction
- Hyperreflexia, Hoffman's sign
- Lhermitte's sign

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Concept
- Acute WAD (0–6 weeks): Grade I–II oscillatory techniques for pain modulation; gentle cervical mobilisation
- Subacute/chronic (>6 weeks): Progress to Grade III–IV techniques addressing stiffness; PAIVMs/PPIVMs
- Comparable sign methodology: identify and track comparable pain response
- Avoid techniques that peripheralise neurological symptoms (WAD III)

### McKenzie / MDT
- Acute WAD: pain frequently multidirectional initially; retraction exercises as starting point
- Look for directional preference (may be slower to emerge than in disc pathology)
- If centralisation occurs → directional exercise
- Education re: natural history and active recovery integral to MDT approach

### Graded Activity / Behavioural Approach
- Evidence supports active management over passive rest in WAD I–II (Rosenfeld et al., 2003)
- Graded activity exposure: progressive increase in meaningful activities despite pain
- Graded motor imagery (GMI) and mirror therapy: limited evidence; consider in complex chronic WAD

### Psychologically Informed Practice (PiP)
- Critical in medium-high risk WAD (IES-R ≥26, TSK ≥37)
- Address fear-avoidance, catastrophising, catastrophic thinking about injury
- CBT-based physiotherapy (Linton & Andersson, 2000 principles)
- Link with psychology services for high-risk presentations

### Sterling's Risk-Stratified Approach (2010)
- **Low risk (WAD I–II, low distress)**: Self-management advice, reassurance, brief physiotherapy
- **Moderate risk (WAD II–III, moderate distress)**: 8–10 sessions physiotherapy + progressive exercise + psychological support
- **High risk (high distress, widespread hyperalgesia)**: Intensive physiotherapy + psychology liaison + pharmacological management of central sensitisation

## Outcome Measures

### Neck Disability Index (NDI)
- Primary outcome for WAD disability
- **MCID**: 7.5 points (50-point scale); chronic WAD: 8.5 points (Jull et al., 2014)
- Use at baseline, 6 weeks, 3 months, 6 months, 12 months
- NDI ≥30% at 1 month predicts chronic disability

### Numeric Pain Rating Scale (NPRS)
- 0–10; **MCID**: 2.1 points in WAD (Sterling et al., 2010)
- Measure arm pain (if WAD III) and neck pain separately

### Patient-Specific Functional Scale (PSFS)
- Highly responsive to change; **MCID**: 2.0 points average
- Functional activities specific to patient's life/work

### Impact of Event Scale-Revised (IES-R)
- 22-item PTSD screening tool; score 0–88
- ≥26: moderate impact (risk of PTSD); ≥33: probable PTSD
- Not an outcome measure per se but critical prognostic screener in WAD
- Re-administer at 4 weeks if initially borderline

### Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK)
- 17-item scale; 17–68; ≥37 = high kinesiophobia
- **MCID**: 4 points; use alongside FABQ to guide psychosocial management

### GROC (Global Rating of Change)
- 15-point scale; meaningful improvement ≥+5
- 6 weeks, 3 months, discharge

### Whiplash Disability Questionnaire (WDQ)
- 13-item WAD-specific disability questionnaire; 0–130
- **MCID**: 13.3 points; validated specifically in whiplash populations (Pinfold et al., 2004)

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**No imaging required for WAD 0–II without high-risk features (Canadian C-Spine Rule).**

**Imaging guidelines:**
- X-ray: rule out fracture/dislocation; dynamic X-rays (flexion/extension) for suspected instability — limited sensitivity
- CT: preferred for high-energy trauma; superior bony detail; rule out fracture, SCIWORA
- MRI: WAD III (neurological signs) or if neurological deterioration; assess disc, cord, nerve root; also for suspected ligamentous injury
- CTA/MRA: Suspected traumatic vertebral or internal carotid artery dissection — urgent

**Prognostic factors (strong evidence — Walton et al., 2013 systematic review):**
- High initial pain intensity (NRS ≥5): negative prognostic factor
- High initial disability (NDI ≥30%): negative prognostic factor
- Cold hyperalgesia: negative prognostic factor (central sensitisation marker)
- Psychological distress (IES-R, anxiety): negative prognostic factor
- Low self-efficacy: negative prognostic factor
- Compensation claims in some jurisdictions: controversial; complex psychosocial interaction

**Active management vs. rest:**
- Rosenfeld et al. (2003) RCT: Active treatment (early mobilisation) superior to collar/rest at 6 months and 3 years
- Söderlund & Lindberg (2001): early activation combined with education reduces progression to chronicity

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| Cervical fracture/dislocation (WAD IV) | High-energy mechanism, midline tenderness, neurological deficit, X-ray/CT confirms |
| Traumatic vertebral artery dissection | Severe occipital headache, brainstem signs, Horner's, ataxia — CTA/MRI |
| Traumatic disc herniation (WAD III) | Radicular symptoms, positive Spurling's/distraction, MRI confirmation |
| Concussion / mild TBI | Associated head impact, cognitive symptoms, headache, vestibular signs |
| TMJ injury (post-MVC) | Jaw pain, crepitus, limited mouth opening, pain with chewing |
| PTSD | Psychological re-experiencing, avoidance, hyperarousal — IES-R ≥33; requires psychology |
| Shoulder injury (rotator cuff) | Mechanism of arm bracing, shoulder-localised pain, arc test |
| Thoracic outlet syndrome | Vascular/neurogenic symptoms with arm elevation, positional |
| SCIWORA | Spinal cord injury without radiological abnormality — MRI required |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Acute WAD (0–12 weeks):**
- GP/ED → early physiotherapy referral (target within 2–4 weeks of injury)
- Community MSK physiotherapy as primary treatment for WAD I–II
- Evidence supports early active physiotherapy over collar/rest (Rosenfeld et al., 2003)
- Insurance/medico-legal context: Physiotherapists should document objectively; be aware of PSNI/motor insurer reporting norms
- **Immediate Access Physiotherapy** at ED/MIU available in some NI ED departments for WAD I–II

**Subacute/Chronic WAD (>12 weeks):**
- Continue community physiotherapy with biopsychosocial approach
- Pain Clinic referral for persistent severe pain or suspected central sensitisation
- Psychology referral for high IES-R, confirmed PTSD features
- Spinal Surgery referral if WAD III with confirmed disc herniation/radiculopathy not responding to 12 weeks conservative management

**Medico-Legal Considerations (NI specific):**
- Personal Injury Assessment Board (PIAB) / Compensation Recovery Unit processes
- Whiplash Reform Programme (England & Wales) — different legislation applies; NI follows Civil Liability (Contribution) Act 1978 and common law
- Standardised assessment and objective documentation essential

### Conservative Management

**Active Treatment — Grade A evidence:**

**Exercise:**
- Early active mobilisation: gentle cervical ROM exercises from day 1 post-injury (Rosenfeld et al., 2003 — significantly better outcomes than immobilisation)
- Progressive cervical and scapulothoracic strengthening — as per mechanical neck pain protocol
- Deep neck flexor activation (CCFT protocol) — impaired in acute and chronic WAD (Jull et al., 2008)
- Proprioceptive/sensorimotor exercises: laser pointer gaze stability, joint position error retraining

**Manual Therapy — Grade B:**
- Cervical mobilisation: Maitland Grade I–II → III–IV as irritability settles
- Thoracic manipulation: safer alternative to cervical HVLA; Grade A evidence as adjunct
- Cervical HVLA: use with caution; screen for CAD (IFOMPT 2012); informed consent mandatory; avoid in acute WAD with signs of instability or high IES-R

**Education — Grade A:**
- Most powerful single intervention for acute WAD (with exercise)
- Reassurance: natural history is favourable in WAD I–II; >70% recover within 3 months
- Pain neuroscience education: address sensitisation, avoid catastrophising
- Activity continuation messaging: "keep moving, stay active"
- Whiplash patient information booklet (RACGP/Arthritis Australia evidence-based resources applicable to NI)

**Physical Modalities:**
- Heat/ice: minimal evidence; low harm; patient-directed short-term use
- TENS: limited evidence; may provide short-term pain relief
- Ultrasound/laser: insufficient evidence; not recommended as primary treatment
- Acupuncture: Moderate evidence for short-term pain relief in chronic neck pain; NICE allows referral

**Psychological Approaches (High-Risk WAD):**
- CBT-based physiotherapy: fear-avoidance model
- Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing (EMDR): for confirmed PTSD — psychology-delivered
- Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) principles incorporated into physiotherapy

### Pharmacological (NICE-aligned)

**Acute (<6 weeks):**
- Paracetamol 1g QDS: first-line
- NSAIDs (Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS or Naproxen 500 mg BD): 2–4 weeks; with food; GI protection if indicated
- Short-term muscle relaxant (Diazepam 2 mg TDS, maximum 5–7 days): for acute severe muscle spasm only
- Avoid opioids in acute WAD: NICE NG193 (2021) — poor evidence, dependency risk

**Subacute/Chronic (>6 weeks):**
- Neuropathic agents if sensory hypersensitivity features: Gabapentin/Pregabalin — NICE NG173
- Amitriptyline (10–30 mg nocte): sleep, pain modulation, low-dose
- Avoid long-term opioids: NICE NG193 does not recommend for chronic primary pain

**Injections:**
- Medial branch blocks / radiofrequency neurotomy for confirmed facet joint pain (Barnsley et al., 1995; Lord et al., 1996): evidence-based for chronic WAD with confirmed facet pain; refer to Pain Clinic/Spinal service

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Emergency:**
- Suspected fracture, dislocation (WAD IV) → ED
- Suspected CAD/vertebral artery dissection → ED + vascular/neurosurgery
- Acute spinal cord injury signs → ED

**Urgent:**
- WAD III with progressive neurological deficit → spine surgery
- Suspected PTSD → psychology within 4 weeks

**Routine:**
- Failure of 12 weeks conservative management → consider Pain Clinic
- Persistent WAD III → spine surgery opinion
- High psychological distress not responding to PiP → formal psychology/CBT referral
