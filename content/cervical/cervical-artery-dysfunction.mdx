## Overview & Pathophysiology

Cervical Artery Dysfunction (CAD) encompasses pathological conditions of the vertebral arteries (VA) and internal carotid arteries (ICA) in the cervical region that may present with symptoms mimicking musculoskeletal neck pain or headache — and may be precipitated or exacerbated by cervical spine movement or manipulation.

**This is a critical safety topic for all physiotherapists performing cervical assessment and treatment.**

### Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency (VBI) / Vertebral Artery Dissection (VAD)
- **Vertebral arteries (VA)**: Arise from subclavian arteries; enter transverse foramina at C6, ascend through C6–C1, pierce dura, enter skull via foramen magnum, and unite to form basilar artery
- **Internal carotid arteries (ICA)**: Ascend anterior to VA; enter petrous canal; supply anterior circulation
- VA supplies posterior brain circulation: brainstem, cerebellum, occipital lobe, medial temporal lobe

**Pathology:**
1. **Atherosclerotic VBI**: Progressive stenosis of VA/basilar artery; older patients (>55); embolic or haemodynamic ischaemia; symptoms often positional but mechanical compression less important than atheroma/embolism
2. **Vertebral Artery Dissection (VAD)**: Intimal tear → intramural haematoma → stenosis, occlusion, or thromboembolism; may be spontaneous or trauma-related (whiplash, manipulation, trivial trauma); peak incidence 40s
3. **Internal Carotid Artery Dissection (ICAD)**: Similar mechanism; anterior circulation TIA/stroke; classically presents with neck pain + ipsilateral Horner's syndrome + contralateral focal deficit
4. **Bow Hunter's Syndrome**: Dynamic VA occlusion with head rotation, usually at C1/2

**Risk factors for dissection:**
- Connective tissue disorders (Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan's, fibromuscular dysplasia) — strongest risk factors
- Hypertension
- Recent infection (especially URTI — inflammatory arteriopathy)
- Oral contraceptive use
- Migraine with aura
- Prior dissection
- Recent trauma (whiplash, manipulation, trivial neck strain)

**Incidence:**
- Spontaneous VAD: ~1–1.5 per 100,000/year
- ICAD: ~2.6 per 100,000/year
- Stroke associated with cervical manipulation: estimated 1–3 per 100,000 manipulations (Cassidy et al., 2008) — controversial; observed association may represent consultation bias (patients with prodromal dissection seek treatment)
- VAD accounts for ~20% of strokes in patients aged <45 years (Dziewas et al., 2003)

## Special Tests

**Critical caveat: Pre-manipulative screening tests for CAD have poor diagnostic accuracy and should NOT be used as a sole safety screen. Clinical decision-making must be based on comprehensive history and risk assessment.**

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-------|----------------|
| Vertebral Artery Test (VAT) / DeKleyn's Test | 0.00–0.57 | 0.67–1.00 | Unacceptably low sensitivity; FALSE NEGATIVE rate very high; DO NOT use to clear for manipulation | Low (Rivett et al., 1999; Kerry et al., 2008) |
| Hautant's Test | ~0.00–0.06 | High | No evidence base as safety screen; not recommended | Very Low |
| Hallpike-Dix Test | Used for BPPV differentiation; not a CAD test | — | — | Moderate (for BPPV) |
| Sustained end-range rotation (10 seconds each way) | Very low sensitivity for CAD | — | Historical test; NOT recommended as safety screen | Low |
| Rancourt's Test (sustained rotation in flexion) | No published data supporting diagnostic accuracy | — | — | Very Low |

**IFOMPT 2012 Consensus Statement (updated 2017):**
- Pre-manipulative testing for VBI (VAT, Hautant's, DeKleyn's) **should not be used** as a means of clearing patients for cervical manipulation — insufficient sensitivity/specificity
- Comprehensive **clinical history and risk factor analysis** is the cornerstone of pre-treatment screening

**What TO use instead — IFOMPT Framework:**

**History-taking red flags for CAD (5 D's + 3 N's + additional):**

| Symptom | Clinical Significance |
|---------|-----------------------|
| **Dizziness** | Vertebrobasilar ischaemia; differentiate from cervicogenic (positional, brief, associated with neck movement) |
| **Diplopia** | Brainstem ischaemia (CN III, IV, VI involvement) |
| **Dysarthria** | Bulbar ischaemia |
| **Dysphagia** | Medullary involvement |
| **Drop attacks** | Sudden loss of postural tone — vertebrobasilar |
| **Nausea** | Dorsal vagal complex ischaemia |
| **Numbness** (face/body) | Lateral medullary/trigeminal involvement |
| **Nystagmus** | Vestibulocerebellum ischaemia |
| **Ataxia** | Cerebellar ischaemia |
| **Headache** (new, severe, "thunderclap") | Subarachnoid haemorrhage or arterial dissection |
| **Horner's Syndrome** (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) | ICA or VA dissection |
| **Neck pain** (new, severe, ipsilateral occipital) | Dissection prodrome |

## Red Flags

**STOP — Do NOT proceed with manual therapy if:**

1. **New, severe, or unusual headache** — especially "thunderclap" (worst headache of life) → Emergency
2. **Bilateral or quadrilateral neurological symptoms** → Urgent investigation
3. **Ataxia or gait disturbance** combined with neck pain
4. **Diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia** — brainstem signs
5. **Drop attacks** — sudden loss of postural tone
6. **Horner's syndrome** (ipsilateral ptosis + miosis ± anhidrosis) combined with neck pain → Suspected ICA/VA dissection → Emergency
7. **Any positive cranial nerve finding** in context of neck pain
8. **Symptoms that worsen in sustained cervical rotation or extension**
9. **Recent trauma** (whiplash, blow to head, forceful neck manipulation) with new neurological symptoms
10. **Known connective tissue disorder** (EDS, Marfan's) — relative contraindication to cervical HVLA

**Absolute contraindications to cervical HVLA:**
- Active or suspected arterial dissection
- Confirmed cervical instability (os odontoideum, AAI in RA/Down's/JIA)
- Vertebrobasilar symptoms provoked by cervical movement
- Myelopathy signs
- Fracture/dislocation
- Malignancy at cervical level
- Anticoagulation (relative — not absolute; risk-benefit discussion)
- Osteoporosis with active vertebral fractures

**Signs of acute stroke/TIA — Call 999 (FAST + BE-FAST):**
- **B**alance loss, **E**yes (diplopia/vision loss), **F**ace drooping, **A**rm weakness, **S**peech difficulty, **T**ime to call 999
- Act FAST: do NOT delay by attempting physiotherapy assessment

## Clinical Frameworks

### IFOMPT International Framework (2012, updated 2017)
The International Federation of Orthopaedic Manipulative Physical Therapists (IFOMPT) framework for management of CAD risk:

**Three-stage clinical decision process:**

1. **Stage 1 — Pre-examination screen:**
   - Comprehensive history of dizziness, headache, neurological symptoms
   - CVD risk factors, connective tissue disorders, medications (anticoagulants)
   - Recent trauma

2. **Stage 2 — Physical examination:**
   - Assess provocation of symptoms with active ROM
   - Sustained positions assessment (not VAT)
   - VBI symptom onset ≥2 minutes sustained position more significant than <30 seconds
   - Neurological examination

3. **Stage 3 — Decision:**
   - If ANY concern → do NOT perform cervical HVLA; consider alternative techniques
   - Document reasoning
   - Share decision-making with patient (informed consent including CAD risk)

### AHA/ASA Scientific Statement (Biller et al., 2014)
- Clinicians should inform patients of the extremely small risk of stroke associated with cervical manipulation
- Shared decision-making and informed consent required
- Prefer cervical mobilisation over HVLA where risk factors present

### Differential Vertebrogenic Dizziness Framework
Distinguish cervicogenic dizziness from vestibular causes:

| Feature | Cervicogenic Dizziness | Vestibular (BPPV/Labyrinthitis) | VBI |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------------------|-----|
| Onset | With neck movement | Head position change | Sustained rotation/extension |
| Duration | Proportional to neck movement | Seconds (BPPV) or days (labyrinthitis) | Seconds–minutes |
| Associated | Neck pain, stiffness | Nausea, positional (Hallpike positive) | Diplopia, dysarthria, ataxia |
| Neurological | None | None | Positive CNS signs |

## Outcome Measures

**Primary goal: SAFETY — accurate identification and appropriate management of CAD risk**

Formal validated outcome measures for the management of CAD are limited — this is primarily a triage/screening condition rather than a primary physiotherapy treatment condition.

**Tools used in assessment:**

### Dizziness Handicap Inventory (DHI)
- 25 items; 0–100 (0=no handicap, 100=maximum)
- Used for dizziness-related disability (differentiate from cervicogenic vs. vestibular)
- **MCID**: 11 points (Whitney et al., 2004)

### Neck Disability Index (NDI)
- For associated neck pain disability tracking
- **MCID**: 7.5/50 points

### NPRS (0–10)
- For headache and neck pain severity
- **MCID**: 2.1 points

### Neurological Examination Documentation
- Serial documentation of cranial nerve findings, upper and lower limb neurology
- Essential for monitoring improvement or deterioration

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**Investigation of Suspected CAD:**

**MRI + MRA (Magnetic Resonance Angiography):**
- First-line investigation for suspected VA or ICA dissection
- T1 fat-saturated axial images: intramural haematoma appears as crescentic hyperintensity surrounding lumen
- MRA: demonstrates stenosis, occlusion, or "flame sign" of dissection
- Sensitivity: ~84% for acute VAD (within 48 hours)

**CTA (CT Angiography):**
- Faster than MRI; better availability in ED
- Sensitivity: ~94–100% for arterial dissection (Provenzale et al., 2003)
- Higher radiation dose; may miss subacute dissection better seen on MRI

**Doppler Ultrasound:**
- Limited sensitivity for VA dissection (poor vessel visualisation at skull base)
- Useful for ICA stenosis screening (carotid atheromatous disease)
- Sensitivity for VAD: ~70%; not sufficient to exclude dissection

**Conventional DSA (Digital Subtraction Angiography):**
- Historical gold standard; now reserved for intervention (thrombolysis, stenting)
- Invasive; risk of stroke ~0.3–0.5%

**Clinical criteria for diagnosis (Brandt criteria for VAD):**
1. Local pain (neck/occipital/periorbital)
2. New neurological deficit
3. Imaging evidence of dissection

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| BPPV | Positive Hallpike-Dix, brief (<60 sec) rotational vertigo, no brainstem signs |
| Vestibular neuritis / Labyrinthitis | Sustained vertigo, hearing loss (labyrinthitis), no neck pain, normal MRI |
| Cervicogenic dizziness | Neck pain + dizziness proportional to cervical ROM, positive FRT, no brainstem signs |
| Subarachnoid haemorrhage | Thunderclap headache, worst of life, neck stiffness, photophobia, CT/LP |
| Posterior fossa tumour | Progressive headache, ataxia, cranial nerve deficit, MRI |
| Migraine with aura | Headache + aura (visual, sensory), family history, no focal deficit, recurrent |
| Multiple sclerosis | Relapsing-remitting, MRI white matter lesions, young adult |
| Brainstem TIA | Transient (<24h), multiple deficits, DWI-MRI, cardiac source |
| Mechanical neck pain | No neurological signs, ROM limited but symptoms peripheral, predictable |
| Orthostatic hypotension | Dizziness with positional change (lying → standing), HR/BP response |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Suspected acute CAD/Dissection:**
- Call 999 / Transfer to ED IMMEDIATELY
- Do NOT perform further cervical treatment
- Administer no physical intervention that could extend or propagate dissection
- **Belfast stroke pathway**: Royal Victoria Hospital / Antrim Area Hospital TIA / stroke assessment
- TIA/Stroke thrombolysis window: 4.5 hours for IV tPA (if ischaemic stroke)
- HSC Regional Stroke Network: all suspected stroke should go to nearest hyperacute stroke unit (HASU)

**Confirmed dissection — acute management (medical team):**
- Anticoagulation vs. antiplatelet therapy: clinical equipoise; CADISS trial (Markus et al., 2019) — no significant difference between aspirin and anticoagulation for VAD stroke prevention
- Antiplatelet (aspirin 300 mg loading, then 75 mg maintenance) or anticoagulation depending on stroke risk profile
- Haematology/vascular neurology input
- Endovascular intervention: stenting for severe stenosis/pseudoaneurysm — specialist decision

**Physiotherapy role in confirmed CAD:**
- NEVER primary treatment provider for acute CAD
- Post-acute rehabilitation (post-stroke/TIA): multidisciplinary neurological rehabilitation
- Subsequent neck pain (post-dissection healing): gentle pain-free mobilisation, NO HVLA, NO traction
- Minimum 3–6 months post-dissection before cervical manual therapy resumption; only with specialist clearance

**Preventing iatrogenic CAD:**
- Apply IFOMPT framework at EVERY new cervical spine assessment
- Document pre-treatment screening
- Obtain verbal informed consent including CVA risk prior to cervical HVLA
- Consider thoracic manipulation as lower-risk alternative for most neck pain indications
- Know your absolute contraindications

### Conservative Management

**For VBI from atherosclerosis (stable, not acute dissection):**
- Medical management (antiplatelet, statin, BP management) is primary
- Physiotherapy: avoid provocative cervical positions; treat associated neck pain with GENTLE Grade I–II mobilisation
- Vestibular rehabilitation if chronic vertiginous symptoms (with medical clearance)

**For cervicogenic dizziness (not VBI):**
- Manual therapy to C1/2 (IFOMPT-compliant, non-HVLA technique)
- Proprioceptive retraining, gaze stability exercises
- Cervical flexion-rotation test (FRT) to confirm C1/2 involvement (Hall et al., 2010; Sens. 91%, Spec. 90%)

**Post-dissection rehabilitation (neurology/physio liaison):**
- Gradual return of cervical mobility (pain-free range)
- No mobilisation of dissected segment until confirmed imaging resolution (typically 3–6 months)
- Core, scapular, and peripheral strengthening safe from early stage
- Address psychological impact (anxiety, PTSD) — dissection can be traumatic event

### Pharmacological (Medical Team-Led)

- **Antiplatelet**: Aspirin 75–300 mg/day or clopidogrel 75 mg/day
- **Anticoagulation**: Heparin → warfarin or DOAC — for dissection with high embolic risk
- **Statins**: For atherosclerotic VBI; risk factor modification
- **Antihypertensives**: ACE inhibitors, ARBs — risk factor management
- **Analgesics**: Paracetamol/NSAIDs for associated neck pain if cleared for anticoagulation; avoid NSAIDs on anticoagulation
- NICE NG128 (Stroke): comprehensive stroke/TIA management guidance

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Emergency (999/ED immediately):**
- Any suspected acute dissection with neurological deficit
- Thunderclap headache ± neck pain
- New brainstem or cerebellar signs
- Post-manipulation onset of neurological symptoms (↑ vigilance within 24 hours of cervical treatment)

**Urgent (same-day referral to neurovascular/stroke team):**
- New onset of 5 D's + 3 N's without resolution
- Horner's syndrome with neck pain
- Posterior circulation TIA

**Routine neurology/vascular:**
- Chronic VBI symptoms; atherosclerotic risk review
- Post-dissection follow-up imaging review
