# Cervicogenic Headache

<EvidenceBadge level="moderate" />

> **Clinical Summary:** Cervicogenic headache (CGH) is a secondary headache arising from structural pathology of the cervical spine, particularly the upper three cervical segments (C1–C3). It accounts for approximately **4–22% of all chronic headaches** and is frequently misdiagnosed as migraine or tension-type headache. Accurate diagnosis and physiotherapy-led management significantly reduce headache burden.

---

## Overview & Pathophysiology

### Mechanism

CGH arises from nociceptive input from the upper cervical spine structures (joints, muscles, ligaments, dura mater) that converges with trigeminal afferents within the **trigeminocervical nucleus (TCN)** in the brainstem.

The TCN extends caudally from the trigeminal nucleus caudalis to approximately C3–C4. Afferents from:
- **C1 (suboccipital / atlantoaxial)** — via the dorsal root of C1 (where present) and the spino-trigeminal tract
- **C2 (atlantoaxial, C2-3 zygapophyseal joints, C2 dorsal root ganglion)**
- **C3 (C2-3 and C3-4 zygapophyseal joints, third occipital nerve)**

...all converge with trigeminal afferents in the TCN. This anatomical convergence creates referred pain to the head and face from cervical structures — **central sensitisation** at the TCN level underpins the perceived headache.

**Primary pain generators** include:
- C1-2 and C2-3 zygapophyseal (facet) joints
- Atlantoaxial and atlanto-occipital joints
- Suboccipital muscles (rectus capitis posterior minor/major, obliquus capitis inferior)
- C2-3 intervertebral disc
- Upper cervical dura mater
- Third occipital nerve (branch of C3 dorsal ramus)

### Prevalence

- **Population prevalence:** 0.4–4% (general population); up to 17–20% in chronic headache clinic populations
- **Post-whiplash:** up to 53% of whiplash patients develop CGH (Bogduk & Govind, 2009)
- **Gender:** female predominance (F:M ≈ 4:1)
- **Age:** peak 30–44 years; increases with age
- **Third occipital headache** (C2-3 facet-mediated) accounts for ~27% of chronic post-whiplash headache (Lord et al, 1994)

### Typical Clinical Presentation

- Unilateral headache without side-shift (though bilateral variants exist)
- Starts in the neck/suboccipital region, radiates to frontotemporal region, orbit, or vertex
- Provoked or aggravated by neck movement or sustained neck posture
- Reduced cervical range of motion (particularly rotation)
- Ipsilateral shoulder/arm symptoms may coexist
- Moderate, non-pulsating quality; not typically severe
- Duration: hours to days (episodic) or continuous with fluctuation (chronic)

### Cervical Flexion-Rotation Test (CFRT) — Detail

**Procedure:**
1. Patient supine; examiner passively flexes cervix to full end-range (locks sub-C2 segments)
2. Examiner rotates the head passively left and right whilst maintaining full flexion
3. Normal: ≥44° rotation bilaterally; mean normal ~45°
4. **Positive:** <32° rotation to symptomatic side, OR ≥10° side-to-side asymmetry, AND reproduction of familiar headache symptoms

**Interpretation (Ogince et al, 2007):**
- Cut-off of <32°: Sn 91%, Sp 90%
- Highly specific when reproduction of headache concurrent with ROM restriction

**Clinical utility:** CFRT is the **single most useful clinical test** for CGH. Its high +LR (9.1) makes it a strong rule-in test. The combination of ROM restriction AND symptom reproduction increases diagnostic confidence.

### Upper Cervical Segmental Assessment

Passive intervertebral motion (PIVM) and passive accessory intervertebral motion (PAIVM) of C0-C3 assessed in prone or supine:
- Assess for **hypomobility, pain provocation, and reproduction of familiar headache**
- Unilateral posterior-anterior (UPA) pressure over C1-C2-C3 articular pillars
- Positive: reproduction of headache ± referred pain pattern

## Red Flags

<RedFlag severity="urgent">
The following features require **immediate medical assessment** and should not be attributed to cervicogenic headache without exclusion of sinister pathology.
</RedFlag>

| Red Flag | Possible Cause | Action |
|----------|----------------|--------|
| **Thunderclap headache** (severe, maximal within 60 seconds) | Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) | Emergency 999 / A&E immediately |
| **"Worst headache of my life"** | SAH, intracranial haemorrhage | Emergency referral |
| **Meningism** (neck stiffness, photophobia, phonophobia, fever, rash) | Meningitis, encephalitis | Emergency 999 |
| **Progressive focal neurological deficit** | Space-occupying lesion, intracranial bleed | Urgent neurology |
| **Papilloedema / visual changes** | Raised intracranial pressure | Same-day ophthalmology / neurology |
| **New onset headache >50 years** | Giant cell arteritis (GCA), malignancy | Urgent ESR/CRP; GP same day |
| **Temporal arteritis signs** (jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, ESR >50) | Giant Cell Arteritis | Urgent GP/rheumatology; do not delay steroids |
| **Postural headache** (worse standing, better lying) | Intracranial hypotension / CSF leak | Urgent neurology |
| **Headache worse with Valsalva, cough, exertion** | Arnold-Chiari, raised ICP | Urgent neuroimaging |
| **Immunocompromised / HIV / cancer history** | CNS malignancy, opportunistic infection | Urgent medical assessment |
| **New onset headache post-head injury** | Subdural haematoma | Urgent CT head |
| **Unsteady gait, diplopia, dysarthria with headache** | Vertebrobasilar insufficiency, posterior fossa pathology | Emergency |

### Vertebral Artery Insufficiency

Prior to upper cervical manual therapy:
- Conduct pre-manipulative screening (IFOMPT framework 2012)
- Document absence of: diplopia, dizziness/vertigo with neck movement, drop attacks, dysarthria, dysphagia, facial numbness
- **Sustained C1-2 rotation testing** is no longer recommended as a single screen — clinical reasoning, history, and risk stratification are paramount

## Clinical Frameworks

### 1. Cervicogenic Headache International Study Group (CHISG) Criteria — Sjaastad et al, 1998

Major criteria (one or more required):

**Group I — Symptoms/signs of neck involvement:**
- Ia: Precipitation of head pain by neck movement or sustained neck posture
- Ib: Precipitation by external pressure over the upper cervical or occipital region
- Ic: Unilateral head pain without side-shift
- Id: Ipsilateral neck, shoulder, and arm pain of non-radicular character

**Group II — Confirmatory evidence:**
- IIa: Diagnostic blockade of cervical structures or their nerve supply abolishes pain
  *(This is the definitive confirmatory criterion)*

**Group III — Other characteristics:**
- IIIa: Moderate-severe, non-excruciating, non-throbbing, non-lancinating pain
- IIIb: Headache starts in the neck
- IIIc: Episodes of varying duration, or fluctuating continuous pain

**Exclusion:** Migraine features (aura, severe throbbing, nausea/vomiting, photophobia) do not exclude CGH but should prompt differential diagnosis consideration.

---

### 2. ICHD-3 Criteria for Cervicogenic Headache (11.2.1)

*(International Classification of Headache Disorders, 3rd Edition, 2018)*

**A.** Any headache fulfilling criterion C  
**B.** Clinical, laboratory, and/or imaging evidence of a disorder or lesion within the cervical spine or soft tissues of the neck known to be able to cause headache  
**C.** Evidence of causation demonstrated by **at least two** of the following:
- 1. Headache has developed in temporal relation to onset of cervical disorder or lesion
- 2. Headache has significantly improved or remitted in parallel with improvement or resolution of cervical disorder
- 3. Cervical ROM is reduced, and headache is significantly worsened by provocative movements
- 4. Headache is abolished following diagnostic blockade of a cervical structure or nerve supply

**D.** Not better accounted for by another ICHD-3 diagnosis

---

### 3. Jull's Assessment Approach (Jull, 1997; Jull et al, 2007)

A structured physiotherapy assessment framework:

**Phase 1 — Subjective assessment:**
- Headache characteristics (frequency, intensity, duration, location, associated symptoms)
- Aggravating/easing factors — identify cervical mechanical contribution
- Headache diary (minimum 4-week baseline)
- Medication use and medication overuse screening
- Psychosocial screening (PHQ-4, pain catastrophising scale)

**Phase 2 — Physical examination:**
- Active and passive cervical ROM (CROM device preferred)
- CFRT (detailed above)
- Upper cervical segmental examination (C0-C3 PAIVM/PIVM)
- Cervicothoracic junction examination (C4-T4)
- Craniocervical flexion test (CCF test) — deep cervical flexor assessment
- Postural assessment — forward head posture, thoracic kyphosis
- Neurological screening

**Craniocervical Flexion Test (CCFT):**
- Assesses neuromuscular control of deep cervical flexors (longus colli, longus capitis)
- Using pressure biofeedback unit (Stabilizer™): baseline 20 mmHg; progressive target 22→30 mmHg
- CGH patients typically show: reduced activation at lower pressure levels, increased activity of superficial flexors (SCM, anterior scalene) as compensation
- Target: achieve and hold 22–26 mmHg without superficial muscle substitution (Jull et al, 2008)

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Cervical Flexion-Rotation Test as Primary Diagnostic Tool

The CFRT is the **most diagnostically accurate clinical test** for CGH currently available:

| Study | n | Reference Standard | Sn | Sp | +LR | -LR |
|-------|---|--------------------|----|----|----|-----|
| Ogince et al, 2007 | 37 CGH + 37 controls | Sjaastad CHISG criteria | 91% | 90% | 9.1 | 0.10 |
| Hall et al, 2010 | 31 CGH + 29 migraine + 31 controls | ICHD-2 | 85% | 83% | 5.0 | 0.18 |
| Hall et al, 2008 | 31 CGH + 31 controls | ICHD-2 | 86% | 87% | 6.6 | 0.16 |
| Arce-Leal et al, 2021 (SR) | Meta-analysis | Various criteria | 87% (pooled) | 85% (pooled) | 5.8 | 0.15 |

**Interpretation:**
- +LR of 9.1 = a positive CFRT makes CGH ~9× more likely
- -LR of 0.10 = a negative CFRT substantially reduces probability of CGH
- **Both ROM restriction AND symptom reproduction** should be present for a positive test

### Cervical Joint Blocks as Reference Standard

The definitive diagnosis of CGH is **anaesthetic blockade of cervical structures**:
- Third occipital nerve (TON) block — for C2-3 facet-mediated headache
- C1-2 and C2-3 medial branch blocks
- Diagnostic threshold: ≥50% (or ≥80% for controlled blocks per International Spine Intervention Society)
- Available via: Pain Management/Neurology at Belfast City Hospital or Royal Victoria Hospital, Belfast

Clinical physiotherapy diagnosis is made on the basis of **clinical criteria** (Sjaastad/ICHD-3) in conjunction with CFRT and upper cervical examination. Diagnostic blocks are reserved for diagnostic uncertainty or pre-intervention confirmation.

### Differentiation from Migraine and Tension-Type Headache

| Feature | Cervicogenic Headache | Migraine (without aura) | Tension-Type Headache |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------------|-----------------------|
| **Laterality** | Unilateral (consistent side) | Unilateral (variable) | Bilateral |
| **Quality** | Dull, pressing, non-pulsating | Pulsating/throbbing | Pressing, tightening |
| **Severity** | Moderate | Moderate–severe | Mild–moderate |
| **Location** | Starts posterior/occipital → frontotemporal | Frontotemporal ± orbital | Band-like, vertex |
| **Cervical trigger** | Yes — consistent and reproducible | Occasional (trigger) | Occasional |
| **CFRT** | Positive (<32°, symptom reproduction) | Normal (>44°) | Normal or minimally reduced |
| **Nausea/vomiting** | Rarely | Common | Rare |
| **Photophobia/phonophobia** | Mild, if present | Moderate–severe | Mild |
| **Aura** | No | Yes (in 1/3 of migraineurs) | No |
| **Response to NSAIDs** | Partial | Partial–good | Good |
| **Response to triptans** | Poor | Good | No |
| **Response to cervical manual therapy** | Good | No benefit | Partial benefit |
| **Duration** | Hours–days (variable) | 4–72 hours | 30 min–7 days |
| **Physical activity** | May worsen | Worsens | Not worsened |

<ClinicalNote>
**Overlap:** ~50% of CGH patients have concurrent features meeting migraine diagnostic criteria (Knackstedt et al, 2010). Comorbid migraine does not exclude CGH and may require concurrent management. Use CFRT and cervical examination to identify the cervical contribution.
</ClinicalNote>

## Differential Diagnosis

| Headache Type | Key Distinguishing Features | CFRT | Cervical Signs | First-Line Management |
|---------------|----------------------------|------|---------------|-----------------------|
| **Cervicogenic Headache** | Unilateral, consistent side; cervical trigger; restricted CFRT; suboccipital onset | **Positive** | **Present** | Cervical physio |
| **Migraine (without aura)** | Pulsating; nausea/vomiting; photophobia; 4–72h; worse with activity | Negative | Absent (trapezius tenderness common but non-specific) | Triptans, preventive agents |
| **Tension-Type Headache** | Bilateral; pressing/tightening; mild–moderate; not worsened by activity | Negative | Pericranial tenderness (not C0-C3 specific) | Analgesia, stress management |
| **Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)** | >15 headache days/month; analgesia use >10–15 days/month; worsens over time | Variable | Variable | Medication withdrawal |
| **Cluster Headache** | Strictly unilateral; periorbital/temporal; severe/excruciating; autonomic features (lacrimation, rhinorrhoea, ptosis); 15–180 min; male predominance | Negative | Absent | 100% O₂, sumatriptan SC, verapamil prophylaxis |
| **Occipital Neuralgia** | Paroxysmal shooting/electric-shock pain in distribution of GON/LON; scalp allodynia; positive Tinel's over occipital nerve; responds to nerve block | May be reduced | GON tender, not facet | Nerve block, gabapentin |
| **Giant Cell Arteritis** | >50 years; temporal artery tenderness; jaw claudication; ESR >50; visual symptoms | Negative | Absent | Urgent steroids (prednisolone 40–60mg) |
| **TMJ Dysfunction** | Pain at TMJ; jaw movement provocation; ear pain; clicking | Negative | Absent | Dental/maxillofacial |
| **Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension** | Obese female; pulsatile tinnitus; visual obscurations; papilloedema; worse lying | Negative | Absent | Urgent neurology |

### Conservative Management

#### Evidence Base

**Jull et al (2002) — Landmark RCT** *(Spine, 27(17): 1835–1843)*
- n=200; 4 groups: manual therapy (MT) alone, specific exercise alone, combined MT+exercise, control
- **Key finding:** Combined MT + specific cervical exercise produced significantly better outcomes than either alone or control
- 10-week intervention; outcomes at 12 months
- Combined group: **76% had ≥50% reduction in headache frequency** at 12 months
- Manual therapy: targeted upper cervical manipulation and mobilisation
- Exercise: specific craniocervical flexion training (deep cervical flexors)

#### C1-2 SNAG (Sustained Natural Apophyseal Glide) — Mulligan Concept

**Technique:**
- Patient seated; therapist applies sustained posteroanterior glide to C1-2 on the symptomatic side using thumb/pisiform contact on the C2 spinous process / articular pillar
- Patient performs active cervical rotation in direction of restriction whilst glide is sustained
- If pain-free rotation improves → positive response, proceed with treatment
- 3 sets × 10 repetitions; can be self-applied with towel (home SNAG)

**Evidence:**
- Hall et al (2007, 2008): C1-2 SNAG significantly reduces headache frequency and intensity in CGH
- Paungmali et al (2003): SNAG produces hypoalgesic effect — pressure pain threshold increases
- Significant improvement in CFRT range of motion following C1-2 SNAG (Hall et al, 2007)

#### Upper Cervical Mobilisation

- Passive accessory intervertebral movements (PAIVMs) at C0-C1, C1-C2, C2-C3
- Grades III–IV (Maitland grading) to restricted, painful segments
- May include: atlanto-occipital decompression, C1-2 rotation mobilisation in flexion
- **Dose:** 3–5 sets of 30–60 second sustained holds or oscillatory grades; reassess after each set
- Evidence: Jull et al (1997, 2002); Zito et al (2006) — upper cervical manual examination and mobilisation reliably identifies and treats CGH

#### Specific Cervical Flexor Retraining

**Deep Cervical Flexor (DCF) Training Protocol** (Jull et al, 2008):

Stage 1 — Activation (Weeks 1–2):
- Craniocervical flexion (nodding) in supine
- Pressure biofeedback: target 22 mmHg; 10 × 10-second holds
- Avoid SCM/anterior scalene substitution

Stage 2 — Endurance (Weeks 3–4):
- Progress biofeedback target: 24–26 mmHg
- 10 × 10-second holds at each level; 3 levels progressed sequentially

Stage 3 — Functional Integration (Weeks 5–10):
- DCF activation during functional tasks
- Sitting posture training
- Progress to 28–30 mmHg as tolerated

Stage 4 — Load/Strength (Weeks 8–12):
- Resistance exercises: supine neck flexion with gravity resistance
- Integration with cervical rotation and lateral flexion

#### Postural Management

- Identify and correct **forward head posture (FHP)** — increases loading on upper cervical joints and suboccipital muscles
- Thoracic mobility: thoracic extension exercises, foam roller mobilisation (T4–T8)
- Workstation ergonomics assessment — screen height, chair position
- Sleep posture: single pillow maintaining neutral cervical alignment; cervical pillow if indicated
- Evidence: Ruivo et al (2017) — FHP correction reduces frequency of cervicogenic-type headache; correlation between FHP and CGH prevalence

#### Exercise Prescription Summary

| Component | Exercise | Sets × Reps | Frequency |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| DCF activation | CCF exercise (biofeedback) | 3 × 10 × 10s holds | Daily |
| Cervical mobility | CFRT self-mobilisation (SNAG in flexion) | 3 × 10 | Daily |
| Suboccipital stretching | Self-suboccipital release / chin tuck stretch | 3 × 30s | 2× daily |
| Thoracic extension | Foam roller extension T4-T8 | 3 × 60s | Daily |
| Scapular stabilisation | Wall angels / low row | 3 × 15 | 3×/week |
| Postural re-education | Chin tuck + retraction in sitting | Hourly (work breaks) | Daily |

### Pharmacological Management

<ClinicalNote>
Physiotherapy is the **first-line recommended treatment** for CGH. Pharmacological management is adjunctive and should be coordinated with the patient's GP.
</ClinicalNote>

#### Analgesia (NICE CG150 — Headaches in over 12s, 2012/Updated 2021)

- **Acute analgesia:** Aspirin 900mg or Ibuprofen 400mg (with food) — first-line for acute CGH episodes
- **Paracetamol:** 1g — alternative but less evidence in CGH specifically
- **Triptans:** Not recommended for CGH (mechanism differs from migraine; poor response rate)
- **Opioids:** Avoid in primary headache disorders; risk of medication overuse headache

#### Nerve Blocks

- **Greater occipital nerve (GON) block:** Methylprednisolone 40–80mg + lidocaine 1%; injection at occipital protuberance (medial 1/3 of line from mastoid to protuberance)
- Evidence: Inan et al (2001) — GON block effective in CGH; duration of relief 4–8 weeks
- **Third occipital nerve (TON) block / C2-3 medial branch block:** Diagnostic and therapeutic; available at Belfast City Hospital Pain Clinic
- **Referral for nerve block:** Via GP or physio urgent recommendation if conservative management fails at 6–8 weeks

#### Radiofrequency Neurotomy

- Third occipital nerve RFN (pulsed or conventional)
- **Indication:** Confirmed C2-3 facet-mediated CGH (positive diagnostic block); failed conservative management
- Evidence: Lord et al (1996) — conventional RFN of TON: median pain-free period 297 days vs 8 days sham
- Available via: Belfast City Hospital Pain Management / Neurosurgery

#### Botulinum Toxin (Botox)

- Limited evidence specifically for CGH (stronger evidence base in chronic migraine)
- May be considered for refractory CGH via neurology/pain clinic
- NICE TA260 (2012): Botox approved for chronic migraine (≥15 headache days/month); not specifically for CGH
- Use in CGH remains off-label / specialist decision

#### Preventive Pharmacology (for refractory/chronic CGH, via neurology)

- **Amitriptyline** 10–25mg nocte (low-dose; not NICE approved for CGH specifically; pragmatic use)
- **Gabapentin** — particularly if occipital neuralgia component
- **NSAIDs** (regular, with PPI cover) — short-term preventive role
- All preventive medications: initiate via GP or neurology; monitor and review at 3–6 months

### Onward Referral Criteria

<ReferralBox type="emergency">
**Emergency referral (999 / A&E) if:**
- Thunderclap headache / worst-ever headache
- Signs of meningism (neck stiffness + fever + photophobia)
- Focal neurological deficit with headache
- Headache + altered consciousness
</ReferralBox>

<ReferralBox type="urgent">
**Urgent GP same-day referral if:**
- New headache in patient >50 years
- Suspected giant cell arteritis (jaw claudication, temporal tenderness, visual symptoms)
- Postural headache (positional; worse upright)
- Headache consistently worse with Valsalva/cough/straining
- Accelerating headache pattern over weeks
</ReferralBox>

<ReferralBox type="routine">
**Routine neurology referral (via GP) if:**
- Failure to progress after 8–12 weeks of appropriate physiotherapy
- Diagnostic uncertainty — atypical features
- Consideration of botulinum toxin or preventive pharmacology
- Sleep disturbance, significant psychological comorbidity requiring multidisciplinary input
- Patient request or GP clinical judgement
</ReferralBox>

<ReferralBox type="pain-clinic">
**Pain Clinic / Interventional Referral (via GP/neurology) if:**
- Diagnostic cervical medial branch blocks required (C2-3, TON)
- GON block indicated (failed conservative management ≥3 months)
- Radiofrequency neurotomy consideration (confirmed C2-3 CGH)
- Spinal cord stimulation (intractable, multidisciplinary team decision)
</ReferralBox>

**Physiotherapy discharge / step-up criteria:**
- Step-up if: no ≥30% improvement in headache frequency after 4–6 sessions
- Discharge if: headache frequency reduced ≥50%, patient self-managing, goals achieved
- Provide written home programme and self-management plan at discharge
