## Overview & Pathophysiology

Cervical radiculopathy (CR) is a clinical syndrome characterised by pain, neurological deficits, or both, caused by compression or irritation of a cervical nerve root. Annual incidence is approximately 83 per 100,000 population, with peak onset between 50–54 years of age (Radhakrishnan et al., 1994).

**Aetiology:**
- **Disc herniation** (~20–25%): acute onset, more common in younger patients (<45 years); posterolateral herniation impinges the exiting nerve root
- **Spondylosis/osteophytes** (~70–75%): degenerative foraminal stenosis, more common in older patients
- **Combined disc + spondylosis:** most frequent overall

**Most affected levels:**
- C6 (60%): affects C5–6 disc, compression of C6 root
- C7 (25–30%): C6–7 disc, C7 root
- C5, C8: less common

**Relevant anatomy:**
- Cervical nerve roots exit above their corresponding vertebra (C1 exits above C1, C8 exits below C7)
- Intervertebral foramina bounded anteriorly by the uncovertebral joint and disc, posteriorly by the facet joint — both sites of spondylotic change
- Dorsal root ganglion lies within or just outside the foramen — highly sensitive to mechanical and chemical irritation
- Nucleus pulposus chemical mediators (phospholipase A2, TNF-α, IL-6) contribute to radicular pain independent of mechanical compression

**Dermatomal/myotomal distribution:**

| Root | Dermatomal Pain | Weakness | Reflex |
|------|-----------------|----------|--------|
| C4 | Neck, upper trapezius | Shoulder shrug | — |
| C5 | Lateral arm to elbow | Deltoid, biceps | Biceps (C5/6) |
| C6 | Lateral forearm, thumb, index finger | Wrist extensors, biceps | Brachioradialis, biceps |
| C7 | Middle finger, dorsal forearm | Triceps, wrist flexors | Triceps |
| C8 | Medial forearm, ring/little finger | Finger flexors, intrinsics | — |

**Natural history:** Favourable in most cases. Majority (~75–90%) improve with conservative management within 4–6 weeks. Surgical outcomes equivalent to conservative care at 1–2 years for mild-moderate cases (Persson et al., 1997).

| Dermatomal sensory deficit | 0.67 | 0.75 | 2.7 | 0.44 | Moderate |

**Wainner et al. (2003) Clinical Prediction Rule for CR** (4-variable cluster):
Positive if ≥3 of 4:
1. Ipsilateral cervical rotation <60°
2. ULNT1 positive
3. Distraction test positive
4. Spurling's test positive

- Sensitivity: 24%, Specificity: 99%, +LR: 30.3 (highly specific cluster for ruling in)
- 3/4 positive: +LR = 6.1; 4/4 positive: +LR = 30.3

**Administration notes:**
- **Spurling's**: cervical lateral flexion + axial compression toward symptomatic side — positive if reproduces or peripheralises arm symptoms
- **SARS**: patient places ipsilateral hand on head — positive if arm/shoulder pain reduces
- **Neck Distraction**: examiner provides manual cervical traction (~10–15 kg) — positive if symptoms centralise or reduce
- **ULNT1**: shoulder depression, abduction 110°, forearm supination, wrist/finger extension, elbow extension, contralateral cervical lateral flexion — positive if reproduces concordant arm pain

## Red Flags

Screen for serious spinal pathology at all initial assessments:

**Myelopathy signs (urgent referral):**
- Upper motor neuron signs: spasticity, hyperreflexia, Hoffman's sign, Babinski
- Bilateral or multisegmental neurological deficits
- Gait disturbance, broad-based gait, falls
- Bladder/bowel dysfunction
- Progressive neurological deterioration

**Cervical Spine Fracture (Canadian C-Spine Rule — high-risk features):**
- Age ≥65
- Dangerous mechanism (fall from >1 m, axial load to head, high-speed MVC, bicycle/motorised recreational vehicle collision)
- Paraesthesia in extremities
- If high-risk feature present → X-ray/CT indicated; do NOT mobilise

**Malignancy:**
- History of malignancy, unexplained weight loss, night pain, age >50 with no prior neck history
- Constant pain unrelated to posture or movement

**Infection:**
- Fever, immunosuppression, recent spinal procedure, IV drug use, neck rigidity
- Spinal epidural abscess — urgent MRI

**Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency / Cervical Artery Dysfunction:**
- 5 D's + 3 N's: Dizziness, Diplopia, Dysarthria, Dysphagia, Drop attacks; Nausea, Numbness, Nystagmus
- Screen before cervical manipulation — see cervical-artery-dysfunction.mdx

**Fracture indicators:**
- Severe trauma, inability to rotate 45° bilaterally, midline cervical tenderness
- Apply Canadian C-Spine Rule; if positive, refer to ED

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Concept
- Grade I–II oscillations for pain modulation in acute/irritable presentations
- Grade III–IV for resistance-dominant presentations in subacute/chronic
- Passive accessory intervertebral movements (PAIVMs) at C5/6, C6/7 to identify comparable signs
- Passive physiological intervertebral movements (PPIVMs) to assess segmental mobility
- Reassess neurological symptoms after each treatment — peripheralisation = contraindication to further technique

### McKenzie / MDT (Mechanical Diagnosis and Therapy)
- Repeated movement assessment: flexion/extension/lateral flexion/rotation in standing and supine
- Identify directional preference: centralisation of radicular symptoms with repeated extension suggests disc pathology with good prognosis
- Classification: Derangement (most common, 80–90%), Dysfunction, Postural
- Derangement: centralisation phenomenon guides treatment direction
- MDT retraction ± extension exercises as first-line for Derangement syndrome
- Centralisation as prognostic indicator: associated with better outcomes (Werneke & Hart, 2003)

### Clinical Prediction Rules
- Wainner CPR (2003): see Special Tests above — for diagnosis
- Fritz & Brennan (2007) CPR for manipulation benefit: ≥3 of 5 predictors associated with success
  1. Symptom duration <38 days
  2. Positive expectation of manipulation
  3. Side-to-side difference in cervical rotation >10°
  4. Pain with PA spring testing at C3–5
  5. Not performing sedentary work

## Outcome Measures

### Neck Disability Index (NDI)
- 10 items, 0–5 scale each, total 0–50 (or 0–100%)
- Scoring: 0–8% = no disability; 10–28% = mild; 30–48% = moderate; 50–64% = severe; ≥64% = complete
- **MCID**: 7.5/50 points (15%) for CR (Young et al., 2009)
- Reliability: ICC 0.89–0.93; Strong validity for neck pain populations

### Numeric Pain Rating Scale (NPRS)
- 0–10 NRS; **MCID**: 2.1 points for cervical radiculopathy (Cleland et al., 2007)
- Use for arm pain AND neck pain separately

### Patient-Specific Functional Scale (PSFS)
- 3–5 patient-identified activities, scored 0–10 each (0=unable, 10=normal)
- **MCID**: 2.0 points (per activity average) for neck pain (Cleland et al., 2006)
- Responsive to clinically important change in CR

### Global Rating of Change (GROC)
- 15-point scale (−7 to +7) or 7-point Likert
- +5 ("quite a bit better") or greater = successful outcome
- Use at 4 weeks and discharge

### Arm Pain NRS
- Separate from neck NRS — important to track radicular symptom change
- Reduction in arm pain more clinically significant than neck pain in CR

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**Diagnostic gold standard:** Electrodiagnostic studies (EMG/NCS) and MRI

**MRI:**
- Preferred imaging for suspected CR
- NICE (NG59, 2016): offer MRI if surgical opinion being sought or if red flags present
- Do NOT routinely offer imaging in first 6 weeks of uncomplicated CR
- MRI has high false positive rate — degenerative changes common in asymptomatic adults (>50% over age 40 have disc bulges)

**Electrodiagnostics:**
- EMG/NCS: sensitivity ~50–70%, specificity ~95% for radiculopathy
- Most useful for confirming root level and ruling out peripheral entrapment (carpal tunnel, cubital tunnel)
- Findings may lag clinical presentation by 3–4 weeks

**Clinical Prediction Rule (Wainner 2003):**
- 4/4 positive tests: +LR 30.3, probability of CR rises from 23% to ~90%
- Most useful as a confirmatory cluster

**IASP Diagnostic Criteria for CR:**
- Unilateral arm pain in a dermatomal distribution
- Corresponding sensory changes (hypoaesthesia/paraesthesia)
- Motor deficit or reflex change at corresponding level
- Positive provocative tests (Spurling's, distraction)
- Imaging correlation (but not required for clinical diagnosis)

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| Rotator cuff pathology | Local shoulder pain, painful arc, negative Spurling's, positive Hawkins/Neer |
| Thoracic outlet syndrome | Provoked by arm elevation, positive Roos test, neurogenic/vascular |
| Carpal tunnel syndrome | Nocturnal wrist/hand pain, thenar wasting, positive Phalen/Tinel at wrist |
| Cubital tunnel syndrome | Medial elbow + ring/little finger symptoms, positive elbow flexion test |
| Brachial neuritis (Parsonage-Turner) | Acute severe shoulder pain followed by rapid weakness, no spinal correlation |
| Peripheral neuropathy | Bilateral, glove distribution, diabetes history |
| Cervical myelopathy | Bilateral symptoms, UMN signs, gait disturbance, bladder/bowel dysfunction |
| Shoulder impingement | Shoulder-localised pain, arc sign positive, negative neurodynamic tests |
| Complex regional pain syndrome | Autonomic changes, disproportionate pain, allodynia |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care / Community MSK:**
- First point of contact via GP referral or direct-access physiotherapy (varies by Trust — Belfast, Northern, South Eastern, Southern, Western)
- Initial assessment within NICE recommended timeframes (target: 18 weeks RTT)
- **Triage:** Urgent same-day/next-day onward referral if red flags present
- Community MSK physiotherapy for uncomplicated CR with conservative management
- If no meaningful improvement at 6–12 weeks → discuss onward referral

**Secondary Care Pathway:**
- Refer to Orthopaedic Spine or Neurosurgery via GP if:
  - Progressive neurological deficit
  - Failure of 6–12 weeks conservative management with significant disability
  - Red flags present
- Pain Clinic referral for chronic refractory radiculopathy (consider epidural steroid injection, nerve root block)
- **NICE NG59 (2016):** Assessment and management of neck pain aligned with this guidance

**Waiting List Context:**
- HSC elective waits for orthopaedics significant in NI (>52 weeks common); use waiting time productively with ongoing conservative management and self-management education
- Belfast Trust MSK interface service may offer earlier triage and physiotherapy-led assessment

### Conservative Management

**Exercise Therapy (Grade A evidence):**
- Specific cervical and scapulothoracic strengthening reduces radicular symptoms
- Deep neck flexor strengthening (craniocervical flexion test biofeedback — CCFT protocol): Jull et al. (2009)
- Neural mobilisation (slider vs. tensioner techniques): Moderate evidence for symptom reduction
  - Median nerve slider: ULNT1-based; gentler, preferred in acute/irritable presentations
  - Radial nerve mobilisation for C6 root involvement
- McKenzie-based exercises: centralisation protocol (see Clinical Frameworks)
- Exercise alone equivalent to combined treatment at 12 months (Ylinen et al., 2003)

**Manual Therapy (Grade B evidence):**
- Cervical manipulation and mobilisation: Short-term pain relief; NCEPOD and NICE caution regarding serious adverse events — shared decision-making essential
- Thoracic spine manipulation: Level B evidence for short-term reduction of neck pain and disability in CR (Cleland et al., 2005)
- HVLA to thoracic spine considered lower risk than cervical HVLA
- Soft tissue techniques: Limited evidence as standalone; useful adjunct

**Education & Self-Management:**
- Pain neuroscience education (PNE): addresses catastrophisation and kinesiophobia
- Postural advice: ergonomic assessment (desk/screen setup), pillow selection
- Activity modification: avoid sustained cervical flexion, repetitive overhead loading
- Reassurance regarding favourable natural history

**Traction:**
- Mechanical/intermittent cervical traction: Moderate evidence for CR (Raney et al., 2009)
- CPR for traction (Raney et al.): ≥3 of 5 predictors:
  1. Age ≥55
  2. Positive shoulder abduction test
  3. Positive ULNT1
  4. Positive neck distraction test
  5. Peripheralisation with lower cervical PA spring testing

### Pharmacological (NICE-aligned)

- **Analgesia:** Paracetamol (limited evidence for neuropathic component) + NSAIDs (short-term, consider GI/CV risk)
- **Neuropathic pain:** Gabapentin (300–1200 mg TDS) or Pregabalin (75–300 mg BD) — NICE NG173 first-line for neuropathic pain; monitor for sedation/dependence
- **Tricyclics:** Amitriptyline (10–75 mg nocte) — off-label for neuropathic pain; NICE NG173 endorsed
- **Oral corticosteroids:** Short course (e.g., Medrol dose pack) may offer short-term relief; not routinely recommended
- **Muscle relaxants:** Diazepam — short-term only for acute muscle spasm; addictive potential
- **Injections:** Epidural steroid injection or transforaminal nerve root block — consider if conservative management failed at 6–12 weeks; refer to Pain Clinic/Spine service

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Urgent/Emergency (same-day):**
- Progressive motor deficit (new/worsening weakness)
- Signs of myelopathy (bilateral UMN signs, bladder/bowel dysfunction)
- Suspected fracture, infection, or malignancy

**Routine Surgical Referral (within 8–12 weeks):**
- Failure of 6–12 weeks conservative management with persistent severe radiculopathy
- Significant or worsening neurological deficit
- Severe radicular pain inadequately controlled

**Pain Clinic:**
- Chronic refractory radiculopathy (>3 months)
- Consideration for nerve root blocks, epidural steroid injection, or spinal cord stimulation
