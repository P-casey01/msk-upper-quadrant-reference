## Overview & Pathophysiology

Mechanical neck pain (MNP) is the most prevalent cervical spine condition, with a point prevalence of ~14–15% globally and lifetime prevalence up to 67% (Fejer et al., 2006). It is the fourth leading cause of disability worldwide (Hoy et al., 2014). Annual incidence in UK primary care is approximately 15% of adult consultations involving neck-related complaints.

**Definition:** Neck pain of musculoskeletal origin without neurological involvement, arising from cervical structures including intervertebral discs, facet joints, muscles, ligaments, and associated fasciae. Reproduced or modified by cervical movement and mechanical loading.

**Pathological sources:**
- **Facet joints (zygapophyseal joints):** Most common identifiable pain source (~54% in chronic neck pain by Bogduk & McGuirk, 2006); intra-articular meniscoid inclusions, capsular strain, synovitis
- **Intervertebral disc (discogenic pain):** Annular tears, internal disc disruption; C5/6 and C6/7 most common
- **Muscles and myofascial tissue:** Trapezius, levator scapulae, splenius capitis, suboccipitals — trigger points, postural overload
- **Ligaments:** Posterior longitudinal, interspinous ligaments with repetitive strain
- **Atlantoaxial and atlanto-occipital joints:** Particularly relevant in upper cervical pain

**Classification (Guzman et al., 2008 — Bone and Joint Decade 2000–2010 Task Force):**
- **Grade I:** No signs of major pathology, does not interfere with daily activities
- **Grade II:** No signs of major pathology, does interfere with daily activities
- **Grade III:** Neck pain with neurological signs (radiculopathy)
- **Grade IV:** Neck pain with signs of major structural pathology

MNP broadly covers Grades I–II.

**Risk factors:**
- Sustained postures (sedentary work, screen use >6 hours/day)
- Psychosocial: high job demands, low control, poor support, depression, anxiety
- Previous neck pain episode (strongest predictor of recurrence)
- Female sex (RR ~1.4)
- Age 45–54 years peak
- Sleep disturbance

**Prognosis:** Most acute MNP (>50%) resolves within 6 weeks; however, ~40–50% develop persistent or recurrent symptoms at 1 year (Haldeman et al., 2008).

## Special Tests

Mechanical neck pain is primarily a **diagnosis of exclusion** — the primary role of clinical assessment is to rule out serious pathology and neurological involvement, then classify the mechanical presentation.

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | −LR | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| Spurling's (negative — rules in MNP over radiculopathy) | 0.50–0.60 | 0.86–0.97 | — | — | High (ruling out radiculopathy) |
| Passive cervical rotation <32° (chronic pain predictor) | 0.77 | 0.69 | 2.5 | 0.33 | Moderate |
| PAIVMs (Maitland): segmental tenderness | ~0.70 | ~0.60 | — | — | Low–Moderate |
| Cervical Flexion-Rotation Test (C1/2 contribution to pain) | 0.91 | 0.90 | 9.1 | 0.10 | High (Hall et al., 2010) |
| Facet joint loading test (quadrant position) | 0.47 | 0.74 | 1.8 | 0.72 | Low |
| Active cervical ROM limitation (any plane) | 0.57 | 0.53 | 1.2 | 0.81 | Low |
| Palpation of upper cervical joints (C0–2) | 0.82 | 0.79 | 3.9 | 0.23 | Moderate (Jull et al., 1988) |
| Craniocervical flexion test (CCFT — deep neck flexor activation) | — | — | — | — | Construct validity |

**Clinical Prediction Rule for Cervical Manipulation (Childs et al., 2004):**
≥3 of 5 predictors → +LR 13.5 for success with HVLA manipulation:
1. Symptom duration <38 days
2. Positive expectation that manipulation will help
3. Side-to-side cervical rotation difference >10°
4. Pain with spring test at C3–5
5. Not performing sedentary work (>50% of day)

**Key assessments:**
- **Active ROM** (goniometry or CROM device): flexion, extension, lateral flexion, rotation — establish baseline and monitor
- **PPIVMs**: C2–7 intersegmental mobility
- **PAIVMs**: posteroanterior pressure C1–7 — identify comparable sign (pain provocation at symptomatic segment)
- **Deep neck flexor endurance**: CCFT or cranio-cervical flexion endurance test — assess neuromuscular control
- **Postural assessment**: forward head posture, thoracic kyphosis, scapular position

## Red Flags

Screening is essential at every new presentation and reassessment:

**NICE CKS Red Flags for Neck Pain:**
- Significant trauma (consider fracture — Canadian C-Spine Rule)
- Malignancy: unexplained weight loss, previous cancer, thoracic pain, rest pain
- Infection: fever, immunosuppression, recent spinal procedure
- Inflammatory arthritis: morning stiffness >1 hour, bilateral joint involvement, systemic features
- Myelopathy signs: bilateral neurological symptoms, gait disturbance, bladder/bowel dysfunction
- Cauda equina equivalent (very rare at cervical): bilateral upper limb weakness + bladder/bowel
- Vertebrobasilar/cervical artery dysfunction: 5 D's + 3 N's (see cervical-artery-dysfunction.mdx)
- Persistent severe headache with neck stiffness + photophobia → consider meningism

**Canadian C-Spine Rule (Stiell et al., 2001):**
No imaging required if ALL: age <65, no dangerous mechanism, able to rotate neck 45° each way; **and** ≥1 low-risk factor (simple rear-end MVC, sitting in ED, ambulatory, delayed neck pain, no midline tenderness)

**STarT MSK screening tool (yellow flags):**
- Identify psychosocial prognostic factors: fear-avoidance, catastrophising, depression, low self-efficacy
- Use Keele STarT MSK or Örebro MSK Questionnaire (Short Form)
- High score (≥4 on STarT MSK) → stratified care with targeted psychosocial management

## Clinical Frameworks

### Maitland Concept
- Identify **comparable sign** (the movement or palpation finding that most reproduces the patient's pain)
- **Symptom/resistance dominance**:
  - Pain-dominant (acute, irritable): Grade I–II oscillatory techniques for pain neurophysiology modulation
  - Resistance-dominant (subacute/chronic, stiff): Grade III–IV to increase mobility
- **Regular reassessment** ("asterisk sign"): track change in ROM, pain, function after each technique
- PAIVMs: C1–7 central PAs, unilateral PAs, transverse pressures
- PPIVMs: rotation and lateral flexion intersegmental mobility assessment
- Progression: add cervical rotation, extension, lateral flexion

### McKenzie / MDT
- Classify as **Derangement** (most common), **Dysfunction**, **Postural Syndrome**, or **Other**
- Repeated movement assessment (10 repetitions each direction)
- **Postural Syndrome**: pain only at end range/sustained posture; resolves with posture correction; no structural change
- **Dysfunction Syndrome**: pain at end range due to contracted/adherent tissue; intermittent pain at end range only; treatment: repeated stretching
- **Derangement**: most common; centralisation/peripheralisation determines direction; treat with directional preference exercises
- **Retraction** exercises (cervical retractions) as first-line for most cervical derangements

### Norwegian (Kaltenborn-Evjenth) Approach
- Segment-specific mobilisation based on concave-convex rules
- Traction grades I–III; glides in plane of facet joint

### Biopsychosocial / Stratified Care
- **STarT MSK (3-tier):**
  - Low risk: brief physiotherapy (2–3 sessions), self-management booklet
  - Medium risk: standard physiotherapy (6–8 sessions)
  - High risk: physiotherapy + targeted psychosocial intervention, consider psychological referral
- **Pain neuroscience education (PNE)**: Moseley & Butler framework — reduces catastrophisation and fear-avoidance

## Outcome Measures

### Neck Disability Index (NDI)
- Gold standard for neck pain disability
- 10 items × 0–5 = 0–50 total (reported as percentage: score/50 × 100)
- **MCID**: 7.5 points (50-point scale) for neck pain; ~5 points for chronic non-specific neck pain (Pool et al., 2007)
- MDC (95% CI): 10.5 points
- High reliability ICC 0.89–0.93

### Numeric Pain Rating Scale (NPRS)
- 0–10; **MCID**: 1.5–2.5 points for non-specific neck pain (Cleland et al., 2008)
- Measure at rest, with movement, worst in 24 hours

### Patient-Specific Functional Scale (PSFS)
- 0–10 per activity (3 patient-identified activities)
- **MCID**: 2.0 points average across activities (Cleland et al., 2006)
- High responsiveness and patient-centredness

### Global Rating of Change (GROC)
- 15-point scale; use at 4 weeks and discharge
- +5 or above = meaningful improvement

### Keele STarT MSK / Örebro MSK Questionnaire (Short Form)
- **Stratification tool**, not outcome measure per se
- Re-administer at 4–6 weeks to confirm psychosocial risk trajectory

### Fear-Avoidance Beliefs Questionnaire (FABQ)
- Work and Physical Activity subscales (0–66 and 0–24)
- High scores (>29 work subscale) predict poor return-to-work; inform targeted management

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**MNP is a clinical diagnosis** — imaging rarely changes management in uncomplicated cases.

**Imaging guidelines:**
- **NICE NG59 (2016):** Do not routinely offer imaging for non-specific neck pain; MRI only if surgical referral or red flags
- X-ray: low utility for soft tissue pathology; use if fracture suspected (post-trauma, osteoporosis, malignancy)
- CT: bony anatomy, foraminal stenosis, OPLL when MRI contraindicated
- Normal imaging does NOT exclude pain; degenerative changes are ubiquitous and poorly correlate with symptoms

**Key evidence:**
- Asymptomatic degenerative changes on MRI present in: 87% at age 70, 50% at age 40 (Boden et al., 1990)
- Imaging findings should be interpreted within clinical context — avoid iatrogenic nocebo
- Systematic review (Rubinstein & van Tulder, 2008): clinical examination sufficient for diagnosis and management in non-complex cases

**Yellow Flag Screening (STarT MSK):**
- Prognostic stratification improves outcomes vs. one-size-fits-all care (Hill et al., 2011 — LBP evidence extrapolated to neck pain; Beneciuk et al., 2013 for cervical)

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| Cervical radiculopathy | Radicular arm pain in dermatomal pattern, positive Spurling's, neurological deficit |
| Cervicogenic headache | Headache referral from C1–3, positive FRT, unilateral, occipital to frontotemporal |
| Fibromyalgia | Widespread pain, multiple tender points, fatigue, sleep disturbance |
| Rheumatoid arthritis / inflammatory arthritis | Morning stiffness, bilateral, systemic features, elevated ESR/CRP, ANA positive |
| Ankylosing spondylitis | Young male, sacroiliac involvement, HLA-B27, bamboo spine on X-ray |
| Thoracic outlet syndrome | Positional vascular/neurogenic symptoms with arm elevation |
| Temporomandibular joint dysfunction | Jaw pain, crepitus, bruxism, ear pain, limited mouth opening |
| Occipital neuralgia | Sharp, stabbing occipital pain, positive Tinel over GON, distinct from cervicogenic headache |
| Whiplash associated disorder | Mechanism of injury (acceleration-deceleration), specific classification (WAD grade) |
| Muscle tension headache | Bilateral, pressure/band-like, no photophobia, not worsened by movement |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care:**
- GP can prescribe short-term analgesia and refer to community physiotherapy
- Direct access physiotherapy available in some NI Trusts (Belfast, Northern)
- Self-referral to community MSK services varies by Trust
- First-contact physiotherapy (FCP) being rolled out in GP surgeries — appropriate for MNP triage

**Community MSK Physiotherapy:**
- Primary treatment setting for Grade I–II MNP
- Stratified care based on STarT MSK risk:
  - Low: 2–3 sessions + self-management advice
  - Medium: 6–8 sessions with structured programme
  - High: 8–12 sessions + psychology/pain service liaison
- NICE NG59: Offer exercise therapy, manual therapy, and psychological support as package
- Consider group education/exercise (cost-effective for low-medium risk)

**Occupational Health / Work-Related Neck Pain:**
- Ergonomic assessment and workplace modification — coordinate with employer OH service
- Fit notes (Statement of Fitness for Work): physiotherapists can provide reports but not fit notes; liaise with GP

### Conservative Management

**Exercise Therapy (Grade A — highest evidence for neck pain):**
- **Deep neck flexor (DNF) strengthening**: Craniocervical flexion test (CCFT) protocol — progressive pressure biofeedback (20–26–30 mmHg); Jull et al. (2002, 2009)
  - Proven superior to general neck exercise for chronic neck pain
  - 8–10 week programme; 3–5 × per week
- **Cervical and scapulothoracic strengthening**: Trapezius, rhomboids, serratus anterior — 3 × 10–15 RM, progressive
- **Aerobic exercise**: Walking, swimming; reduces disability and pain catastrophising; NICE recommendation
- **Proprioceptive/sensorimotor training**: Joint position error retraining; laser pointer head exercises; supports neuromuscular control deficits in chronic neck pain (Revel et al., 1994)
- **Stretching**: Suboccipital, levator scapulae, scalenes; limited standalone evidence but patient-valued and safe

**Manual Therapy (Grade B–A):**
- Combined manipulation + exercise superior to either alone (Hoving et al., 2002; Hurwitz et al., 2002)
- **Cervical mobilisation**: Safe and effective; Maitland Grade III–IV PAIVMs/PPIVMs
- **Cervical HVLA manipulation**: Short-term benefit for acute/subacute neck pain; requires informed consent, CAD screening (IFOMPT 2012 framework); contraindicated in red flags/myelopathy
- **Thoracic manipulation**: Level A evidence adjunct for cervical pain (Cleland et al., 2005, 2007)
- **Soft tissue therapy**: Trigger point release, myofascial release — adjunct; limited standalone evidence

**Patient Education (Grade A):**
- PNE: Explain pain as non-threatening; reassure re: natural history; de-medicalize
- Postural advice: Ergonomic screen height, chair adjustment, monitor distance, pillow height (cervical neutral)
- Activity modification: Avoid sustained end-range postures >20–30 minutes without movement break
- Return to activity encouraged: "motion is lotion" messaging

**Dry Needling / Acupuncture:**
- Trigger point dry needling: Moderate evidence for short-term pain relief in myofascial neck pain (Tough et al., 2009)
- Acupuncture: NICE allows referral for chronic pain where appropriate (NICE NG193, 2021); 10 sessions maximum via NHS

### Pharmacological (NICE-aligned)

- **Paracetamol**: First-line; 1g QDS; limited evidence for musculoskeletal neck pain (NICE NG59)
- **NSAIDs**: Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS or Naproxen 500 mg BD with food; 2–4 weeks maximum; GI protection (omeprazole) if >45 years, smoker, or prior GI history (NICE NG193)
- **Topical NSAIDs**: Diclofenac gel — preferred in elderly for GI/CV risk reduction
- **Muscle relaxants**: Diazepam 2 mg TDS (acute muscle spasm only; 5–7 days maximum); addictive potential — use sparingly
- **Amitriptyline** (10–30 mg nocte): for sleep disturbance and chronic pain modulation; off-label
- **Avoid** long-term opioids for mechanical neck pain (NICE NG193 2021 — chronic primary pain: do not offer opioids)

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Urgent (same-day):**
- Red flags: suspected fracture, malignancy, infection, myelopathy
- New bladder/bowel dysfunction

**Routine referral:**
- Failure to improve after 6–12 weeks of optimal physiotherapy
- Suspected radiculopathy or myelopathy requiring imaging
- Diagnostic uncertainty requiring specialist assessment
- Persistent severe pain requiring Pain Clinic input
- Significant psychosocial barriers → Psychology/CBT referral

**Occupational:**
- Inability to return to work after 4–6 weeks → Occupational health referral
