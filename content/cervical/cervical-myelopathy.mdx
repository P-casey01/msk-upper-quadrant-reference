## Overview & Pathophysiology

Cervical myelopathy (CM) — more precisely **degenerative cervical myelopathy (DCM)** — is the most common cause of spinal cord dysfunction in adults over 50 years, with an estimated prevalence of 60 per 100,000 in North America/Europe (Nouri et al., 2015). It is the leading cause of acquired spinal cord disability in adults worldwide.

**Pathophysiology:**
DCM results from progressive narrowing of the cervical spinal canal leading to spinal cord compression and ischaemia:
- **Static compression**: osteophytes, hypertrophied ligamentum flavum (especially in extension), ossification of posterior longitudinal ligament (OPLL), degenerative disc bulges, facet joint hypertrophy
- **Dynamic compression**: cord compression exacerbated by flexion/extension movement; cord is stretched over ventral osteophytes in flexion and folded by buckled ligamentum flavum in extension
- **Vascular compromise**: compression of anterior spinal artery and intrinsic cord microvasculature → ischaemia → central grey matter necrosis
- **Congenital canal narrowing**: canal diameter <13 mm (critical stenosis <10 mm) → Torg-Pavlov ratio; predisposes to earlier/more severe myelopathy
- Spinal cord injury pattern: central cord syndrome most common pattern

**Canal dimensions:**
- Normal C3–7 canal diameter: 17–18 mm
- Relative stenosis: <13 mm
- Absolute stenosis: <10 mm
- Torg-Pavlov ratio (canal diameter / vertebral body AP diameter): <0.8 = stenosis

**Clinical presentation:**
- Insidious onset; often subclinical initially
- **Upper extremities**: clumsy hands, grip weakness, difficulty with fine motor tasks (buttons, writing, chopsticks — "myelopathy hand"), wasting of intrinsic hand muscles
- **Lower extremities**: spastic gait (wide-based, shuffling), lower limb weakness, falls
- **Bladder**: urgency, frequency, hesitancy, incomplete emptying
- **Sensory**: burning dysaesthesia in hands, loss of proprioception, L'hermitte's sign (electric shock down spine with neck flexion)
- Natural history: variable — stepwise deterioration most common (~36%), gradual decline (~20%), stable with episodes of deterioration (~40%)

| Multivariable examination cluster (3+ UMN signs) | 0.94 | 0.57 | 2.2 | 0.10 | High (Cook et al., 2009) |

**Cook et al. (2009) Cluster for DCM:**
- ≥2 of 5 signs: Hoffman's, Inverted supinator, Babinski, Age >45, gait deviation
- +LR = 30.9 (3 positive); sensitivity 99% (at threshold ≥1 sign)

**Key examination findings:**

- **Hoffman's Sign**: flick middle finger distal phalanx — positive if IP flexion of thumb/index; indicates UMN dysfunction
- **Finger Escape Sign**: patient holds fingers extended, adducted — ulnar 2–3 fingers spontaneously abduct/flex; positive in DCM
- **10-Second Step Test**: patient taps each foot alternately ≥20 times in 10 seconds — <18 taps considered abnormal (Bohlman 1977)
- **Grip and Release Test**: open and close fist as fast as possible for 10 seconds — normal ≥20 cycles; <20 suggests hand dysfunction (Yukawa et al., 2012)
- **Tandem Gait Test**: inability to tandem walk (heel-to-toe) 2–3 steps; sensitive for gait ataxia in early myelopathy
- **Modified Japanese Orthopaedic Association Scale (mJOA)**: functional severity scoring used to guide surgical timing

## Red Flags

**DCM IS a red flag condition** — all physiotherapists must recognise DCM as requiring urgent onward referral, not conservative physiotherapy as primary treatment.

**Urgent referral triggers:**
- **Acute neurological deterioration**: sudden increase in weakness, new bladder/bowel dysfunction, rapidly progressing myelopathy → Emergency/same-day referral to neurosurgery or spine surgery
- Inability to walk safely due to spasticity
- New or rapidly worsening hand dysfunction
- Bilateral upper limb weakness

**Do NOT:**
- Apply cervical HVLA manipulation to patients with suspected or confirmed CM
- Prescribe aggressive cervical mobilisation in the presence of UMN signs
- Delay referral pending conservative management trial

**Signs mandating emergency referral:**
- Acute cord compression (post-trauma with neurological deficit)
- Bilateral lower extremity spasticity with acute-onset bladder/bowel changes
- Vascular myelopathy presentation

**Additional red flags to screen:**
- Malignancy: unexplained weight loss, known primary tumour, night pain
- Infection: fever, immunosuppression, spinal tenderness
- OPLL: Japanese/East Asian descent, age >40, rigid cervical spine with myelopathy → CT required

## Clinical Frameworks

### Severity Classification (mJOA Scale)
The modified Japanese Orthopaedic Association (mJOA) scale is the most widely used tool for DCM severity:

| mJOA Score | Severity |
|------------|----------|
| 18 | Normal |
| 15–17 | Mild |
| 12–14 | Moderate |
| <12 | Severe |

**mJOA domains:** Upper extremity motor function (0–5), Lower extremity motor function (0–7), Sensory (0–3), Sphincter function (0–3)

### Maitland (with significant caveats)
- Maitland mobilisation is NOT appropriate as primary treatment for confirmed DCM
- Grade I–II soft tissue work to peripheral joints (shoulder, elbow) may be used if confirmed nerve root + cord involvement coexist — only under specialist physiotherapy with neurosurgical knowledge
- Post-surgical rehabilitation: Maitland principles applicable once surgical clearance obtained

### AO Spine DCM Grading (Research Framework)
- Grade 0: No signs/symptoms
- Grade 1: Signs only, no symptoms
- Grade 2: Symptoms but no functional limitation
- Grade 3: Mild functional limitation — independent
- Grade 4: Moderate functional limitation — requires some assistance
- Grade 5: Severe — completely dependent

### Physiotherapy Role Post-Operatively (ACDF/Laminoplasty/Laminectomy)
- 80% of patients improve post-decompression surgery (Fehlings et al., 2013)
- Post-op physiotherapy: gentle ROM, neurological recovery monitoring, functional gait retraining, ADL rehabilitation
- Inpatient neuro-physio → community follow-up pathway

## Outcome Measures

### Modified Japanese Orthopaedic Association Scale (mJOA)
- 18-point scale; **MCID**: 2.0 points (Tetreault et al., 2015)
- Most validated for surgical outcome measurement in DCM
- Recovery rate = [(post-op mJOA − pre-op mJOA) / (18 − pre-op mJOA)] × 100

### Neck Disability Index (NDI)
- **MCID**: 7.5/50 points (15%)
- Useful for tracking functional disability alongside neurological severity

### Nurick Grade
- 0–5 functional walking classification; less sensitive to small changes
- Grade 0: No root/cord signs; Grade 5: Chair-bound or bedridden

### European Myelopathy Score (EMS)
- 5-domain, 5–18 scale; validated European alternative to mJOA
- **MCID**: approximately 2 points

### Patient-Specific Functional Scale (PSFS)
- **MCID**: 2.0 points average
- Appropriate for post-surgical rehabilitation goal-setting

### GROC (Global Rating of Change)
- 15-point scale; use at post-op physiotherapy review points

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**MRI (gold standard):**
- T2 hyperintensity in spinal cord at level of compression = **poor prognostic marker** (indicates myelomalacia)
- T1 hypointensity = cavitation/necrosis — even worse prognosis
- STIR sequences for inflammatory/infective pathology
- NICE NG59 / AO Spine: MRI should be obtained urgently for suspected CM

**CT Myelography:**
- Used when MRI contraindicated or inconclusive
- Identifies bony compression, OPLL, calcified disc herniation with high resolution

**Electrodiagnostics:**
- Motor Evoked Potentials (MEPs) and Somatosensory Evoked Potentials (SSEPs): sensitivity ~85% for functional cord conduction abnormalities
- EMG/NCS to differentiate coexisting radiculopathy

**Clinical Diagnosis:**
- No single pathognomonic test; diagnosis requires combination of:
  1. Consistent symptom profile
  2. ≥2 UMN signs on examination
  3. MRI evidence of cord compression ± signal change
- Cook et al. (2009): multivariable cluster achieves sensitivity approaching 99% — high clinical utility

**AO Spine / AOSpine North America Clinical Practice Guidelines (Fehlings et al., 2017):**
- Recommend surgery for moderate-severe DCM (mJOA ≤14)
- For mild DCM (mJOA 15–17): structured rehabilitation trial with watchful waiting; surgery if deterioration

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| Multiple sclerosis | Age <40, relapsing-remitting course, MRI white matter lesions, oligoclonal bands |
| Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS/MND) | UMN + LMN mixed, bulbar involvement, no sensory deficit, EMG fasciculations |
| Subacute combined degeneration (B12 deficiency) | Posterior column signs, B12 low, macrocytic anaemia, no bony compression on MRI |
| Normal pressure hydrocephalus | Triad: gait apraxia, cognitive decline, urinary incontinence; no cervical compression |
| Syringomyelia | Cape distribution sensory loss, dissociated sensory deficit, often young adults |
| Peripheral neuropathy | No UMN signs, glove-stocking distribution, NCS abnormal |
| Cervical radiculopathy | Unilateral radicular symptoms, no UMN signs, no gait disturbance |
| Parkinson's disease | Tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, pill-rolling tremor; no cord compression |
| Hereditary spastic paraplegia | Family history, gradual onset, lower extremity predominant, genetic testing |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Confirmed or suspected DCM (moderate/severe):**
- Urgent referral to Neurosurgery or Orthopaedic Spinal Surgery via GP
- In NI: Regional Neurosurgery at Royal Victoria Hospital (Belfast HSC Trust); Orthopaedic Spine at Musgrave Park Hospital
- For suspected acute deterioration → Emergency Department (A&E) with cervical spine imaging
- Community physiotherapy: assess, recognise, refer — do NOT treat as primary condition

**Mild DCM with watchful waiting:**
- Structured physiotherapy programme with explicit safety boundaries:
  - No cervical HVLA manipulation
  - No cervical traction in confirmed cord compression
  - Functional rehabilitation, balance, UE fine motor retraining
  - Aquatic therapy: non-impact, offloads gravity
  - Serial neurological monitoring (3-monthly): grip strength, gait, bladder

**Post-surgical physiotherapy (Trust-specific protocols):**
- Inpatient physiotherapy from post-op day 1 (bed mobilisation, sitting balance)
- Discharge criteria: independent mobility, safe stairs
- Community physiotherapy 6–12 weeks post-surgery for functional rehabilitation
- Driving restriction post-ACDF: minimum 6 weeks; varies by surgeon

### Conservative Management

**Physiotherapy for mild DCM (watchful waiting) — Grade C evidence:**
- **Cervical collar**: Short-term rigid collar (4–8 weeks) for symptomatic relief — limited evidence; may cause deconditioning
- **Balance and proprioceptive retraining**: Standing balance, tandem stance, Wii Fit / perturbation training
- **Upper extremity fine motor retraining**: Occupational therapy-led ADL retraining
- **Strengthening**: Gentle scapular/shoulder stabilisation; avoid heavy loading through cervical spine
- **Gait retraining**: Physiotherapy-led gait retraining with walking aids if required; referral to orthotics if foot drop
- **Neurological monitoring**: Document serial mJOA, grip strength, 10-second step/grip-release test at each visit

**Post-surgical rehabilitation — Grade B evidence:**
- Most improvement in first 6 months post-decompression
- Targeted: grip/pinch strength, fine motor retraining, gait normalisation, return to ADLs
- Core stability and cervical muscle strengthening from 6–8 weeks post-fusion
- 80% of patients gain ≥2 mJOA points (Fehlings et al., 2013)

### Pharmacological

- **Neuropathic pain** (associated radiculopathy): Gabapentin/Pregabalin — NICE NG173
- **NSAIDs**: Short-term for pain; avoid long-term in elderly given GI/CV/renal risk
- **Corticosteroids**: High-dose methylprednisolone for acute traumatic SCI; NOT recommended for degenerative CM (Fehlings AANS 2013)
- **Baclofen/Tizanidine**: For spasticity management in confirmed CM; specialist-initiated
- **Bladder management**: Anticholinergics (oxybutynin) for urgency if confirmed; refer urology

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Emergency (same-day via A&E):**
- Acute deterioration in myelopathy symptoms
- New bladder/bowel dysfunction
- Post-trauma with neurological deficit

**Urgent neurosurgical referral (within 2 weeks):**
- Moderate/severe DCM (mJOA ≤14)
- Progressive neurological deterioration
- Bilateral hand dysfunction affecting ADLs

**Routine surgical review:**
- Mild DCM with symptom worsening despite structured 3-month conservative management
- Patient preference for surgical opinion
