# Elbow Osteoarthritis

## Overview

Elbow osteoarthritis (OA) is a degenerative joint disease characterised by progressive loss of articular cartilage, subchondral bone changes, osteophyte formation, and synovial inflammation within the elbow joint complex (humeroulnar, humeroradial, and proximal radioulnar joints). It is relatively uncommon compared to hip and knee OA, accounting for approximately **2% of all OA presentations**, but causes significant functional impairment given the elbow's role in positioning the hand in space.

### Epidemiology

- Prevalence: ~2% of the general population; higher in heavy manual workers and throwing athletes
- Peak onset: 5th–6th decade; male:female ratio approximately 4:1 in primary OA
- **Primary (idiopathic) OA** accounts for ~50% of elbow OA; often bilateral
- **Secondary OA** follows prior trauma (post-traumatic), inflammatory arthropathy, osteochondral defects, or repetitive occupational loading

### Pathoanatomy

Elbow OA characteristically produces:
- Osteophyte formation at the **coronoid fossa, olecranon fossa, and radial head**
- **Loose body formation** — a cardinal feature; fragments from osteophytes or osteochondral surfaces cause mechanical symptoms
- Progressive loss of terminal flexion and extension (terminal arc most affected first)
- Relative preservation of the mid-arc (60°–120°) until advanced disease
- Capsular contracture contributing to stiffness

### Typical Clinical Presentation

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Pain** | End-range aching; may be sharp with loose body impingement |
| **Stiffness** | Loss of terminal extension > terminal flexion initially |
| **Functional arc** | Typically 30°–130° preserved in mild–moderate disease |
| **Locking/catching** | Suggests loose body; episodic, position-dependent |
| **Crepitus** | Coarse, palpable; reproducible with passive ROM |
| **Swelling** | Effusion palpable in posterolateral triangle |
| **Occupation/sport history** | Heavy manual work, throwing athletes, combat sports |

> **Normal elbow ROM for reference:** Flexion 0°–145°; Pronation 75°; Supination 85°. Functional arc: 30°–130° flexion-extension; 50° pronation–50° supination (Morrey et al., 1981).

## Red Flags

Red flags require urgent or urgent-plus escalation — do not manage as routine OA.

| Red Flag | Possible Pathology | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Significant trauma + inability to extend | Radial head fracture; distal humerus fracture | Same-day imaging; ED/orthopaedics |
| Hot, swollen, erythematous joint + fever / systemically unwell | Septic arthritis | **Emergency** — same-day ED referral |
| Progressive neurological deficit (hand weakness, wasting) | Cubital tunnel syndrome; cervical myelopathy; Pancoast tumour | Urgent neurology / orthopaedics |
| Night pain unresponsive to analgesia; unexplained weight loss | Primary bone tumour; metastatic disease | Urgent 2-week-wait referral (suspected cancer pathway) |
| Bilateral symmetrical joint involvement + systemic features | Rheumatoid arthritis; psoriatic arthritis | Urgent rheumatology referral |
| Elbow pain + ipsilateral shoulder/neck pain + C-spine symptoms | Cervical radiculopathy (C6/C7); referred pain | Full cervical spine screen; consider nerve conduction |
| Haemarthrosis without adequate trauma | Haemophilia; pigmented villonodular synovitis (PVNS) | Haematology / rheumatology |
| Skin changes over olecranon (erythema, tracking) | Olecranon bursitis with cellulitis | Antibiotic review; may require aspiration |

## Special Tests

> Sensitivity (Sn) and specificity (Sp) data are derived from published validation studies. Most elbow OA-specific test accuracy data are limited; values reflect best available evidence and are presented with appropriate caution.

### Range of Motion Examination (Essential)

Active and passive ROM measurement with a standard goniometer is the cornerstone of elbow OA assessment. Loss of terminal extension is the earliest and most consistent finding.

### Provocative & Diagnostic Tests

| Test | Technique | Positive Finding | Sensitivity | Specificity | Clinical Use in Elbow OA |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Terminal arc pain** (Outerbridge–Kashiwagi sign) | Passive flexion–extension through full range; note where pain is provoked | Pain at end-range extension (<30°) and/or end-range flexion (>120°) | ~85% (expert consensus) | Moderate | Confirms terminal arc impingement — hallmark of elbow OA |
| **Loose body provocation test** | Examiner passively moves elbow through arc while applying axial compression | Reproducible locking, catching, or sharp pain | Not formally validated | — | Suggests intra-articular loose body; consider imaging |
| **Posterolateral rotatory instability (PLRI) test** (lateral pivot shift) | Supine; shoulder abducted 90°, forearm supinated; apply valgus + supination + axial compression through elbow flexion | Apprehension or subluxation of radial head | Sn 38%–100% (wide range; highly examiner-dependent) | Sp ~96% (O'Driscoll et al., 1991) | Rules in ligamentous instability contributing to OA pattern |
| **Valgus stress test** (UCL integrity) | Elbow at 15°–30° flexion; apply valgus force; compare medial joint opening | Pain ± gapping medially | Sn 65% (Safran & Ahmad, 2005) | Sp 60% | UCL insufficiency secondary to valgus overload; relevant in throwing athletes |
| **Milking manoeuvre** (UCL) | Traction on thumb with elbow >90° flexion and shoulder ER; valgus stress applied | Medial elbow pain | Sn 82% (O'Driscoll et al., 2005) | Sp 91% | UCL integrity; differentiates medial pain source |
| **Tinel's sign at cubital tunnel** | Percussion over ulnar nerve in cubital tunnel (medial epicondyle groove) | Paraesthesia in ring/little finger distribution | Sn 54–70% | Sp 77–98% (Novak et al., 1994) | Detects cubital tunnel syndrome — common OA co-pathology |
| **Elbow flexion test** | Maximal elbow flexion + wrist extension sustained 60 seconds | Paraesthesia ulnar nerve distribution | Sn 75% | Sp 99% (Novak et al., 1994) | Sensitive screen for cubital tunnel syndrome |
| **Radiocapitellar compression test** | Elbow slightly flexed; passive forearm rotation (pronation/supination) with axial compression | Lateral elbow pain | Not formally validated for OA | — | Screens radiocapitellar joint involvement; also used in radial head pathology |
| **Extension impingement test** | Rapid passive extension of elbow | Posterior elbow pain at end-range | Not formally validated | — | Posterior osteophyte / olecranon impingement |
| **Crepitus assessment** | Palpation during passive ROM | Coarse crepitus throughout arc or at terminal range | Moderate examiner agreement | — | Consistent feature of cartilage degeneration |

> **Note:** Elbow OA diagnosis is primarily **clinical + radiological**. No single provocative test is pathognomonic. Imaging (plain radiograph) is required for confirmation and surgical planning.

## Clinical Frameworks

### Outerbridge–Kashiwagi (OK) Classification (Radiological/Intraoperative)

Widely used for surgical planning but informs clinical staging:

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| I | Osteophytes at coronoid/olecranon tip; joint space preserved |
| II | Moderate osteophytes; mild joint space narrowing; loose bodies may be present |
| III | Severe osteophytes; significant joint space narrowing; multiple loose bodies |
| IV | End-stage: complete joint space loss; gross deformity |

### Broberg & Morrey Functional Rating System

Used in outcome assessment for elbow OA interventions:
- **Score 0–100** based on pain (45 pts), motion (25 pts), stability (10 pts), function (20 pts)
- Excellent: 96–100 | Good: 80–95 | Fair: 60–79 | Poor: <60

### Gschwend Classification (Clinical Staging)

| Stage | Clinical Features |
|---|---|
| 1 | Mild stiffness; normal or near-normal plain film |
| 2 | Moderate stiffness + pain; osteophytes; mild joint space narrowing |
| 3 | Severe stiffness; pain throughout arc; significant radiological change |
| 4 | End-stage; gross deformity; instability possible |

### ICF Framework Application (HSC NI)

| ICF Domain | Elbow OA Application |
|---|---|
| **Body structure/function** | ROM loss, pain, crepitus, effusion, muscle strength deficit |
| **Activity** | Reaching overhead, carrying, fine motor tasks, driving |
| **Participation** | Work capability (especially manual workers), sports/leisure, ADLs |
| **Contextual factors** | Heavy occupational exposure, prior trauma, BMI, psychological factors |

## Outcome Measures

### 1. DASH (Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder and Hand)

- **Type:** Patient-reported outcome measure (PROM); 30-item questionnaire
- **Scale:** 0–100 (0 = no disability; 100 = maximum disability)
- **Domains:** Physical function/symptoms (mandatory); optional work and sport/performing arts modules
- **MCID:** **10–15 points** (Gummesson et al., 2006; Franchignoni et al., 2014)
- **MDC (90%):** 12.7 points
- **Recall period:** Past week
- **Time to complete:** 5–10 minutes
- **Strengths:** Well-validated across upper limb conditions; widely used in NHS and HSC NI
- **Limitations:** Less sensitive than QuickDASH for detecting change in elbow-specific conditions; ceiling effects in mild-moderate disease

### 2. QuickDASH

- **Type:** 11-item abbreviated DASH
- **Scale:** 0–100
- **MCID:** **15.9 points** (Mintken et al., 2009)
- **MDC:** 21.1 points
- **Note:** Acceptable alternative to DASH where brevity required; strong correlation (r=0.97) with full DASH

### 3. Oxford Elbow Score (OES)

- **Type:** Elbow-specific PROM; 12 items across 3 domains
- **Domains:** Elbow function (4 items), pain (4 items), social-psychological (4 items)
- **Scale:** 0–48 (higher = better outcome) OR converted to 0–100 scale
- **MCID:** **~11 points** (on 0–100 scale) (Dawson et al., 2008; Baker et al., 2012)
- **MDC:** Data limited; approximately 12–14 points suggested
- **Strengths:** Elbow-specific; validated for total elbow arthroplasty and elbow OA; sensitive to change post-intervention
- **Limitations:** Less widely known than DASH; less normative data outside surgical populations

### 4. ASES Elbow Score (American Shoulder and Elbow Surgeons)

- **Type:** Combined physician-rated + patient-reported; 100-point scale
- **Domains:** Pain (Visual Analogue Scale, 50 pts) + Function/ADLs (50 pts)
- **Scale:** 0–100 (higher = better)
- **MCID:** **11–13 points** (Jayakumar et al., 2017; formally validated in arthroplasty but extrapolated to conservative management)
- **Strengths:** Used in most elbow surgical outcome literature; allows direct comparison with operative cohorts; separates pain from function
- **Limitations:** Physician component requires trained administration; less validated for conservative physiotherapy outcomes than DASH

### Recommended Outcome Measure Protocol (HSC NI)

| Timepoint | Recommended Measures |
|---|---|
| Initial assessment | DASH (or QuickDASH) + OES + NRS pain (0–10) + ROM (goniometry) |
| 6-week review | DASH/QuickDASH + NRS pain + ROM |
| Discharge | DASH/QuickDASH + OES + Global Rating of Change (GRC) |
| Surgical referral | DASH + OES + ASES (for surgical baseline) |

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Diagnostic Criteria (Clinical + Radiological)

Elbow OA diagnosis requires **at least two of the following** (adapted from OARSI and clinical consensus):

1. **Age ≥40 years** + insidious or post-traumatic onset
2. **Elbow pain** provoked by terminal ROM
3. **Crepitus** on passive ROM
4. **Radiological changes**: joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, osteophytes, loose bodies

No validated standalone clinical diagnostic criteria exist analogous to ACR criteria for knee/hip OA. Imaging remains the standard for confirmation.

### Imaging

| Modality | Indication | Key Findings |
|---|---|---|
| **Plain radiograph (AP + lateral)** | First-line for all suspected elbow OA | Osteophytes (coronoid, olecranon, radial head); joint space narrowing; subchondral sclerosis; loose bodies |
| **CT** | Pre-surgical planning; loose body localisation | 3D osteophyte mapping; accurate loose body count and location |
| **MRI** | Suspected soft tissue co-pathology; UCL assessment; AVN; early cartilage changes | Cartilage loss, synovitis, ligamentous injury, nerve assessment |
| **Ultrasound** | Guided injection; effusion assessment; dynamic tendon assessment | Effusion; synovitis; osteophyte margins (limited joint visualisation) |

### Nerve Conduction Studies / EMG

Indicated when cubital tunnel syndrome (ulnar neuropathy) is suspected as a co-pathology — present in up to **20% of elbow OA cases** (Takahara et al., 2006). Should be requested prior to surgical referral if neurological symptoms are present.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Differentiating Features | Investigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Rheumatoid arthritis (elbow)** | Bilateral; morning stiffness >1hr; systemic features; warm joint; raised CRP/ESR; RF+/anti-CCP+ | Bloods: CRP, ESR, RF, anti-CCP; hand/wrist X-ray |
| **Psoriatic arthritis** | Psoriasis history/family history; DIP involvement; enthesitis; dactylitis | Dermatology review; HLA-B27; imaging |
| **Gout / pseudogout** | Acute episodic flares; olecranon tophi (gout); hot swollen joint; hyperuricaemia | Serum urate; synovial fluid for crystals; X-ray |
| **Septic arthritis** | Fever; raised WBC/CRP; very hot/swollen joint; systemically unwell; rapid onset | **Emergency**: joint aspiration; blood cultures; WBC |
| **Cubital tunnel syndrome** | Medial elbow pain + ulnar paraesthesia (ring/little finger); intrinsic wasting | NCS/EMG; Tinel's; elbow flexion test |
| **Lateral epicondylalgia** | Lateral pain on resisted wrist extension/gripping; point-tender lateral epicondyle; no ROM loss initially | Clinical diagnosis; USS if uncertain; exclude radial nerve entrapment |
| **Medial epicondylalgia** | Medial pain on resisted wrist flexion/pronation; no joint line tenderness | Clinical diagnosis |
| **Osteochondritis dissecans (OCD)** | Younger patient (<25); lateral elbow pain; activity-related; radiocapitellar on X-ray/MRI | Plain X-ray; MRI for lesion stability |
| **Radial head fracture/non-union** | Trauma history; lateral pain; limited supination/pronation; X-ray confirms | X-ray; CT if occult |
| **Posterior interosseous nerve entrapment** | Weakness of finger/wrist extensors without sensory loss; lateral elbow pain; no joint line crepitus | EMG/NCS; MRI |
| **Cervical radiculopathy (C6/C7)** | Neck pain; dermatomal distribution; Spurling's +ve; pain not localised to joint | Cervical X-ray/MRI; NCS |
| **Olecranon bursitis** | Posterior swelling over olecranon tip; joint ROM preserved; fluctuant; may be septic | Aspiration if septic suspected; USS |
| **Haemophilic arthropathy** | Known haemophilia; recurrent haemarthroses; X-ray changes similar to OA | Clotting screen; haematology |
| **PVNS (pigmented villonodular synovitis)** | Recurrent haemarthrosis without adequate trauma; young adult; MRI characteristic | MRI (blooming artefact on T2*); synovial biopsy |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Physiotherapy Pathway

The following pathway is consistent with **NICE OA guidelines (NG226, 2022)** and **RCOT/CSP standards**. Elbow OA should be managed within the musculoskeletal physiotherapy service with clear criteria for onward referral.

```
Presentation → Physiotherapy Initial Assessment
        ↓
  Red flags? → YES → Escalate (see Red Flags section)
        ↓ NO
  Confirm diagnosis (clinical + radiological if not done)
        ↓
  Core Conservative Management (All patients — NG226)
  [Education + exercise + weight management if relevant]
        ↓
  6–12 week review with PROMs (DASH, OES, NRS)
        ↓
  Inadequate response / significant functional limitation?
        ↓ YES
  Consider: Intra-articular injection / Orthopaedic referral
        ↓
  Orthopaedic review (conservative options exhausted)
        ↓
  Surgical options: arthroscopic debridement / arthroplasty
```

### Conservative Management

#### 1. Education & Self-Management (NICE NG226 — Core Treatment)

All patients with elbow OA should receive:
- **Explanation of the diagnosis and natural history**: Elbow OA is manageable; most patients maintain function without surgery
- **Activity modification**: Avoid provocative end-range loading; adapt occupational activities where possible
- **Joint protection principles**: Ergonomic advice for work and daily tasks
- **Self-management resources**: Arthritis Research UK / Versus Arthritis patient information

#### 2. Exercise Therapy (NICE NG226 — Core Treatment; high priority)

- **Strengthening**: Progressive resistance exercise targeting elbow flexors/extensors, forearm pronators/supinators, shoulder girdle musculature
  - Evidence: Strengthening reduces pain and improves function in peripheral joint OA (NICE NG226; Fernandes et al., 2013)
  - Frequency: 2–3x/week progressive loading; 8–12 weeks minimum
- **Range of motion / flexibility**: Daily active and active-assisted ROM exercises to maintain functional arc
  - Focus: Maintaining 30°–130° functional arc
  - Caution: Avoid aggressive passive stretching in the presence of significant osteophytes — can exacerbate impingement
- **Aerobic exercise**: NICE NG226 recommends general aerobic exercise for all OA patients — cardiovascular benefit and pain modulation
- **Hydrotherapy**: May be considered for patients with significant stiffness and pain limiting land-based exercise (moderate evidence)

#### 3. Manual Therapy

- May be used **adjunctively** with exercise (not as a standalone treatment — NICE NG226)
- Mobilisation with movement (Mulligan concept) for ROM restoration: limited but positive evidence in elbow stiffness
- Soft tissue techniques for capsular tightness: some benefit in improving ROM; evidence extrapolated from post-traumatic elbow stiffness literature
- **Evidence level**: Low–moderate; insufficient elbow OA-specific RCT data

#### 4. Thermotherapy

- **Heat (moist heat/hot pack)**: Pre-exercise; may improve tissue extensibility and reduce pain; patient preference guides use
- **Ice**: Post-exercise; for acute inflammatory flares; olecranon bursitis management adjunct
- **NICE NG226**: Acknowledges thermotherapy may provide short-term symptomatic relief; insufficient evidence to recommend or discourage

#### 5. Orthoses / Splinting

- **Dynamic splinting for flexion contracture**: Turnbuckle or static progressive splints — evidence from post-traumatic elbow contracture literature; may apply in severe OA contracture
- **Activity-modification bracing**: Limited role in elbow OA specifically; may reduce load during heavy occupational tasks
- **Referral to occupational therapy**: For adaptive equipment, workplace assessment, ADL strategies

#### 6. TENS

- May provide short-term analgesia; NICE NG226 does not recommend for long-term management but acknowledges patient preference may guide use

### Pharmacological Management (NICE NG226, 2022)

> Physiotherapists should be aware of the NICE NG226 pharmacological pathway for co-ordinated MDT management. Prescribing remains with the GP/prescribing clinician.

| Step | Agent | NICE NG226 Recommendation | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Step 1** | **Topical NSAIDs** (e.g., diclofenac 1% gel) | **First-line analgesia** for peripheral joint OA | Apply 3–4x/day; lower systemic risk; trial for 4–6 weeks; limited evidence specifically for elbow OA |
| **Step 2** | **Oral NSAIDs** (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen, celecoxib) | Consider if topical insufficient | Use lowest effective dose, shortest duration; gastroprotection (PPI) if indicated; caution in CKD, CVD, GI risk |
| **Step 3** | **Paracetamol** | NG226 (2022 update): **no longer recommended as routine first-line** for OA (evidence insufficient for meaningful pain relief at recommended doses) | May still be used for individual patient preference alongside other analgesia |
| **Step 3** | **Intra-articular corticosteroid injection** | Consider for short-term flare management when other treatments inadequate | Benefit typically 4–12 weeks; ultrasound guidance improves accuracy; no more than 3 injections/year; evidence modest for elbow specifically |
| **Avoid** | **Opioids** | NG226: Do not offer opioids for OA management | Harms outweigh benefits in OA; significant addiction risk |
| **Avoid** | **Glucosamine / chondroitin** | NG226: Do not offer | No clinically meaningful benefit demonstrated |
| **Avoid** | **Intra-articular hyaluronic acid** | Not recommended by NICE NG226 | Insufficient evidence of benefit in peripheral joint OA |

> **NICE NG226 core principle**: Pharmacological management should always be adjunctive to core treatments (education + exercise). Do not use pharmacology as a substitute for exercise-based management.

### Onward Referral Criteria

#### Orthopaedic Referral (Elective)

Refer when the following criteria are met:
- Conservative physiotherapy management for **≥12 weeks** has failed to achieve adequate pain control or functional improvement
- Significant functional limitation confirmed by PROMs (DASH ≥40; OES ≤30/100)
- Radiological evidence of moderate-severe OA (Grade II–IV)
- **Loose bodies** causing recurrent locking episodes — arthroscopic removal indicated
- **Flexion contracture >30°** with significant functional impact unresponsive to conservative management
- Patient is willing and medically fit to consider surgical intervention

#### Surgical Options (for Orthopaedic Awareness)

| Procedure | Indication | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Arthroscopic debridement** (Outerbridge–Kashiwagi procedure) | Loose body removal; osteophyte resection; capsular release; mild–moderate OA | Good short-term outcomes; 70–90% satisfaction at 2 years (Antuna et al., 2002; Krishnan et al., 2007) |
| **Open debridement** | Moderate–severe OA where arthroscopic access limited; posterior osteophytes | Historical gold standard; superseded by arthroscopy in many centres |
| **Total Elbow Arthroplasty (TEA)** | End-stage OA; RA; failed previous surgery | 10-year survival ~85–90% (Cheung et al., 2010); **activity restrictions post-TEA**: lifting limit 2.3kg (5lbs) long-term |
| **Radial head arthroplasty** | Isolated radiocapitellar OA; failed radial head excision | Limited elbow OA-specific data |
| **Interposition arthroplasty** | Young active patients unsuitable for TEA | Alternative to TEA; preserves bone stock |

#### Urgent/Expedited Orthopaedic Referral

- Acute locking with inability to extend — possible displaced loose body
- Rapid neurological deterioration (cubital tunnel compression by osteophyte)
- Suspected septic arthritis (same-day ED)
- Suspected malignancy (2-week-wait pathway)

#### Rheumatology Referral

- Suspected inflammatory arthropathy (RA, PsA, gout refractory to GP management)
- Unusual presentation; young age; polyarthritis

#### Occupational Therapy Referral

- Patients with significant ADL or work-related functional impairment
- Manual workers requiring workstation/tool adaptation
- Post-surgical rehabilitation (TEA)
