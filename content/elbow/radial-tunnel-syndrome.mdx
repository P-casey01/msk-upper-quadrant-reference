## Overview & Pathophysiology

Radial tunnel syndrome (RTS) is a chronic pain condition resulting from compression or irritation of the radial nerve (specifically the posterior interosseous nerve — PIN) within the radial tunnel. It is arguably the most diagnostically challenging and controversial condition in elbow pathology, with debate persisting in the literature regarding its existence as a distinct clinical entity separate from lateral epicondylalgia.

**Prevalence:** Poorly defined due to diagnostic difficulty. Often under-diagnosed. Estimated to account for 5% of cases initially presenting as lateral epicondylalgia. More common in manual workers.

**Anatomy of the Radial Tunnel:**
The radial tunnel extends from the radiocapitellar joint to the proximal border of the supinator muscle — approximately 5 cm long. The posterior interosseous nerve (deep branch of radial nerve) traverses this tunnel.

**Sites of compression within the radial tunnel (medial to lateral):**
1. **Fibrous bands anterior to radiocapitellar joint** — most distal compression point
2. **Radial recurrent vessels (leash of Henry)** — vascular tether against nerve
3. **Tendinous medial border of ECRB** — sharp tendinous edge impinges nerve with pronation/wrist extension
4. **Arcade of Frohse (proximal edge of superficial head of supinator)** — most common compression site (75% of cases)
5. **Distal edge of supinator** — less common

**Pathophysiology:**
Compression at any of the above sites produces:
- Intraneural oedema → ischaemia → pain and (in severe cases) axonal compromise
- In pure RTS: pain without significant motor deficit (distinguishing from posterior interosseous nerve palsy — PINP)
- Mechanism: repetitive forearm rotation (pronation/supination) + elbow extension creates intermittent compression at Arcade of Frohse

**Important distinction:**
- **Radial Tunnel Syndrome:** Primarily painful; minimal or no motor deficit; often confused with lateral epicondylalgia
- **Posterior Interosseous Nerve Palsy (PINP):** Motor deficit prominent (finger/wrist drop); may be painless; different management

**Concurrent lateral epicondylalgia:** Co-exists in up to 5% of cases; the two conditions can be concurrent but require independent assessment.

**Note on evidence quality:** RTS diagnostic test evidence is of low quality. No high-quality systematic review of special tests exists. Values above represent best available data (primarily from Lister et al. 1979, Portilla Molina et al. 1998, Sotereanos et al. 1999).

**Radial Tunnel Palpation:** Tenderness localised 3–5 cm distal and anterior to lateral epicondyle (over radial tunnel), NOT over the lateral epicondyle itself. This is the key clinical differentiator from lateral epicondylalgia.

**Resisted Supination Test:** Elbow extended, forearm fully pronated. Patient supinates against resistance. Positive = radial tunnel pain. Compresses PIN against Arcade of Frohse.

**Diagnostic Nerve Block:** Infiltration of local anaesthetic (e.g., 1% lidocaine 3–5 ml) into the radial tunnel. Resolution of symptoms confirms diagnosis. More reliable than clinical tests alone in ambiguous cases.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Distinguishing RTS from posterior interosseous nerve palsy is critical — motor deficit requires urgent escalation.

- **Finger/wrist drop (PINP):** Inability to extend fingers/wrist at MCP level; wrist extensors may be partially preserved (ECRL preserved — exits proximal to tunnel); requires urgent assessment → MRI ± NCS/EMG ± surgical decompression
- **Rapidly progressive weakness** developing over days-weeks → urgent MRI + orthopaedic/neurosurgical referral
- **Swelling or mass at lateral elbow/radial tunnel:** Lipoma, ganglion, rheumatoid synovitis, tumour — common and treatable causes of compressive PINP → MRI + referral
- **Post-traumatic radial nerve palsy** (radial head fracture, proximal radius, elbow dislocation): PINP is a known complication → urgent orthopaedic assessment
- **Radial nerve palsy at spiral groove** (Saturday night palsy, humeral fracture): affects wrist extension + supinator; different level from RTS — distinguish on NCS
- **Systemic inflammatory disease** (RA, sarcoidosis): can cause nerve compression via synovial proliferation → systemic assessment

## Clinical Frameworks

### Differential Framework: RTS vs Lateral Epicondylalgia

| Feature | Lateral Epicondylalgia | Radial Tunnel Syndrome |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Pain location | Over lateral epicondyle | 3–5 cm distal/anterior to LE (radial tunnel) |
| Resisted wrist extension | Painful (Cozen's positive) | Usually negative |
| Resisted supination | Usually negative | Positive |
| Palpation tenderness | At lateral epicondyle | At radial tunnel (over extensor muscle bulk) |
| Night pain | Uncommon | More common (forearm ache) |
| US/MRI changes | Tendon changes at epicondyle | Normal tendons; may show nerve changes |
| NCS/EMG | Normal | Normal or mildly abnormal in severe |
| Diagnostic nerve block response | Partial (local anaesthetic) | Complete resolution in true RTS |

### Severity Framework (Lister Classification)
- **Type I:** Diffuse ache in dorsal forearm; no neurological deficit; tender at Arcade of Frohse
- **Type II:** More localised pain; early motor fatigue; slight grip weakness
- **Type III (PINP):** Motor deficit prominent; wrist/finger extension weakness; surgical priority

## Outcome Measures

### Patient-Reported Outcomes
**DASH / QuickDASH:**
- Most commonly used in RTS research and clinical practice
- MCID: DASH 10.2; QuickDASH 16 points
- Captures functional disability from forearm pain and any motor deficit

**PRTEE (Patient-Rated Tennis Elbow Evaluation):**
- Often used in RTS studies due to clinical overlap with LE
- 15 items; 0–100; MCID 11 points
- Useful for monitoring symptom response to intervention

**Numeric Pain Rating Scale:**
- Forearm pain (0–10); assess at rest, with resisted supination, with sustained grip
- MCID: 2 points

### Objective Measures
**Grip Strength:**
- Reduced in RTS due to pain inhibition and intrinsic weakness
- Baseline measurement important; Jamar at 90° elbow flexion
- MCID: 6.5 kg

**Pinch Strength:**
- Less affected in RTS vs PINP (intrinsics partially spared)
- Baseline assessment useful

**Forearm Rotation ROM:**
- Supination may be restricted/painful in active radial tunnel compression
- Goniometry at baseline and review

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

RTS remains a **clinical diagnosis** with no definitive objective test. The evidence base for diagnostic criteria is weak. Approach:

**Step 1 — Clinical Assessment:**
- History: occupational repetitive supination, lateral forearm ache (often nocturnal), no sensory deficit, no motor weakness
- Examination: tenderness at radial tunnel (NOT lateral epicondyle); positive resisted supination; negative Cozen's

**Step 2 — Exclude Lateral Epicondylalgia:**
- If Cozen's positive AND radial tunnel tenderness AND lateral epicondyle tenderness → concurrent LE and RTS possible
- NCS normal in both conditions; does not differentiate

**Step 3 — Diagnostic Nerve Block:**
- If clinical diagnosis unclear: infiltrate 2–3 ml 1% lidocaine into radial tunnel
- Complete relief: confirms RTS; partial relief: concurrent LE likely; no relief: alternative diagnosis

**Nerve Conduction Studies:**
- Usually NORMAL in RTS (motor nerve conduction across tunnel may be mildly reduced: <50 m/s)
- EMG: normal in RTS; denervation in PINP (FDI, EDC — finger extensors)
- Primary role: EXCLUDE cervical radiculopathy (C6/C7) and confirm PINP diagnosis
- Indicated if: motor deficit present, atypical features, pre-surgical

**Ultrasound:**
- Limited sensitivity for RTS; may show nerve oedema, perineural fibrosis, compressive mass
- Useful to exclude ganglion, lipoma, other compressive lesions
- Cross-sectional area of PIN at Arcade of Frohse: >3 mm² suggests compression (limited data)

**MRI:**
- Detects mass lesions, PINP, post-inflammatory changes
- Not routine for RTS; indicated for surgical planning or atypical cases
- Can show denervation oedema in extensor muscles in PINP

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features | Key Investigation |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Lateral epicondylalgia | Lateral epicondyle tenderness; positive Cozen's; US tendinopathy | Clinical; US |
| Posterior interosseous nerve palsy | Finger/wrist drop; no sensory deficit; motor deficit on EMG | NCS/EMG; MRI |
| Cervical radiculopathy C6/C7 | Neck pain; Spurling's positive; dermatomal sensory change; reflex changes | Cervical MRI |
| Radial nerve compression at spiral groove | Wrist drop; impaired brachioradialis; after prolonged arm compression | NCS/EMG |
| Elbow OA | Stiffness; crepitus; X-ray changes; older patient | X-ray |
| Synovitis of radiocapitellar joint | Joint line tenderness; effusion; ROM loss | US; MRI |
| Lateral elbow instability (PLRI) | Clicking; apprehension; trauma history | Pivot shift test; MRI |
| Radial head fracture | Acute trauma; bony tenderness; X-ray fracture | X-ray |
| Tendinopathy of ECRB (isolated) | Mid-muscle belly tenderness; not at tunnel | US |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Community Physiotherapy / GP:**
- Initial management of mild-moderate RTS
- Education, activity modification, ergonomic advice, trial of conservative treatment
- GP review if no improvement at 6–8 weeks

**Secondary Care Referral:**
- Orthopaedics/Hand Surgery if:
  - Diagnostic uncertainty requiring nerve block or MRI
  - PINP (motor deficit) — urgent pathway
  - Failed 6 months conservative management
- Neurophysiology for NCS/EMG: via GP or consultant; confirms diagnosis/excludes PINP

---

### Conservative Management

**Activity Modification (Grade C):**
- Avoid repetitive forearm pronation-supination
- Reduce sustained elbow extension with forearm pronated
- Ergonomic review: tool use, keyboard, assembly line tasks

**Rest and Splinting (Grade C):**
- Cock-up wrist splint + forearm in neutral position during symptomatic phases
- Reduces traction on PIN; no RCT evidence but theoretically sound
- Avoid prolonged immobilisation (>2–3 weeks) — may worsen stiffness

**Exercise Therapy (Grade C — extrapolated from LE evidence):**
- Supinator stretching: forearm pronation + wrist ulnar deviation (gently holds 30 seconds × 5)
- Progressive supinator and wrist extensor strengthening
- No specific validated exercise protocol for RTS; adapt LE programme

**Neural Mobilisation (Grade C):**
- Radial nerve neurodynamic technique:
  - Position: shoulder depression + internal rotation; elbow extension; forearm pronation; wrist/finger flexion; thumb flexion
  - Gentle sliding technique (oscillate neck lateral flexion toward/away)
  - 3 × 10 repetitions; 0–4/10 pain acceptable
- Useful for concurrent neural tension component

**Manual Therapy (Grade C):**
- Radiohumeral joint mobilisation (lateral distraction, posterior glide)
- Limited evidence but may improve symptoms in concurrent LE/RTS presentations

---

### Pharmacological

**Oral NSAIDs (Grade C):**
- Short course for acute inflammatory flares
- Limited evidence specific to RTS; extrapolated from LE
- NICE standard: lowest effective dose; gastroprotection if >7 days

**Corticosteroid Injection (Grade B for diagnostic/therapeutic):**
- Combined diagnostic nerve block + corticosteroid: methylprednisolone 40 mg + 1–2 ml local anaesthetic
- Provides diagnostic confirmation and therapeutic effect
- Up to 70% respond to injection with sustained relief (Roles & Maudsley 1972)
- Ultrasound guidance recommended for accurate radial tunnel placement
- Risk: PIN injury if intra-neural injection (low with experienced operator + US guidance)
- Repeat injection: consider if initial response was good; maximum 2–3 injections

**Neuropathic agents:**
- If neuropathic component prominent (burning, allodynia): pregabalin or gabapentin (NICE neuropathic pain guidelines)

---

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Refer to Orthopaedics/Hand Surgery if:**
- PINP with motor deficit → urgent referral within 2–4 weeks for surgical decompression
- Mass lesion compressing PIN → semi-urgent referral
- Failed 6 months conservative management with confirmed RTS
- Diagnostic uncertainty after nerve block trial

**Surgical Options:**
- **Open decompression:** Release of all potential compression sites (Arcade of Frohse, ECRB medial edge, leash of Henry, fibrous bands)
- **Approach:** Anterior (Henry), posterior (Thompson), or lateral approaches used
- **Outcomes:** Variable; 67–92% improvement reported (Roles & Maudsley 1972; Lister et al. 1979)
- **Recovery:** 3–6 months for full return to manual work; physiotherapy for ROM and strengthening post-op

**Caution:** Surgical outcomes for RTS inferior to surgical outcomes for PINP or lateral epicondylalgia. Patient selection critical — ensure diagnosis confirmed with nerve block prior to surgery.
