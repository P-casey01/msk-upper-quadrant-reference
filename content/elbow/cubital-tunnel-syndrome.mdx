## Overview & Pathophysiology

Cubital tunnel syndrome (CuTS) is the second most common peripheral nerve entrapment syndrome (after carpal tunnel syndrome), accounting for approximately 20% of peripheral nerve compressions. Prevalence estimates range from 25–38 per 100,000 person-years; higher in manual workers, musicians, and those with habitual elbow flexion.

**Anatomy of the Cubital Tunnel:**
The cubital tunnel is a fibroosseous tunnel at the medial elbow through which the ulnar nerve passes. Key anatomical structures:
- **Floor:** Medial collateral ligament (ulnar head) and posterior capsule
- **Roof:** Osborne's fascia (arcuate ligament) — the most common site of compression
- **Walls:** Medial epicondyle (medial), olecranon (lateral)
- **Contents:** Ulnar nerve + accompanying vessels

**Other sites of ulnar nerve entrapment at the elbow:**
1. Medial intermuscular septum (proximal compression)
2. Arcade of Struthers (fascial band, 8 cm proximal to medial epicondyle)
3. Cubital tunnel (Osborne's fascia) — most common
4. Between heads of flexor carpi ulnaris (distal to cubital tunnel)

**Pathophysiology of compression:**
- During elbow flexion, the cubital tunnel narrows by 55% (MacNicol 1979), increasing intraneural pressure
- Normal nerve: 7–8 mmHg at rest; cubital tunnel pressure rises to 100–200 mmHg at 90° flexion
- Sustained compression → intraneural oedema → myelin loss → axonal degeneration (in severe/chronic cases)
- Nerve subluxation (snapping) over medial epicondyle in 20% of population → repetitive traction injury

**Risk factors:**
- Habitual elbow flexion (sleeping, driving, phone use)
- Prolonged leaning on medial elbow
- Repetitive elbow flexion-extension (overhead athletes, musicians)
- Medial epicondyle fracture/deformity
- Cubitus valgus deformity
- Medial elbow osteoarthritis, loose bodies
- Diabetes mellitus (peripheral neuropathy — double crush)
- Obesity, smoking

**Elbow Flexion Test (Wadsworth 1987):** Patient maximally flexes elbow with wrist extended, held for 60 seconds. Positive = tingling/numbness in ring and little finger. Best single test for CuTS.

**Novak's Combined Provocation Test:** Elbow flexion + wrist extension + pressure over cubital tunnel × 60 seconds. Near-perfect sensitivity; highest diagnostic value.

**Scratch Collapse Test (Cheng et al. 2008):** Examiner scratches skin over suspected compression site; patient momentarily loses shoulder resistance. Highly specific for nerve compression location.

**Froment's Sign:** Patient holds paper between thumb and index finger; examiner pulls paper away. Positive = thumb IP joint flexes (compensating for adductor pollicis weakness with FPL) — indicates ulnar nerve motor involvement.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Urgent assessment and escalation required.

- **Rapidly progressive motor deficit:** Significant intrinsic muscle wasting or clawing developing over days-weeks → urgent surgical referral (nerve decompression within weeks to prevent permanent deficit)
- **Complete sensory loss in ulnar distribution** with marked motor weakness → severe/advanced compression; surgical review as priority
- **Post-traumatic ulnar neuropathy** following elbow fracture (medial epicondyle, supracondylar, distal humerus): late-onset ulnar palsy may be progressive → orthopaedic + neurophysiology
- **Cubitus valgus with ulnar neuropathy** (tardy ulnar palsy): may be post-traumatic (childhood fracture); progressive without intervention → surgical decompression/transposition
- **Rapidly expanding medial elbow mass:** Suspect ganglion, lipoma, tumour compressing nerve → urgent imaging + referral
- **Pancoast tumour/Thoracic outlet:** Pain radiating from shoulder/neck into ulnar distribution; Horner's syndrome; weight loss → urgent chest X-ray + CT
- **Double crush syndrome:** Concurrent cervical radiculopathy (C8/T1) + cubital tunnel compression; both sites need treatment

## Clinical Frameworks

### McGowan Classification of CuTS Severity

| Grade | Symptoms | Sensory | Motor | Management |
|-------|----------|---------|-------|------------|
| I (Mild) | Intermittent paraesthesia; minimal | Normal or intermittent loss | Normal intrinsics | Conservative (splinting, activity modification) |
| II (Moderate) | Persistent paraesthesia; aching | Constant sensory deficit | Moderate weakness | Conservative trial 3–6 months; consider surgery |
| III (Severe) | Constant symptoms | Marked sensory deficit | Significant wasting; clawing | Surgical decompression; conservative unlikely to succeed |

### Dellon Classification
- Mild: Intermittent paraesthesia; positive elbow flexion test; no muscle weakness
- Moderate: Intermittent paraesthesia; positive provocative tests; weak interossei
- Severe: Persistent paraesthesia; clawing; intrinsic muscle atrophy

### Neurodynamic Assessment
Upper Limb Neurodynamic Test 3 (ULNT3 — ulnar bias):
- Position: Shoulder depression, wrist/finger extension, elbow flexion, shoulder abduction, cervical lateral flexion away
- Sensitising: Cervical lateral flexion toward; wrist extension/flexion
- Positive: Reproduction of concordant symptoms; asymmetrical response vs contralateral
- Use for: Screening, differentiating peripheral vs central sensitisation, guiding neural mobilisation

## Outcome Measures

### Patient-Reported Measures
**DASH / QuickDASH:**
- Most widely used for upper limb nerve compression
- MCID: DASH 10.2 points; QuickDASH 16 points
- Work module available for occupational contexts

**Boston/Levine CTS Questionnaire:** Not validated for CuTS; use DASH instead.

**Patient-Rated Ulnar Nerve Evaluation (PRUNE):** Validated specifically for ulnar neuropathy; 17 items; symptom and function subscales. Less widely available but more condition-specific.

### Objective Measures
**Grip Strength (Jamar):**
- Compare dominant vs non-dominant; affected vs unaffected
- MCID: 6.5 kg; ICC 0.97–0.98
- Serial measurement tracks motor recovery post-decompression

**Pinch Strength (lateral and tip pinch):**
- Tip pinch directly measures intrinsic function
- Lateral pinch tests adductor pollicis (ulnar nerve) + FPL (median nerve)

**Sensory Testing:**
- Semmes-Weinstein monofilament testing: quantifies sensory threshold in ring/little finger
- 2-point discrimination (static and moving): >6 mm static 2PD = abnormal; tracks recovery
- Vibration threshold (128 Hz tuning fork): reduced in ulnar distribution in moderate/severe CuTS

**Functional Dexterity:**
- Purdue Pegboard test (manual dexterity; sensitive to intrinsic muscle weakness)
- Nine-hole peg test: functional fine motor speed assessment

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**Clinical diagnosis** based on symptom pattern + provocative tests is appropriate for initial management. NCS confirmation required before surgical intervention.

**Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) — Gold Standard:**
- Ulnar motor conduction velocity across elbow: **<50 m/s** (or >10 m/s slowing compared to forearm segment) is diagnostic
- Compound Motor Action Potential (CMAP) amplitude: reduced in axonal degeneration
- Sensory nerve action potential (SNAP): reduced or absent in sensory fibre involvement
- Inching technique: localises compression to specific 2 cm segment
- **Referral pathway (HSC NI):** Via GP or outpatient consultant; Neurophysiology departments in Belfast Trust, Southern Trust, Western Trust; typical wait 6–16 weeks

**EMG:**
- Detects denervation in FDI, ADM, FCU
- Chronic denervation: fibrillations, positive sharp waves, reduced recruitment
- Useful for severity stratification and surgical planning

**Ultrasound:**
- Cross-sectional area of ulnar nerve at cubital tunnel: >10 mm² associated with compression (sensitivity 70–80%)
- Dynamic assessment: nerve subluxation over medial epicondyle
- Increasingly used as first-line imaging; lower cost than MRI

**MRI:**
- Nerve signal change, perineural fibrosis, intraneural oedema
- Identifies compressive lesions (ganglion, accessory muscle, post-traumatic fibrosis)
- Reserved for diagnostic uncertainty or surgical planning

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features | Key Investigation |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Cervical radiculopathy C8 | Neck pain; Spurling's positive; triceps weakness; absent triceps reflex | Cervical MRI; NCS shows normal forearm ulnar velocity |
| Thoracic outlet syndrome | Positional; overhead provocation; vascular signs; Adson's/ROOS positive | Cervical X-ray; vascular duplex; NCS |
| Medial epicondylalgia | Tendinopathy signs; no sensory deficit; no intrinsic weakness | Clinical; NCS normal |
| Guyon's canal syndrome (ulnar at wrist) | Spares FCU (wrist flexion intact); NCS shows distal compression; dorsal ulnar sensory SNAP normal | NCS/EMG; wrist ultrasound |
| Double crush syndrome | Combined cervical + cubital compression | NCS both regions; MRI spine |
| Pancoast tumour | Shoulder girdle pain; Horner's; weight loss; smoking history | Chest X-ray; CT chest |
| Peripheral neuropathy (diabetic, alcoholic) | Bilateral; length-dependent; other nerves involved | NCS bilateral; bloods |
| Ulnar nerve tumour | Firm, fixed mass on nerve; Tinel's at mass | MRI; ultrasound |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Community Physiotherapy:**
- Mild-moderate CuTS (McGowan Grade I–II): initial management in community
- Activity modification, splinting, neural mobilisation, ergonomic advice
- Monitor neurological status; refer urgently if motor deficit progresses

**Neurophysiology:**
- NCS via GP referral (or consultant referral in secondary care)
- Belfast Trust (Royal Victoria Hospital, Musgrave Park), Southern Trust (Daisy Hill), Western Trust (Altnagelvin)
- Wait times: 6–16 weeks typically; urgent pathway via consultant if rapid neurological decline

**Orthopaedics / Hand Surgery (Secondary Care):**
- Referral via GP (elective) or direct from neurophysiology if severe NCS findings
- McGowan Grade II–III with failed conservative management → surgical assessment
- Pre-surgical NCS/EMG mandatory in most units

**Private / Expedited Pathway:**
- Private neurophysiology available in Belfast, Derry, Newry for faster access
- Private hand surgery available at Ulster Independent Clinic, Kingsbridge Private Hospital

### Conservative Management

#### Activity Modification (Grade B)
- **Avoid sustained elbow flexion >90°**: phone use (speaker/headset), sleeping position
- **Avoid repetitive elbow flexion-extension** under load
- **Avoid medial elbow pressure**: padding on work surfaces, armrests
- Workplace ergonomic assessment (HSE NI guidance)

#### Elbow Extension Splinting at Night (Grade A)
- **Evidence:** RCT (Dellon 1989; Seror 1993) — night splinting equivalent to surgical decompression in mild-moderate CuTS at 12 months
- Splint: elbow at 30–45° flexion (extension); wrist neutral or slight extension
- Fabricated thermoplastic splint (hand therapy) or prefabricated elbow extension orthosis
- Duration: minimum 3 months; worn during sleep and prolonged elbow-flexion activities
- Compliance is the limiting factor

#### Nerve Gliding Exercises (Grade B)
Upper Limb Neurodynamic Mobilisation (ULNT3 — Ulnar Bias):
- Position: shoulder abduction, external rotation; elbow flexion; wrist/finger extension
- **Sliding technique (neural flossing):** Alternating neck lateral flexion away with elbow extension; 10 × 3 reps/day
- **Tensioner technique:** For more chronic presentations; hold position at neural tissue tension
- Pain should not exceed 4/10 during exercise; no lasting post-exercise symptoms

#### Education
- Sleep position modification (pillow under arm to prevent elbow flexion >90°)
- Phone/device use modification
- Workplace assessment: seated ergonomics, tool use, forearm support

### Pharmacological

**Oral NSAIDs (Grade C — limited evidence for nerve entrapment):**
- Short course for acute inflammatory flare or concurrent medial epicondylalgia
- No direct evidence in CuTS; reduce perineural inflammation theoretically
- NICE: lowest effective dose; gastroprotection if >7 days; avoid in renal impairment

**Corticosteroid Injection:**
- Limited evidence for CuTS vs carpal tunnel (where CSI has good evidence)
- Risk: ulnar nerve injury with perilesional injection → ultrasound guidance mandatory
- Not standard practice; occasionally used for severe inflammatory/rheumatoid cases
- Discuss risk/benefit with patient; document informed consent

**Gabapentinoids (neuropathic pain):**
- Pregabalin 25–150 mg BD or gabapentin 300 mg TDS for neuropathic pain component
- NICE guidance: consider if neuropathic pain component prominent
- Caution: sedation, dependence potential (Schedule 3 CD in UK since 2019)

**Vitamin B6/B12:**
- No robust evidence for CuTS; sometimes used empirically in peripheral neuropathy
- Not routinely recommended in NICE guidance

---

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Refer to Orthopaedics/Hand Surgery (elective) if:**
- McGowan Grade II–III (moderate-severe)
- Failed ≥3–6 months conservative management (splinting + activity modification)
- NCS confirmed significant conduction slowing (ulnar CV <50 m/s across elbow) or axonal loss
- Occupation at risk (musician, manual worker) with persistent symptoms

**URGENT referral if:**
- Progressive motor deficit (intrinsic wasting, clawing) over weeks
- Severe NCS: CMAP amplitude <50% of normal; fibrillations on EMG
- Anatomical cause identified (post-traumatic, ganglion, accessory muscle)

**Surgical Options (Consultant Decision):**

| Procedure | Indication | Evidence |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| In-situ decompression | Mild-moderate; no nerve subluxation; no anatomical cause | Good; 70–85% success |
| Anterior transposition (subcutaneous) | Nerve subluxation; anatomical compression | Equivalent to in-situ in most; 75–90% success |
| Anterior transposition (submuscular) | Severe compression; revision; valgus deformity | More complex; longest recovery |
| Medial epicondylectomy | Selected cases; less common | Equivalent outcomes but nerve risk |

Post-operative physiotherapy: scar management, oedema, ROM, progressive strengthening; hand therapy involvement essential.
