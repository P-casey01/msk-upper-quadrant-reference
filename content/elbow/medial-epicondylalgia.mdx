## Overview & Pathophysiology

Medial epicondylalgia (ME) — historically termed "golfer's elbow" or medial epicondylitis — is a common cause of medial elbow pain, accounting for approximately 10–20% of elbow tendinopathies presenting in clinical practice. True incidence is lower than lateral epicondylalgia; estimated at 0.4% of the general population per year, with peak onset in the 4th–5th decade.

**Pathophysiology:** As with lateral epicondylalgia, histological evidence confirms angiofibroblastic tendinosis rather than true inflammation. Nirschl (1992) described the same pathological changes: disorganised collagen, fibroblast proliferation, neovascularisation, absence of inflammatory cells. The condition represents failed tendon healing at the common flexor-pronator origin.

**Primary structures involved:**
- **Pronator teres** — most commonly affected (originates from medial epicondyle)
- **Flexor carpi radialis** — frequently co-involved
- **Flexor carpi ulnaris, palmaris longus, flexor digitorum superficialis** — less commonly

The origin is on the anterior aspect of the medial epicondyle; valgus stress during acceleration phases of throwing or golfing creates repetitive tensile overload.

**Mechanism of injury:**
- Repetitive wrist flexion and forearm pronation under load
- Valgus loading of medial elbow (throwing, golf, racquet sports)
- Occupational exposure: carpentry, plumbing, manual handling

**Important association — Ulnar collateral ligament (UCL):**
- UCL insufficiency co-exists in up to 60% of throwing athletes with ME
- Distinguish clinically as management differs (UCL reconstruction vs tendinopathy rehabilitation)

**Concurrent cubital tunnel syndrome:**
- Ulnar nerve involvement in 20–60% of ME cases
- Critical to assess for ulnar neuropathy at every consultation
- Failure to identify ulnar nerve involvement leads to suboptimal outcomes

| Passive wrist extension stretch (medial Mill's) | 0.62 | 0.72 | 2.21 | 0.53 | Low |

| Valgus stress test (UCL assessment) | 0.65 | 0.48 | 1.25 | 0.73 | Low |
| Moving valgus stress test (O'Driscoll) | 0.100 | 0.75 | 4.00 | 0.00 | Moderate (throwing athletes) |
| Tinel's at cubital tunnel | 0.70 | 0.66 | 2.06 | 0.45 | Moderate |
| Elbow flexion test (ulnar nerve provocation) | 0.75 | 0.99 | 75.0 | 0.25 | Moderate-High |

**Resisted Wrist Flexion Protocol:** Elbow extended, forearm supinated. Examiner resists wrist flexion. Positive = medial epicondyle pain reproduction.

**Moving Valgus Stress Test (O'Driscoll et al. 2004):** Shoulder abducted 90°, elbow taken from 120° flexion to 70° extension while maintaining constant valgus stress. Positive = medial elbow pain between 120–70° (shear angle). High sensitivity/specificity for UCL injury in throwing athletes.

**Elbow Flexion Test:** Maximum elbow flexion with wrist neutral maintained for 60 seconds. Positive = tingling/numbness in ring/little finger distribution. Best test for cubital tunnel syndrome.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Urgent or time-sensitive pathology requiring escalation.

- **Medial epicondyle fracture** (particularly in children/adolescents — "Little Leaguer's elbow"): acute trauma, point tenderness, avulsion on X-ray → urgent X-ray + orthopaedic assessment
- **Medial epicondyle apophysitis** in skeletally immature athletes: growth plate disruption → X-ray; activity restriction
- **UCL rupture** with acute valgus trauma: significant instability, immediate swelling, inability to continue sport → MRI arthrogram + orthopaedic referral
- **Ulnar artery thrombosis** (hypothenar hammer syndrome): medial forearm pain, Raynaud-like symptoms, pulsatility changes → urgent vascular assessment
- **Septic olecranon bursitis or septic arthritis**: fever, systemic signs, erythema, warmth → emergency aspiration + bloods
- **Cubital tunnel syndrome with rapid neurological deterioration**: progressive weakness of intrinsic hand muscles, clawing → urgent neurophysiology + surgical review
- **Medial elbow malignancy**: rare; pain at rest, night pain, unexplained constitutional symptoms, age extremes → X-ray + referral

## Clinical Frameworks

### Tendinopathy Continuum Model (Cook & Purdam 2009)
Apply same staging as lateral epicondylalgia:
- **Reactive:** Acute load spike; manage with relative rest, isometrics, provocative load avoidance
- **Dysrepair:** Subacute; graduated isotonic loading; monitor 24-hour pain response
- **Degenerative:** Chronic; accept working pain (NRS 0–4); long-term loading programme; psychosocial screen

### Ulnar Nerve Assessment Framework
In all ME cases, systematically assess:

1. **Subjective:** Paraesthesia/numbness in ring/little finger; nocturnal symptoms (elbow flexion in sleep); pain radiating down medial forearm
2. **Objective:** Froment's sign (intrinsic weakness); first dorsal interosseous wasting; clawing (ring/little finger); sensory testing (light touch, 2-point discrimination) in ulnar nerve territory
3. **Special tests:** Elbow flexion test, Tinel's at cubital tunnel
4. **Severity classification:**
   - Mild: Intermittent paraesthesia; no motor deficit
   - Moderate: Persistent paraesthesia; early motor weakness
   - Severe: Constant symptoms; significant weakness/wasting; consider urgent surgical referral

### Valgus Extension Overload Assessment (throwing athletes)
- Screen for UCL insufficiency (moving valgus stress test)
- Screen for posterior impingement (valgus extension overload test)
- Screen for radiocapitellar pathology

## Outcome Measures

### Patient-Rated Tennis Elbow Evaluation (PRTEE)
- Although named for tennis elbow, validated for use in medial epicondylalgia
- **Score:** 0–100; 0 = no pain/disability
- **MCID:** 11 points

### DASH / QuickDASH
- **DASH MCID:** 10.2 points (LE/ME combined upper limb)
- **QuickDASH MCID:** 16 points
- Useful for occupational/functional tracking

### Grip Strength (Jamar Dynamometer)
- Protocol: Elbow at 90°; 3 trials; dominant vs non-dominant comparison
- **Pain-free grip** is more clinically relevant than maximum grip in ME
- Expect deficit on affected side; monitor improvement over 6–8 weeks

### Numeric Pain Rating Scale (NPRS)
- **MCID:** 2 points (0–10 scale) for musculoskeletal pain
- Assess: worst pain (last 7 days), average pain, pain during resisted testing

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**Diagnosis is primarily clinical** based on:
1. Medial elbow pain ± radiation into proximal forearm
2. Reproduction with resisted wrist flexion and/or forearm pronation
3. Medial epicondyle tenderness on palpation

**Ultrasound:**
- Sensitivity 72–90%; shows hypoechoic changes, tendon thickening, neovascularity at common flexor-pronator origin
- Power Doppler: neovascularisation correlates with symptom severity
- Indicated for diagnostic uncertainty, injection guidance, pre-surgical planning
- Can also assess UCL integrity (dynamic valgus stress view)

**MRI:**
- Gold standard for full characterisation: tendinopathy, partial tear, UCL injury, ulnar nerve oedema
- Indicated if UCL injury suspected, pre-surgical planning, diagnostic uncertainty
- MRI arthrogram superior for UCL assessment in throwing athletes

**NCS/EMG:**
- Indicated if ulnar neuropathy suspected (Elbow flexion test positive, Tinel's positive, motor deficit)
- Neurophysiology referral via GP or consultant (HSC NI: Belfast Trust, Southern Trust, Western Trust)
- NCS findings: reduced ulnar nerve conduction velocity across elbow (<50 m/s diagnostic); reduced compound motor action potential

**X-ray:**
- Not diagnostic for tendinopathy; may show medial epicondyle calcification
- Essential with trauma history to exclude fracture
- Valgus stress views for UCL assessment (comparative)

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features | Key Investigation |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Cubital tunnel syndrome | Paraesthesia in ring/little finger; positive elbow flexion test; motor deficit | NCS/EMG |
| UCL injury/instability | Valgus instability; throwing athlete; positive moving valgus stress test | MRI arthrogram |
| Medial epicondyle stress fracture | Young throwing athlete; X-ray/MRI changes | X-ray; MRI |
| Cervical radiculopathy C8/T1 | Neck pain; dermatomal pattern; positive Spurling's; reflex changes | Cervical X-ray; MRI cervical spine |
| Thoracic outlet syndrome | Positional symptoms; vascular signs; overhead provocation | Adson's; RROOS; vascular duplex |
| Pronator teres syndrome | Forearm pain; pronation-aggravated; tenderness distal to medial epicondyle; no elbow flexion test | Resisted pronation; NCS |
| Pancoast tumour | Progressive ulnar-distribution symptoms; shoulder pain; Horner's syndrome; smoker | Chest X-ray; CT chest |
| Medial plica irritation | Snapping sensation; no tendinopathy signs | MRI |
| Elbow OA | Diffuse pain; stiffness; crepitus; X-ray changes | X-ray |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Community Physiotherapy:**
- Majority managed in primary care MSK physiotherapy
- Trust self-referral where available; otherwise GP referral
- Occupational health input for work-related cases

**Hand Therapy:**
- Refer for complex cases, fabricated orthoses, functional rehabilitation post-injection or surgery
- Available via secondary care referral (Orthopaedics/Rheumatology/Hand Surgery)

**Neurophysiology:**
- Referral for NCS/EMG if ulnar neuropathy clinically suspected
- Via GP or outpatient consultant referral (Trust-specific waiting times; typically 6–16 weeks)

**Secondary Care:**
- Orthopaedic or Hand Surgery referral if: conservative failure at 6 months, UCL reconstruction consideration, surgical cubital tunnel decompression required
- Rheumatology if systemic inflammatory arthropathy suspected

---

### Conservative Management

#### Load Management (Grade A)
- Relative rest from provocative activity during acute phase
- Activity modification: avoid sustained wrist flexion under load; modify grip size/tool handles
- Wrist splint (neutral position) may reduce flexor-pronator load during acute phase

#### Exercise Programme (Grade B–A)
**Isometric Exercise (Phase 1 — Reactive):**
- Wrist flexion isometric holds: 5 × 45 seconds at 70% MVC
- Forearm pronation isometric holds
- Pain ≤5/10 acceptable; aim for immediate analgesic effect

**Eccentric Loading Programme (Phase 2 — Main Rehabilitation):**
- Eccentric wrist flexion over table edge (dumbbell/theraband): 3 × 15 reps × 2/day
- Eccentric forearm pronation with light resistance
- Progress load over 8–12 weeks; monitor 24-hour pain response
- Pain 0–4/10 during exercise is acceptable

**Progressive Resistance (Phase 3):**
- Wrist flexion, forearm pronation concentric-eccentric loading
- Grip strengthening (putty, hand exerciser)
- Elbow stability exercises (forearm plank, rotation under load)

#### Mobilisation (Grade B)
- Mulligan's Medial Glide + wrist flexion (adapted MWM): limited evidence but rationale similar to lateral epicondylalgia
- Neural mobilisation if ulnar nerve involvement identified (submaximal range; 3 × 10 reps)

#### Ergonomic Advice (Grade B)
- Wrist flexion avoidance; tool handle modification
- Sleep positioning (avoid elbow flexion >90° at night) — particularly important if ulnar neuropathy
- Elbow extension orthosis for sleep if cubital tunnel concurrent diagnosis

---

### Pharmacological

**Topical NSAIDs (Grade A):**
- Topical diclofenac gel — NICE CKS first-line recommendation
- 2–4× daily application; 4–6 week trial

**Oral NSAIDs (Grade B):**
- Short course (7–14 days) for acute presentations
- NICE: use lowest effective dose; prescribe gastroprotection if >7 days; avoid if renal impairment

**Corticosteroid Injection (Grade B — short-term):**
- Methylprednisolone 40 mg / triamcinolone 40 mg at common flexor-pronator origin
- Short-term pain relief (6–8 weeks) but no evidence of long-term benefit over physiotherapy
- Risk: ulnar nerve injury if poor technique (landmark or US-guided preferred)
- **Important:** Landmark injection at medial elbow carries risk of ulnar nerve damage; ultrasound guidance recommended
- Maximum 2 injections; minimum 6-week interval

**Nitric Oxide Patches:**
- Limited evidence for ME; extrapolated from Achilles/lateral elbow data
- GTN 1.25 mg/24h patch applied over medial epicondyle

---

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Refer to Orthopaedics/Hand Surgery if:**
- Failure of ≥6 months conservative management (structured physiotherapy)
- UCL insufficiency confirmed on MRI requiring reconstruction (particularly throwing athletes)
- Progressive ulnar neuropathy not responding to conservative management
- Medial epicondyle fracture or avulsion

**Surgical options (consultant-led):**
- Medial epicondyle debridement (open or arthroscopic)
- UCL reconstruction (Tommy John procedure) — throwing athletes
- Cubital tunnel decompression (concurrent ulnar neuropathy)
- Outcomes: 85–90% good/excellent at 2-year follow-up with surgical debridement
