## Overview & Pathophysiology

Lateral epicondylalgia (LE) — commonly termed "tennis elbow" — is the most prevalent elbow disorder in primary care, with a population prevalence of 1–3% and peak incidence in the 35–54 age group. It accounts for approximately 5% of all shoulder and arm complaints presenting to GPs, with an estimated annual incidence of 4–7 per 1000 patients.

**Pathophysiology:** Contemporary understanding has shifted decisively away from an inflammatory model. Histological studies (Nirschl & Pettrone 1979; Khan et al. 2002) consistently demonstrate angiofibroblastic hyperplasia rather than inflammatory infiltrate — absence of inflammatory cells, disordered collagen (Type III predominance), neurovascular in-growth, and failed healing response. The condition is therefore better classified as a **tendinopathy** rather than tendinitis.

**Primary structure involved:** Extensor carpi radialis brevis (ECRB) origin at the lateral epicondyle — specifically the anterior and superior aspect. ECRB is most vulnerable due to:
- Mechanical impingement against the capitellum during elbow extension and forearm pronation
- High tensile load during wrist extension under load
- Relatively avascular region at the origin contributing to failed healing

**Contributing structures:** Extensor digitorum communis, extensor carpi radialis longus, extensor carpi ulnaris.

**Risk factors:** Manual occupations, repetitive gripping >2 kg, keyboard/mouse use, racquet sports (backhand technique errors), age 40–50, smoking, obesity, previous episode.

**Natural history:** Spontaneous resolution expected in 80–90% within 12–18 months without treatment. However, recurrence rates are high (up to 72% at 5 years in some cohorts).

|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| Cozen's Test (resisted wrist extension) | 0.84 | 0.75 | 3.36 | 0.21 | Moderate |
| Mill's Test (passive wrist flexion, elbow extended) | 0.68 | 0.69 | 2.19 | 0.46 | Moderate |
| Maudsley's Test (resisted middle finger extension) | 0.88 | 0.58 | 2.10 | 0.21 | Low-Moderate |
| Lateral epicondyle palpation tenderness | 0.91 | 0.45 | 1.65 | 0.20 | Moderate |
| Resisted wrist extension with elbow extended (loaded) | 0.79 | 0.83 | 4.65 | 0.25 | Moderate |
| Grip strength deficit (affected vs unaffected) | 0.73 | 0.68 | 2.28 | 0.40 | Low |
| Chair pick-up test (patient version Cozen's) | 0.72 | 0.74 | 2.77 | 0.38 | Low |

**Cozen's Test Protocol:** Stabilise the elbow; patient makes fist with wrist in neutral. Examiner resists wrist extension. Positive = pain at lateral epicondyle.

**Mill's Test Protocol:** Elbow extended, forearm pronated. Examiner passively flexes wrist fully. Positive = pain at lateral epicondyle.

**Maudsley's Test Protocol:** Patient resists examiner pressing down on middle finger with elbow extended. Positive = lateral epicondyle pain.

**Clinical note:** No single test is diagnostic in isolation. A cluster of positive tests (≥2 of: lateral epicondyle tenderness, positive Cozen's, positive Mill's) increases diagnostic confidence. Exclude radial nerve compression (radial tunnel syndrome) as concurrent or alternative diagnosis.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ The following presentations require urgent assessment and should not be attributed to simple tendinopathy.

- **Bony tenderness directly over lateral epicondyle with trauma history** → suspect lateral epicondyle avulsion/fracture; request X-ray
- **Elbow effusion, erythema, warmth, systemic fever** → suspect septic arthritis or crystal arthropathy; urgent bloods + aspiration
- **Progressive neurological deficit** (weakness, sensory loss in radial nerve territory) → suspect posterior interosseous nerve entrapment or cervical radiculopathy; urgent imaging
- **Night pain, unexplained weight loss, age >50 with new-onset lateral elbow pain** → consider malignancy; urgent X-ray + referral
- **Rapidly progressive elbow stiffness** → consider aggressive articular pathology, tumour, or infection
- **Vascular compromise** (pulselessness, pallor, paraesthesia, pain, paralysis) → emergency vascular referral

## Clinical Frameworks

### Tendinopathy Continuum Model (Cook & Purdam 2009; updated Cook et al. 2016)

The continuum model provides a framework for staging tendinopathy and guiding management:

| Stage | Pathology | Clinical Features | Management Approach |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Reactive tendinopathy** | Short-term adaptive response; increased proteoglycan, water content | Acute onset after sudden load increase; swelling possible; pain with all load | Load reduction; relative rest; isometrics may reduce pain |
| **Tendon dysrepair** | Failed healing; early matrix disruption; neurovascular in-growth | Sub-acute; pain with higher loads; some thickening on US | Moderate load modification; graduated isotonic loading |
| **Degenerative tendinopathy** | Cell death, matrix disruption, failed collagen; angiofibroblastic changes | Chronic; pain with daily activities; may have pain-free window mid-activity | Graded loading; accept some pain during exercise (NRS 0–4); longer rehab timeline |

### Pain Monitoring Model
- **0–4/10 NRS during loading** — acceptable; continue programme
- **5–7/10 NRS** — monitor closely; modify load
- **>7/10 NRS or pain lingering >24 hours post-exercise** — reduce load; reassess stage

### Biopsychosocial Considerations
Central sensitisation is prevalent in chronic LE (>3 months). Assess:
- Pain catastrophising (PCS >30 → elevated risk of poor outcome)
- Fear-avoidance (FABQ)
- Work-related psychosocial factors (job demands, control, support)
- Sleep quality

## Outcome Measures

### Patient-Rated Tennis Elbow Evaluation (PRTEE)
- **Format:** 15 items; 0–100 scale (10 pain items + 5 function specific + 10 usual activities)
- **Score interpretation:** 0 = no pain/disability; 100 = worst possible
- **MCID:** 11 points (Stratford et al. 2001)
- **MDC:** 17 points
- **Time to complete:** 5 minutes

### DASH / QuickDASH
- **DASH:** 30 items; 0–100 (0 = no disability)
- **QuickDASH:** 11 items; 0–100
- **MCID for LE:** DASH 10.2 points; QuickDASH 16 points
- **Limitation:** Less condition-specific; useful for cross-condition comparison

### Grip Strength (Jamar Dynamometer)
- **Protocol:** Elbow at 90°, neutral forearm; 3 trials; mean recorded
- **Normative comparison:** Compare to contralateral limb (expect 5–10% dominant hand advantage)
- **Pain-free grip strength** often more clinically useful than maximum grip
- **MCID:** Approximately 6.5 kg for meaningful change
- **Reliability:** ICC 0.97–0.98 for Jamar at standardised handle position

### Global Rating of Change (GRC)
- 15-point scale (−7 to +7); clinically meaningful change = ±3 points

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**Clinical diagnosis** is appropriate in typical presentations. Imaging is not routinely required for initial diagnosis.

**Ultrasound (US):**
- Sensitivity 64–82%; specificity 64–100% for tendinopathic changes (hypoechogenicity, thickening, neovascularity)
- Useful to confirm diagnosis in atypical cases, guide injection, rule out partial/full-thickness tear
- Power Doppler demonstrates neovascularisation but correlation with symptoms is inconsistent
- **Indication:** Persistent symptoms >6 months despite conservative management, prior to injection, surgical planning

**MRI:**
- Higher soft tissue resolution than US; gold standard for detecting intra-tendinous tears
- T2 signal increase at ECRB origin diagnostic of tendinopathy
- Reserve for surgical planning or diagnostic uncertainty
- Not routinely indicated in primary/community physiotherapy

**X-ray:**
- Not required for diagnosis; may show calcification (25–30% of chronic cases) or reveal alternative pathology
- Indicated with trauma history or red flags

**NCS/EMG:**
- Not indicated for lateral epicondylalgia
- Consider if radial tunnel syndrome or posterior interosseous nerve palsy suspected

**Clinical Prediction Rule:** No validated CPR exists for LE. Diagnosis is clinical based on:
1. Lateral epicondyle pain
2. Reproduced by resisted wrist extension and/or passive wrist flexion
3. Tenderness on palpation of the lateral epicondyle

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features | Key Test |
|-----------|------------------------|----------|
| Radial tunnel syndrome | Pain 3–5 cm distal to lateral epicondyle; deep ache; nocturnal pain; weakness; positive resisted supination | Resisted supination at 0°; Frohse arcade tenderness |
| Posterior interosseous nerve palsy | Finger/wrist drop; weakness of finger extensors; may be painless | Neurological examination; NCS/EMG |
| Cervical radiculopathy (C6/C7) | Neck pain; dermatomal sensory loss; reduced reflexes; Spurling's positive | Spurling's test; cervical spine examination |
| Lateral elbow instability (PLRI) | Clicking, locking, giving way; trauma history; positive lateral pivot shift | Lateral pivot shift test; MRI |
| Synovial plica irritation | Intermittent snapping sensation; pain with full extension | Clinical; MRI |
| Elbow OA | Age >50; stiffness; crepitus; full ROM loss; X-ray changes | X-ray |
| Osteochondral loose body | Locking, catching, extension block | X-ray; MRI |
| Referred pain from shoulder/neck | Absence of local signs; pain not reproduced by local provocation | Full UL neurodynamic screen |
| Lateral epicondyle stress fracture | Rare; athletes; local bony tenderness; positive imaging | X-ray; MRI |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care / Community Physiotherapy:**
- First-line management; majority should be managed in community MSK services
- GP referral to community physiotherapy or self-referral where available (Trust-dependent)
- Occupational health physiotherapy for work-related cases
- Hand therapy services: referral via GP or hospital consultant; appropriate for complex/chronic cases

**Secondary Care:**
- Consultant referral (Orthopaedics or Rheumatology) if:
  - Failed 6 months conservative management
  - Diagnostic uncertainty
  - Consideration of injection therapy (PRP, autologous blood, corticosteroid)
  - Surgical assessment
- Nerve conduction studies: available via Neurophysiology (Belfast Trust, Southern Trust); referral via GP or consultant; waitlist variable

**Regional variation:** Check individual Trust physiotherapy department protocols; self-referral available in some Trusts (Western, Southern). BHSCT community MSK direct access available.

### Conservative Management

#### Load Management (Grade A evidence)
- **Acute phase:** Relative rest from provocative activities; avoid sustained gripping >2 kg; modify keyboard/mouse ergonomics
- **Wrist splinting:** Cockup wrist splint (20–30° wrist extension) reduces ECRB load; evidence for symptom reduction in acute phase (moderate); not recommended long-term
- **Counterforce brace (epicondyle strap):** Moderate evidence for short-term pain reduction with activity; mechanism = force dampening at muscle belly

#### Exercise Therapy (Grade A evidence — strongest recommendation)
**Eccentric Exercise Programme (Svernlöv & Adolfsson 2001; Tyler et al. 2010):**
- Wrist extension eccentrics with forearm pronated over table edge
- 3 × 15 repetitions; 2× daily; 12-week programme
- Progress via increase in load (dumbbell/putty/theraband)
- Pain up to 5/10 acceptable

**Isometric Exercise (Stasinopoulos et al. 2017; Rio et al. 2015):**
- Wrist extension isometric holds: 5 × 45-second holds at 70% MVC
- Analgesic effect demonstrated; useful in reactive/irritable tendinopathy
- Can precede isotonic loading programme

**Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) Protocol (Beyer et al. 2015 — adapted for LE):**
- Wrist extension with load: 3 sets; 15 reps → 8 reps → 6 reps over 12 weeks
- Load progressed to near-maximum effort
- Shown superior to eccentric-only in Achilles; extrapolated to upper limb

**Elbow Manipulation + Exercise (Bisset et al. 2006 — landmark RCT):**
- Mulligan's Mobilisation with Movement (MWM): lateral glide of elbow combined with gripping
- Combined with exercise showed superior outcomes to corticosteroid injection at 12 months
- 8 sessions over 8 weeks; pain-free grip force primary outcome

#### Ergonomic Advice (Grade B)
- Keyboard positioning; mouse size; tool grip modification
- Avoiding repetitive wrist extension under load
- Occupational therapy assessment if work-related

### Pharmacological

**Topical NSAIDs (Grade A):**
- Topical diclofenac gel — NICE recommended first-line; good local effect; avoids systemic side effects
- Apply 2–4× daily to lateral epicondyle region; 4–6 week trial

**Oral NSAIDs (Grade B):**
- Short-term (1–2 weeks) for acute flares
- NICE: lowest effective dose; consider GI protection if >7 days
- Evidence limited for chronic tendinopathy

**Corticosteroid Injection (CSI):**
- Short-term pain relief (6–8 weeks) — Grade A
- **Long-term outcomes inferior** to physiotherapy and wait-and-see at 12 months (Bisset et al. 2006 — high quality RCT)
- Risk of recurrence and tendon weakening with repeat injections
- Consider: single injection for severe acute presentation to enable rehabilitation engagement
- **NICE CKS guidance:** Advise patients of short-term benefit but inferior long-term outcome vs exercise

**Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP):**
- Emerging evidence; some RCTs show benefit over placebo/corticosteroid at 6–12 months
- Not currently commissioned within HSC NI (patient-funded in private sector)
- Systematic review (Arirachakaran et al. 2016) shows possible superiority over CSI at 6+ months
- Insufficient evidence to recommend routinely

**Autologous Blood Injection:**
- Similar rationale to PRP; limited high-quality RCT evidence
- Not routinely commissioned

**Nitric Oxide Patches (GTN):**
- Topical glyceryl trinitrate patches 1.25 mg/24h over lateral epicondyle
- Paoloni et al. (2003) RCT showed significant improvement vs placebo
- Poor palatability (headache side effect); limited uptake in clinical practice

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Refer to Orthopaedics/Hand Surgery if:**
- Symptoms persisting >12 months despite optimal conservative management (minimum 6 months of structured physiotherapy)
- Significant functional impairment affecting work or daily activities
- Evidence of structural tear on imaging requiring surgical consideration
- Consideration for PRP, autologous blood injection, or dry needling under image guidance

**Surgical options (consultant-led decision):**
- Open ECRB debridement (Nirschl technique) — Gold standard; 85–90% good/excellent outcomes
- Arthroscopic debridement — Equivalent outcomes to open; shorter recovery
- Percutaneous tenotomy — Less invasive option
- Ultrasound-guided percutaneous needle tenotomy (TENEX/Tenex Health TX) — emerging

**Criteria for surgical consideration (NICE / BOA guidance):**
- Failed ≥6 months conservative management including physiotherapy
- Documented tendinopathic changes on US or MRI
- Significant functional limitation
