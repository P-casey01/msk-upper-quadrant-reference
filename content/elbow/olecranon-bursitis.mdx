## Overview & Pathophysiology

Olecranon bursitis is inflammation of the olecranon bursa — a superficial synovial-lined sac located between the olecranon process of the ulna and the overlying skin. It is the most common bursitis of the elbow and one of the most frequently encountered superficial bursitides in clinical practice.

**Anatomy:** The olecranon bursa sits subcutaneously over the posterior olecranon. It does not normally communicate with the elbow joint. Normal bursa contains negligible fluid; in pathological states may enlarge significantly (2–3 cm or greater).

**Prevalence:** Exact epidemiology uncertain; more common in males and in manual occupations involving prolonged elbow contact surfaces. Gout, pseudogout, and rheumatoid arthritis are systemic risk factors.

**Classification by aetiology:**

| Type | Mechanism | Features |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Traumatic** | Direct blow (acute) or repetitive pressure (chronic) | Most common; clear history |
| **Septic** | Bacterial infection (usually Staph. aureus 80%) | Erythema, warmth, fever, elevated WBC |
| **Crystal-induced** | Gout (uric acid) or pseudogout (CPPD) | Aspirate shows crystals; may be recurrent |
| **Inflammatory** | Rheumatoid arthritis, other spondyloarthropathies | Bilateral; systemic features; rheumatoid nodules |
| **Idiopathic** | No identifiable cause | Diagnosis of exclusion |

**Occupational associations:** "Student's elbow," "miner's elbow," "plumber's elbow" — prolonged pressure on elbows during work, studying, or sports (wrestling, hockey goalies).

**Pathophysiology:** Trauma or sustained pressure causes microhaemorrhage within the bursal walls → inflammatory response → synovial hypertrophy → increased fluid production → bursal enlargement. In septic bursitis, bacteria enter via skin abrasion, puncture wound, or haematogenous spread. Gout deposits urate crystals which trigger intense inflammatory response.

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|
| Bursal fluctuance on palpation (fluid wave) | 0.85 | 0.72 | 3.04 | 0.21 | Moderate |
| Bursal erythema extending beyond bursa | 0.72 | 0.82 | 4.00 | 0.34 | Moderate (for septic) |
| Skin abrasion/puncture wound at olecranon | 0.68 | 0.90 | 6.80 | 0.36 | Moderate (for septic) |
| Fever (>38°C) | 0.40 | 0.97 | 13.3 | 0.62 | Moderate (for septic) |
| Tenderness of bursal wall (not just fluid) | 0.78 | 0.65 | 2.23 | 0.34 | Low-Moderate |
| Elbow ROM preserved (distinguishes from joint pathology) | 0.88 | 0.70 | 2.93 | 0.17 | Moderate |

**Key Distinction — Septic vs Non-Septic:**
No clinical sign reliably discriminates septic from non-septic olecranon bursitis. The gold standard is **bursal fluid aspiration and analysis**.

**Aspiration findings:**
| Parameter | Non-Septic | Septic |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| WBC/mm³ | <2,000 | >2,000 (often >50,000) |
| % Neutrophils | <25% | >75% |
| Glucose | Normal | Decreased |
| Gram stain | Negative | Positive in 50–70% |
| Culture | Negative | Positive in 80–90% |
| Crystals | Absent (unless crystal-induced) | Absent (unless concurrent) |

**Elbow ROM:** Should be full and pain-free in isolated olecranon bursitis. Restricted ROM suggests intra-articular pathology.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ Septic olecranon bursitis is a medical emergency requiring prompt diagnosis and treatment.

- **Signs of septic bursitis** (ANY combination): fever, overlying cellulitis, rapid enlargement, warmth significantly greater than contralateral, skin breakdown/wound, haematogenous source (e.g., IVDU, recent dental procedure, skin infection) → **urgent aspiration + bloods; admit if systemically unwell**
- **MRSA risk factors:** Healthcare workers, previous MRSA, institutional care, IVDU → adjust antibiotic choice accordingly
- **Rapid elbow joint involvement:** Loss of ROM, joint line tenderness, joint effusion → suspect septic arthritis (medical emergency; mortality if untreated) → **emergency joint aspiration**
- **Immunocompromised patients** (HIV, diabetes, steroid use, immunosuppressants): higher risk of septic bursitis; lower threshold for aspiration and admission
- **Diabetic patients:** More aggressive clinical course; higher risk of complications; lower threshold for IV antibiotics
- **Bursal sinus formation:** Chronically infected bursa may spontaneously drain; requires surgical excision
- **Systemic features of gout flare:** Consider concurrent gout arthropathy; serum urate; joint aspiration

## Clinical Frameworks

### Diagnostic Algorithm for Olecranon Bursitis

```
Posterior elbow swelling
        ↓
Is elbow ROM preserved?
   ├── NO → Consider joint pathology (OA, inflammatory arthritis, septic arthritis) → Joint aspiration
   └── YES → Isolated bursal pathology likely
              ↓
         Risk factors for septic bursitis?
         (fever, erythema, skin wound, immunocompromise, rapid onset)
            ├── YES → Aspirate bursa → send MC&S, crystals, WBC, glucose
            │         If WBC >2000 or clinical suspicion → treat as septic (antibiotics)
            └── NO → Likely traumatic/chronic mechanical
                      → Conservative management
                      → Aspirate if: diagnostic uncertainty, large painful bursa, or no improvement at 4 weeks
```

### Septic Bursitis Management Framework
- **Mild/moderate** (no systemic sepsis, immunocompetent, outpatient): oral flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS × 10–14 days; repeat aspiration at 3–5 days
- **Severe** (systemically unwell, rapid progression, immunocompromised, failed oral antibiotics): IV antibiotics + surgical drainage or excision
- **MRSA confirmed/suspected:** Vancomycin IV or oral clindamycin; infectious disease advice

## Outcome Measures

### Primary Clinical Measures
- **Bursal size:** Measure maximum diameter with tape measure or ultrasound at baseline and follow-up
- **NPRS (Numeric Pain Rating Scale):** 0–10; MCID = 2 points; assess posterior elbow pain
- **Elbow ROM:** Flexion/extension/pronation/supination (goniometry); should remain full in isolated bursitis

### DASH / QuickDASH
- Useful for functional impact assessment
- MCID for upper limb: DASH 10.2; QuickDASH 16 points
- Less specific to olecranon bursitis but useful in occupational contexts

### Patient Satisfaction
- Global Rating of Change (GRC) scale at 4, 8, and 12 weeks post-intervention
- Return to work/sport as functional endpoint

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

**Clinical diagnosis** is reliable in typical traumatic cases with characteristic swelling over the olecranon and preserved elbow ROM.

**Aspiration:**
- Recommended if: septic bursitis suspected, diagnostic uncertainty, crystal arthropathy queried, large painful bursa limiting function
- Ultrasound-guided aspiration preferred for complete drainage; reduces recurrence vs landmark aspiration
- Send aspirate for: MC&S (septic), crystal analysis (gout/pseudogout), WBC + differential

**Ultrasound:**
- Confirms bursal fluid, size, wall thickening, septation
- Doppler: hyperaemia in septic/inflammatory bursitis
- Guides aspiration and injection
- Identifies concurrent triceps tendon pathology

**X-ray:**
- Not required for diagnosis; may show:
  - Olecranon spur (associated with chronic bursitis)
  - Gout tophi (soft tissue calcification)
  - Fracture (traumatic)
- Indicated with trauma or concern for bony pathology

**MRI:**
- Reserved for diagnostic uncertainty, surgical planning
- Distinguishes bursitis from triceps tendon pathology, tumour
- Not routine in straightforward cases

**Bloods (if septic bursitis suspected):**
- FBC, CRP, ESR, urea/electrolytes
- Blood cultures if febrile
- Serum urate (gout screen — note: may be normal during acute flare)
- Rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP if inflammatory arthropathy suspected

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features | Key Investigation |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Septic arthritis of elbow | ROM restricted/painful; fever; systemically unwell; joint effusion | Joint aspiration; bloods |
| Gout/crystal arthropathy | Very painful; acute onset; hyperuricaemia; crystals on aspiration | Serum urate; aspiration |
| Rheumatoid arthritis | Bilateral; rheumatoid nodules; systemic features; joint involvement | Rheumatoid factor; anti-CCP; X-ray |
| Psoriatic arthritis | Psoriasis; dactylitis; enthesopathy; asymmetric joint involvement | Clinical; X-ray |
| Triceps tendon tear | Posterior elbow pain with resisted extension weakness; palpable defect | MRI; ultrasound |
| Posterior elbow impingement | Pain at end-range extension; bone spur; athletes | X-ray; MRI |
| Lipoma | Soft, non-tender, no ROM loss, chronic | Ultrasound; MRI |
| Ganglion cyst | Tense, translucent, may transilluminate | Ultrasound |
| Tumour (synovial sarcoma) | Rare; firm; progressive; systemic features | MRI; biopsy |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Community Physiotherapy / GP:**
- Traumatic/chronic non-septic bursitis: managed in community
- GP: aspiration, oral antibiotics (septic), referral decision
- Physiotherapy: activity modification, compression, ergonomic advice

**Emergency Department / Acute Medical:**
- Systemically unwell septic bursitis → A&E assessment
- IV antibiotics + surgical opinion if not responding to oral treatment within 48–72 hours

**Secondary Care — Orthopaedics/Hand Surgery:**
- Recurrent bursitis (≥2 episodes requiring aspiration) → consider surgical bursectomy
- Chronically infected bursa with sinus formation → surgical excision
- Confirmed septic bursitis not responding to antibiotics → surgical washout/excision
- Referral via GP or Emergency Department

**Rheumatology:**
- Crystal-induced bursitis — consider referral for urate-lowering therapy (gout) or systemic disease management
- Inflammatory arthropathy causing bursitis

---

### Conservative Management

#### Non-Septic Traumatic/Mechanical Bursitis (Grade B)

**Load Modification:**
- Avoid direct pressure on olecranon; protective elbow padding (neoprene sleeve, foam pad)
- Avoid prolonged elbow weight-bearing
- Avoid contact sports until resolved

**Compression:**
- Compression bandage or tubigrip reduces re-accumulation of fluid
- Evidence: moderate; reduces swelling recurrence vs no compression
- Elbow compression sleeve (sport-specific) for return to activity

**Ice Therapy:**
- 15–20 minutes, 3–4× daily; reduces acute inflammatory swelling
- No high-quality RCT evidence but widely recommended and safe

**Aspiration (GP/Secondary Care):**
- If large, painful, or limiting function: aspiration provides symptom relief
- Recurrence common (50–70% without compression); aspiration + compression dressing reduces recurrence
- Ultrasound-guided aspiration superior for complete drainage

**Corticosteroid Injection (into bursa — non-septic only):**
- Reduces recurrence vs aspiration alone in non-septic bursitis (Weinstein et al. 1984)
- Methylprednisolone 40 mg or triamcinolone 40 mg
- Risk: skin atrophy, depigmentation, bursal sinus, infection → **exclude septic bursitis before injecting**
- Maximum 2 injections

---

### Pharmacological

**NSAIDs (oral or topical — non-septic):**
- Short course oral NSAIDs for acute traumatic bursitis
- Indomethacin particularly effective for crystal-induced flares
- Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel) for localised pain control

**Antibiotics (septic bursitis — NICE/BNF aligned):**
- **First-line (non-MRSA):** Flucloxacillin 500 mg QDS orally × 10–14 days
- **Penicillin allergy:** Clarithromycin 500 mg BD or clindamycin 300–450 mg QDS
- **MRSA confirmed:** Vancomycin IV (weight-adjusted); seek microbiologist/infectious disease advice
- **IV therapy** if: systemically unwell, failed oral therapy, immunocompromised, deep infection, MRSA

**Gout Management:**
- Acute flare: colchicine 500 mcg BD–TDS (NICE preferred for olecranon gout) or oral NSAIDs; short-course prednisolone if contraindicated
- Long-term urate-lowering therapy (allopurinol): if ≥2 gout episodes per year; commence after acute flare resolves
- Rheumatology referral for complex/refractory gout

---

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Refer to Orthopaedics/Hand Surgery if:**
- Recurrent non-septic bursitis (≥3 episodes)
- Failed conservative management including aspiration × 2
- Chronic infected bursa / sinus formation
- Diagnostic uncertainty (solid lesion, tumour, extensive inflammation)
- Septic bursitis not responding to 48–72 hours of appropriate antibiotics

**Surgical options:**
- **Bursectomy:** Excision of bursa ± skin graft if skin contracted; 85–90% resolution; wound healing complications in 10–20%
- **Arthroscopic bursectomy:** Less common; smaller wounds; suitable for recurrent non-septic
- **Surgical drainage/debridement:** For infected bursa with sinus

**Post-surgical:** Physiotherapy for wound management, ROM restoration, scar management, compression to prevent recurrence. Typically 6–8 weeks before return to manual work.
