## Overview & Pathophysiology

Shoulder instability refers to symptomatic excessive translation of the humeral head relative to the glenoid, ranging from subtle microinstability to recurrent dislocation. It is the most common joint dislocation, accounting for approximately 45% of all dislocations presenting to emergency departments. It represents a significant clinical challenge due to its heterogeneous aetiology, high recurrence rates, and impact on young, active populations.

**Prevalence / Incidence:**
- Annual incidence of primary glenohumeral dislocation: 11–27 per 100,000
- Peak incidence: males 20–30 years (contact sport); females 20–50 years (atraumatic)
- Recurrence rate after first dislocation: 55–97% in those <20 years; 10–40% in those >40 years
- Recurrence is the dominant clinical concern in young athletes

### Classification (TUBS / AMBRI / AIOS)

**TUBS (Traumatic Unilateral Bankart Surgery):**
- Traumatic onset (single event)
- Unilateral
- Bankart lesion (anteroinferior labral avulsion)
- Surgery often required for high recurrence risk

**AMBRI (Atraumatic Multidirectional Bilateral Rehabilitation):**
- Atraumatic onset (no single traumatic event)
- Multidirectional laxity (inferior ± anterior ± posterior)
- Bilateral (often)
- Rehabilitation primary treatment
- Inferior capsular shift if surgery required

**AIOS (Acquired Instability from Overuse in Sport):**
- Overhead throwing athletes
- Acquired internal impingement; microinstability
- Direction-specific; sport-related

**Stanmore Classification (Rowe & Zarins / Revised Bayley et al.):**
Three-point triangle:
- Type I: Structural (traumatic — Bankart, Hill-Sachs)
- Type II: Atraumatic structural (involuntary; dysplastic glenoid)
- Type III: Muscle patterning (non-structural; habitual; voluntary)

### Relevant Anatomy and Stabilisers

**Static stabilisers:**
- **Glenoid labrum:** Deepens glenoid cavity by ~50%; increases contact area; functions as chock-block for humeral head
- **Glenohumeral ligaments (GHL):**
  - Superior GHL: resists inferior translation in neutral and adduction
  - Middle GHL: resists anterior translation at 45° abduction
  - Inferior GHL (IGHL): primary restraint at 90° abduction — most important ligament
  - IGHL anterior band: resists anterior translation with ER
  - IGHL posterior band: resists posterior translation with IR
- **Rotator cuff interval:** Coracohumeral + superior GHL; resists inferior translation
- **Bony glenoid:** Pear-shaped; inverted glenoid version (>15° retroversion = risk for posterior instability)

**Dynamic stabilisers:**
- **Rotator cuff:** Compression into glenoid; concavity-compression mechanism
- **Long head of biceps:** Superior glenohumeral stability
- **Scapular rotators:** Position glenoid face; scapular dyskinesis → decreased stability

**Common Structural Lesions:**
| Lesion | Description | Direction | Imaging |
|--------|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Bankart lesion | Anteroinferior labral avulsion from glenoid | Anterior | MR arthrogram |
| Bony Bankart | Bankart + glenoid bone loss (>20–25% = critical) | Anterior | CT (glenoid morphology) |
| Hill-Sachs defect | Posterolateral humeral head impaction fracture | Anterior | CT; MRI |
| HAGL (Humeral Avulsion GHL) | GHL avulsion from humerus (not labrum) | Anterior | MR arthrogram |
| Reverse Bankart | Posteroinferior labral tear | Posterior | MR arthrogram |
| Reverse Hill-Sachs | Anteromedial humeral head (posterior dislocation) | Posterior | CT; MRI |
| SLAP tear | Superior labrum anterior-posterior | — | MR arthrogram |

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Acute dislocation with inability to reduce | Locked dislocation; fracture | Emergency Department; X-ray before reduction |
| Post-reduction neurological deficit | Axillary nerve injury (most common); brachial plexus | Deltoid sensation/function; EMG at 4–6 weeks |
| Anterior shoulder pulsatile mass | Axillary artery injury | Vascular surgery — Emergency |
| Posterior shoulder with wrist drop post-trauma | Radial nerve injury (rare) | Urgent neurology |
| Instability in older patient (>40 with trauma) | Associated full-thickness RC tear (60% in >40 years post-dislocation) | USS/MRI post-reduction |
| First dislocation >50 years | Much higher RC tear association; lower recurrence | USS/MRI; managed differently |
| Voluntary habitual dislocation | Type III Stanmore — muscle patterning | Specialist shoulder unit; psychiatric assessment if needed |
| Progressive instability without trauma | Tumour; infection; Charcot arthropathy | MRI; investigations |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Instability Severity Index Score (ISIS — Balg & Boileau, 2007)
Pre-operative scoring tool for predicting recurrence after Latarjet vs Bankart repair:

| Factor | Score |
|--------|-------|
| Age ≤20 | 2 pts |
| Competitive sport | 2 pts |
| Contact/overhead sport | 1 pt |
| Hyperlaxity (anterior apprehension <60° ER or sulcus >2 cm) | 1 pt |
| Hill-Sachs on AP X-ray | 2 pts |
| Loss of sclerotic inferior glenoid contour on AP X-ray | 2 pts |

- Score ≤3: Low recurrence risk — arthroscopic Bankart suitable
- Score ≥4: High recurrence risk — Latarjet procedure recommended
- Score ≥6: Very high risk — Latarjet mandatory

### FEDS Classification (Functional, Emotion, Direction, Severity)
Modern classification for MDI — integrates structural, neuromuscular, and psychological components.

### Rehabilitation Framework for Instability (Wilk & Macrina, 2012)

**Phase 1 — Acute protection (0–4 weeks):**
- Sling immobilisation (IR position — debate ongoing; ER immobilisation: reduced recurrence in young males)
- Pain management; rotator cuff isometrics; elbow/wrist AROM
- Early scapular control: retraction; no provocative positions

**Phase 2 — Intermediate (4–8 weeks):**
- Progressive ER; RC strengthening; scapular stabilisers
- Proprioception training (joint position sense)
- Activity modification — avoid apprehension position (90°ABD/ER)

**Phase 3 — Advanced strengthening (8–16 weeks):**
- Progressive loading; functional patterns; perturbation training
- Sport-specific preparation

**Phase 4 — Return to sport (4–6 months):**
- Graduated sport return; sport-specific exercises
- Bracing consideration for contact sports

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Imaging

**X-ray (first-line — mandatory after acute dislocation):**
- AP: Hill-Sachs; glenoid erosion; fracture
- Axillary lateral: confirms anterior/posterior direction; glenoid morphology
- Stryker Notch view: Hill-Sachs optimised
- West Point view: anterior glenoid rim (Bankart bony lesion)

**CT (glenoid bone loss assessment):**
- Mandated when bony Bankart or significant glenoid loss suspected
- Critical threshold: >20–25% glenoid bone loss = Latarjet preferred over Bankart
- CT arthrogram: bone loss + labral assessment combined

**MRI arthrogram (definitive soft tissue assessment):**
- Sensitivity for Bankart: 85–91%; Specificity: 91–99% (Waldt et al., 2005)
- Sensitivity HAGL: MR arthrogram >> standard MRI
- Preferred over standard MRI for labral tears (gadolinium distension)
- Identifies: Bankart; HAGL; SLAP; Hill-Sachs; rotator cuff; posterior lesions

**USS:**
- Limited for instability; cannot adequately assess labrum
- Useful adjunct for associated RC pathology (especially in >40 years post-dislocation)

### Key Evidence

- **Itoi et al. (2007) JBJS:** ER immobilisation after primary dislocation reduces recurrence in young males (26% vs 42% at 2 years) — but difficult to sustain compliance
- **Balg & Boileau (2007):** ISIS score — validated prediction of recurrence and surgical decision-making
- **Burkhart & De Beer (2000):** Glenoid bone loss >25% = "engaging" Hill-Sachs; Bankart repair fails; Latarjet required
- **Bessière et al. (2014):** Latarjet superior recurrence rate vs arthroscopic Bankart in high-risk patients

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Features | Differentiation |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Rotator cuff tear (post-dislocation) | Weakness; lag signs; >40 years | USS/MRI post-reduction |
| Acromioclavicular dislocation | Direct impact; AC step deformity | X-ray; AC palpation |
| Multidirectional laxity without instability | Asymptomatic hypermobility | Beighton score; symptom correlation |
| SLAP tear | Overhead athlete; mechanical symptoms | MR arthrogram; special tests |
| SAPS/rotator cuff tendinopathy | Painful arc; no apprehension | Apprehension test negative |
| Cervical radiculopathy | Neck; neurological signs; referred | Cervical exam; ULTT |
| Brachial neuritis | Post-viral; acute severe pain; wasting | EMG; MRI |
| Glenohumeral OA | Older; crepitus; X-ray changes | X-ray |
| Posterior shoulder tightness (GIRD) | Overhead athlete; IR deficit | IR ROM measurement |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Emergency/Acute Dislocation:**
- ED presentation: X-ray confirmation; reduction (Cunningham/Milch/Kocher techniques)
- Post-reduction X-ray mandatory
- Neurovascular assessment (axillary nerve — deltoid sensation and function)
- Sling 1–3 weeks; GP/physio follow-up arranged from ED
- Bloods not required routinely; consider in elderly or atypical

**Primary Care:**
- Post-reduction follow-up: assess neurovascular; arrange physiotherapy
- Young patient first dislocation: refer early to orthopaedic shoulder specialist (especially <25 years)
- USS/MRI if >40 years post-dislocation (high RC tear association)
- MSK self-referral or GP physio referral for rehabilitation

**Orthopaedic Pathway (HSC NI):**
- Referral: first dislocation age <25; recurrent dislocation (≥2 episodes); failed conservative management
- Specialist shoulder units: Musgrave Park Hospital (Belfast); Altnagelvin (Western Trust); Antrim/Ulster Hospitals
- Waiting times: trauma/acute orthopaedic immediate; routine 12–26+ months (post-COVID backlogs 2024–2025)

### Conservative Management

**Structured Rehabilitation (Evidence Grade B–A):**

| Phase | Duration | Focus | Key Exercises |
|-------|----------|-------|---------------|
| Acute protection | 0–4 weeks | Sling; pain control; early activation | Pendulum; elbow/wrist AROM; isometrics |
| Activation | 4–8 weeks | RC activation; scapular control | Side-lying ER; scapular retraction; prone Y/T/W |
| Strengthening | 8–16 weeks | Progressive loading; proprioception | Band ER/IR; shoulder press; perturbation |
| Function/sport | 16–24 weeks | Return to sport/work | Sport-specific; dynamic stability |

**MDI specific (AMBRI):**
- Primary treatment is rehabilitation — 80% avoid surgery with structured programme (Burkhead & Rockwood, 1992)
- Focus: rotator cuff co-contraction; proprioception; scapular control
- Minimum 6 months rehabilitation trial before surgical consideration
- Avoid provocative positions during initial rehabilitation

### Pharmacological

**NICE NG194 / Acute management:**
- **Acute post-reduction:** NSAIDs (oral/topical) for pain management; paracetamol
- **Opioids:** Short-term (2–5 days) if severe acute pain at ED
- **Corticosteroid injection:** Not indicated for instability (may weaken capsuloligamentous structures)
- **No specific pharmacological intervention** for recurrent instability — rehabilitation is primary

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Urgent surgical referral:**
- Associated neurovascular compromise (vascular injury — emergency; axillary nerve — monitor/EMG)
- Locked dislocation (posterior most common missed)
- Fracture-dislocation (greater tuberosity, glenoid rim)

**Routine surgical referral:**
- First dislocation in patient <25 years — ISIS score ≥4
- Recurrent dislocation (≥2 episodes) at any age
- Failed 6 months structured conservative management (MDI)
- Significant glenoid bone loss (bony Bankart — requires CT/MR arthrogram)
- Athlete requiring surgical stabilisation for return to high-demand sport
- Posterior instability failing rehabilitation

**Surgical Options (HSC NI):**
- Arthroscopic Bankart repair: low ISIS score; no significant bone loss
- Latarjet coracoid transfer: high ISIS score; bone loss >20–25%; failed Bankart
- Posterior capsulorrhaphy/capsular plication: posterior/MDI
- Remplissage: Hill-Sachs lesion engaging; combined with Bankart
