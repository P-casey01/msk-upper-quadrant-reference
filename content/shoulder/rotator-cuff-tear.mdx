## Overview & Pathophysiology

Rotator cuff tears are among the most clinically significant shoulder conditions encountered in musculoskeletal physiotherapy. They represent a spectrum from partial-thickness tears through full-thickness (complete) tears to massive rotator cuff tears involving multiple tendons. The condition sits at the severe end of the tendinopathy continuum model.

**Prevalence:**
- Asymptomatic tears identified in 22% of adults overall; rises to 54% in those >60 years (Yamamoto et al., 2010)
- Symptomatic full-thickness tears: ~2–3% of the general population
- Prevalence doubles each decade after age 50
- Bilateral tears in ~35% of those with symptomatic unilateral tear

**Classification:**

| Classification | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Partial-thickness** | Bursal-sided, articular-sided (PASTA lesion), or intratendinous |
| **Full-thickness small** | <1 cm; typically supraspinatus |
| **Full-thickness medium** | 1–3 cm |
| **Full-thickness large** | 3–5 cm |
| **Massive** | >5 cm or involving ≥2 tendons |
| **Irreparable massive** | With muscle atrophy, fatty infiltration (Goutallier Grade 3–4) |

### Pathophysiology

**Degenerative tears (>90% of all RC tears):**
- Culmination of the continuum model — reactive → dysrepair → degenerative tendinopathy → tear
- Critical zone of supraspinatus (1 cm from insertion): zone of relative avascularity
- Fatty infiltration (Goutallier classification) correlates with chronicity and repair prognosis
- Increasing tear size over time in 40–80% of untreated full-thickness tears (Moosmayer et al., 2013)

**Traumatic tears:**
- Acute forced abduction/ER against resistance (e.g., fall on outstretched arm)
- More common in younger patients with previously healthy tendons
- More likely surgically repairable; better healing rates

### Relevant Anatomy

- **Rotator cuff interval:** Between supraspinatus and subscapularis; contains biceps tendon and coracohumeral ligament; disruption affects inferior capsule stability
- **Subscapularis:** Largest and strongest cuff muscle; anterior stabiliser; tears often missed clinically
- **Fatty infiltration (Goutallier):** Grade 0 (normal) → Grade 4 (fat > muscle); Grade ≥3 = poor surgical prognosis
- **Pseudoparalysis:** Inability to actively elevate arm despite passive ROM due to massive cuff tear

### Supraspinatus Tests

|------|------------|------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **Empty Can (Jobe's)** | 69% | 66% | 2.03 | 0.47 | High | 90° abduction, 30° horizontal adduction, IR, resist downward force |
| **Full Can Test** | 74% | 78% | 3.36 | 0.33 | Moderate | ER throughout; less painful than empty can |
| **Drop Arm Test** | 27% | 88% | 2.25 | 0.83 | Moderate | Arm slowly lowered from 90° — drops = full-thickness tear |

### Infraspinatus / Teres Minor Tests (ER weakness)

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|------------|------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **External Rotation Lag Sign (ERLS)** | 70% | 100% | ∞ | 0.30 | High | Arm at 20° ER, near-ER — inability to hold position = large/full-thickness IR/ER tear |
| **Hornblower's Sign** | 100% | 93% | 14.3 | 0.00 | Moderate | Abduct 90°, elbow 90°, resist ER — positive = teres minor tear |
| **External Rotation Strength** | 85% | 55% | 1.89 | 0.27 | Moderate | Grade 3 or less ER strength — associated with large tears |

### Subscapularis Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|------------|------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **Lift-Off Test (Gerber)** | 62% | 100% | ∞ | 0.38 | High | Hand behind back, palm out — inability to lift from back = subscapularis tear |
| **Belly-Press Test** | 40% | 98% | 20.0 | 0.61 | Moderate | Press palm into abdomen — elbow drops back = positive |
| **Bear-Hug Test** | 60% | 92% | 7.50 | 0.43 | Moderate | Elbow forward, resist palm pull from shoulder; most sensitive for upper subscapularis |
| **Napoleon Test** | 55% | 90% | 5.5 | 0.50 | Moderate | Cannot press belly with flat wrist = positive |
| **Internal Rotation Lag Sign** | 97% | 96% | 24.3 | 0.03 | Moderate | Near-IR, maintain position — lag = subscapularis tear |

### Full-Thickness Tear Cluster (Murrell & Walton, 2001)

If 3 of the following positive → Sensitivity 98%, Specificity 96%:
- Age >60
- Positive supraspinatus weakness
- Positive impingement sign
- Night pain

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Acute onset weakness after trauma (fall, FOOSH) | Full-thickness traumatic tear; fracture | Urgent X-ray; orthopaedic review within 2 weeks |
| Pseudoparalysis (cannot initiate elevation) | Massive RC tear | Urgent USS/MRI; orthopaedic referral |
| Signs of axillary nerve injury post-dislocation | Axillary nerve palsy (deltoid paralysis) | Urgent neurology; EMG at 4–6 weeks |
| Rapid wasting supraspinatus/infraspinatus fossa | Suprascapular nerve entrapment; tumour | MRI; neurology referral |
| Systemic features — fever, constitutional symptoms | Septic arthritis; tumour | Emergency/urgent medical review |
| Severe pain, marked ecchymosis, deformity | Fracture or complete muscle rupture | X-ray; Emergency Department |
| Left arm pain with cardiac symptoms | Cardiac event | 999 — Emergency |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Surgical Decision Framework (Kuhn et al.)

The decision between conservative and surgical management for rotator cuff tears involves:

1. **Tear characteristics:** Size, location, fatty infiltration (Goutallier), tendon retraction
2. **Patient factors:** Age, activity level, hand dominance, comorbidities
3. **Response to conservative management:** 3–6 months' trial first for most tears
4. **Chronicity:** Acute traumatic tears → earlier surgical review

**Prognosis for conservative management:**
- Small/medium tears: 75–80% satisfactory outcomes at 2 years with structured rehabilitation (Moosmayer et al., 2010; RCT — surgery vs physio comparable at 2 years for small-medium tears)
- Massive tears: poorer conservative outcomes; augmentation strategies (superior capsule reconstruction, tendon transfer) emerging

### Lewis Framework Applied to RC Tears

- Differentiate symptomatic from asymptomatic (prevalence of asymptomatic tears is high)
- Functional deficit is the key clinical driver — not imaging findings alone
- Imaging should not drive surgery unless functional deficit correlates

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Imaging for RC Tears

**Ultrasound (USS) — first-line:**
- Sensitivity full-thickness: 92%; Specificity: 97% (de Jesus et al., 2009 — meta-analysis, n=2,144)
- Sensitivity partial-thickness: 67%; Specificity: 94%
- Advantages: dynamic assessment, bilateral comparison, real-time, no radiation, guided injection
- Limitations: operator-dependent; cannot assess labrum; poor visualisation under acromion

**MRI — gold standard for surgical planning:**
- Sensitivity full-thickness: 94%; Specificity: 93%
- Sensitivity partial-thickness: 64%; Specificity: 92%
- Advantages: multiplanar; Goutallier grading; fatty infiltration; bone assessment; labrum
- MR arthrogram: preferred for partial-thickness tears (sensitivity increases to 86%)

**X-ray:**
- Subacromial space <7 mm correlates with massive tear (sensitivity 40%; Specificity 95%)
- Greater tuberosity irregularity/sclerosis
- Calcific deposits; glenohumeral arthritis

### Key Evidence

- **Moosmayer et al. (2010) JBJS-A:** RCT — surgery vs supervised exercise for small-medium full-thickness tears. No significant difference at 1 year; surgery superior at 2 years on Constant-Murley only.
- **Kukkonen et al. (2015) JBJS-A:** RCT — 3 groups (physio; physio + acromioplasty; physio + acromioplasty + cuff repair). No significant difference at 2 years for small tears.
- **Ainsworth et al. (2009):** Structured physiotherapy produces clinically meaningful outcomes in 75% of full-thickness tear patients avoiding surgery.
- **Colvin et al. (2012):** Re-tear rates after surgical repair range from 13–94% depending on tear size and fatty infiltration.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Features | Differentiating |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Rotator cuff tendinopathy | No lag signs; strength preserved (pain-limited) | Strength testing with lag signs |
| Subacromial bursitis | Pain without significant weakness | USS: isolated bursal pathology |
| Adhesive capsulitis | Global ROM restriction; no weakness | Capsular pattern; ROM assessment |
| Glenohumeral arthritis | Crepitus; age; gradual onset | X-ray: joint space narrowing |
| Cervical radiculopathy | Neck symptoms; neurological deficit; normal cuff strength | Neurological exam; ULTT; Spurling's |
| SLAP tear | Young overhead athlete; Popeye sign if associated biceps | O'Brien's; MR arthrogram |
| Acromioclavicular pathology | Direct AC tenderness; cross-body adduction pain | AC joint injection test; X-ray |
| Brachial neuritis | Severe acute pain → rapid weakness; no trauma | EMG; MRI neurography |
| Suprascapular nerve entrapment | Wasting infraspinatus; deep aching; ER weakness | EMG; MRI (ganglion cyst) |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care:**
- GP referral to community physiotherapy or MSK self-referral
- USS arranged by GP or MSK service to confirm and classify tear prior to orthopaedic referral
- Image-guided injection (where indicated) via GPwSI or MSK interface clinic

**MSK Interface (HSC NI):**
- First Contact Practitioners (FCPs) increasingly managing RC tear conservatively
- Structured exercise programme; education regarding natural history and asymptomatic tears
- Clear criteria for onward orthopaedic referral (see below)
- Waiting times: orthopaedic outpatient currently 12–24+ months across most trusts; early referral for acute traumatic tears

**Surgical:**
- Arthroscopic repair predominantly; open repair for massive/complex tears
- Day-case procedure at NI orthopaedic centres (Musgrave Park Hospital, Altnagelvin, Antrim, Ulster Hospital)
- Post-operative rehabilitation via community/trust physiotherapy (protocol-driven sling phase 4–6 weeks → progressive exercise)

### Conservative Management

**Structured Rehabilitation Programme:**

| Phase | Duration | Goals | Key Exercises | Loading |
|-------|----------|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Phase 1: Protection** | Weeks 0–4 | Pain control; ROM; protect cuff | Pendulum; elbow/wrist AROM; scapular retraction; submaximal isometrics | Isometric only |
| **Phase 2: Activation** | Weeks 4–8 | Rotator cuff activation; scapular control | Side-lying ER; standing ER with band; prone Y/T/W; side-lying IR | Light isotonic |
| **Phase 3: Strengthening** | Weeks 8–16 | Strength and endurance; functional ROM | Progressive band ER/IR; scaption; shoulder press; serratus activation; HSR protocol | Progressive load 3×10–15 |
| **Phase 4: Function** | Weeks 16–24 | Return to activity/work/sport | Functional patterns; overhead mechanics; work simulation | Sport/task-specific |

**Evidence base:**
- Cochrane (Page et al., 2016): Supervised exercise superior to no treatment; comparable to surgery for small-medium tears in 2-year outcomes
- Specific exercises (ER, IR, scaption) preferred over non-specific — Kuhn et al. (2010)
- Heavy slow resistance (HSR): emerging evidence comparable to surgical outcomes for small-medium tears

### Pharmacological

Per **NICE NG194:**
- **Topical NSAIDs:** First-line analgesic for RC pathology — short-term benefit
- **Oral NSAIDs:** Minimum dose/duration; consider PPI if >45 years; COX-2 selective if high GI risk
- **Corticosteroid injection:**
  - Subacromial injection: short-term pain relief (Level I — Cochrane); useful to enable rehabilitation
  - Intra-tendinous injection contraindicated — associated with tendon weakening and rupture
  - USS-guided preferred; up to 3 per year per NICE/HSC guidance
- **Avoid:** Opioids for chronic shoulder pain; hyaluronic acid (insufficient evidence)

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Urgent orthopaedic referral (within 2–4 weeks):**
- Acute traumatic full-thickness tear with significant weakness, age <60
- Pseudoparalysis following acute trauma
- Associated fracture or dislocation

**Routine orthopaedic referral (standard pathway):**
- Failed 3–6 months structured conservative management (documented)
- Large/massive tear with functional deficit impacting activities of daily living or work
- Progressive weakness despite conservative management
- Young, active patient (<60) with symptomatic full-thickness tear
- Patient preference for surgical discussion after fully informed

**Pre-referral requirements (HSC NI standard):**
- USS or MRI confirming tear size and characteristics
- Documented physiotherapy trial (minimum 3 months)
- Outcome measure scores (SPADI/WORC) at baseline and discharge
