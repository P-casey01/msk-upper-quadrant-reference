## Overview & Pathophysiology

The glenoid labrum is a fibrocartilaginous rim attached to the periphery of the glenoid fossa, deepening the socket by approximately 50% and contributing significantly to glenohumeral stability. Labral tears fall into two main clinical categories encountered in MSK physiotherapy practice:

### SLAP Lesions (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior)

SLAP lesions involve the superior labrum at the biceps anchor (long head of biceps tendon inserts at the supraglenoid tubercle and superior labrum). They are common in overhead athletes, throwing sports, and following traction/compression injuries.

**Snyder Classification (1990):**

| Type | Description | Prevalence | Management Implication |
|------|-------------|-----------|------------------------|
| Type I | Superior labral fraying/degeneration; biceps anchor intact | ~11% | Conservative |
| Type II | Detachment of superior labrum and biceps anchor from glenoid (most common) | ~41% | Often surgical if conservative fails |
| Type III | Bucket-handle tear of superior labrum; biceps anchor intact | ~33% | Surgical resection |
| Type IV | Bucket-handle tear extending into biceps tendon | ~15% | Surgical repair/tenotomy |
| Type V–X | Extended classifications (Morgan/Maffet) including combined lesions | Variable | Specialist-led |

**Pathomechanics:**
- **Repetitive overhead loading**: Peel-back mechanism during late cocking phase — posterior biceps vector peels the labrum off the glenoid
- **GIRD (Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit)**: Posterior capsule tightness shifts humeral head posterosuperiorly, increasing shear on superior labrum
- **Traction injury**: Sudden inferior distraction force (e.g., catching a heavy object)
- **Compression**: FOOSH (fall on outstretched hand) with superior shear force

### Bankart Lesions (Anterior Labral Tears)

Bankart lesions involve detachment of the anteroinferior labrum (typically 3–6 o'clock position) from the glenoid rim. They are the primary pathological lesion in traumatic anterior glenohumeral instability, present in **>85% of first-time anterior dislocations**.

**Subtypes:**
- **Classic Bankart**: Labrum and periosteum stripped from glenoid rim
- **Bony Bankart**: Associated anterior glenoid bone loss (>15–20% glenoid width indicates critical bone loss and alters surgical planning)
- **ALPSA (Anterior Labroligamentous Periosteal Sleeve Avulsion)**: Periosteum intact; labrum medially displaced — poorer proprioceptive outcome if untreated
- **HAGL (Humeral Avulsion of Glenohumeral Ligament)**: Less common; IGHL avulses from humeral attachment

**Associated lesions:** Hill-Sachs defect (posterolateral humeral head impression fracture) in 47–90% of dislocations; engaging Hill-Sachs lesions complicate surgical planning.

## Special Tests

> **Clinical Note:** No single special test confirms labral pathology with sufficient diagnostic certainty. A cluster approach combining ≥3 positive tests improves accuracy. MR arthrography remains the gold standard for SLAP (sensitivity 82–92%, specificity 85–98%).

### SLAP-Specific Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **O'Brien's Active Compression** | 0.47–0.67 | 0.11–0.98 | 1.1–3.0 | 0.50–0.68 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | High false-positive rate; AC joint pathology also positive |
| **Crank Test** | 0.46 | 0.56 | 1.05 | 0.97 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | Supine: 90° abduction, axial load + IR/ER rotation; pain/clunk = positive |
| **Biceps Load II** | 0.90 | 0.97 | 29.9 | 0.10 | High (⭐⭐⭐⭐) | Best individual SLAP test; 90° abduction, 90° ER, resisted elbow flexion from supination |
| **Anterior Slide Test** | 0.78 | 0.92 | 9.75 | 0.24 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | Anterior directed force on elbow with hand on hip; pain/click at anterosuperior joint |
| **Kim Test (SLAP variant)** | 0.80 | 0.94 | 13.3 | 0.21 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | 90° abduction; axial load + 45° diagonal elevation + posterior-inferior force |
| **Speed's Test** | 0.32–0.69 | 0.55–0.67 | 1.27 | 0.90 | Low (⭐⭐) | Primarily biceps tendinopathy; poor SLAP discrimination |
| **Yergason's Test** | 0.43 | 0.79 | 2.05 | 0.72 | Low (⭐⭐) | Bicipital groove tenderness more informative |
| **Compression Rotation** | 0.24 | 0.76 | 1.00 | 1.00 | Low (⭐⭐) | Poor LRs; not recommended in isolation |

**SLAP Test Cluster (Meserve et al.):** O'Brien's + Crank + Anterior Slide — ≥2 positive tests: sensitivity 0.75, specificity 0.85, +LR 5.0

### Instability / Bankart Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **Apprehension Test** | 0.50–0.72 | 0.96–0.99 | 17.6–20.0 | 0.32–0.53 | High (⭐⭐⭐⭐) | Apprehension (not just pain) is key finding |
| **Relocation Test (Jobe)** | 0.57–0.81 | 0.87–0.92 | 6.7–10.0 | 0.21–0.48 | High (⭐⭐⭐⭐) | Relief of apprehension with posterior pressure = positive |
| **Anterior Release Test** | 0.92 | 0.89 | 8.4 | 0.09 | High (⭐⭐⭐⭐) | Removal of posterior force causes return of apprehension |
| **Sulcus Sign** | 0.28 | 0.97 | 9.33 | 0.74 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | Inferior instability / MDI component |
| **Load and Shift** | 0.50 | 0.66 | 1.47 | 0.76 | Low (⭐⭐) | Examiner-dependent; establishes direction of laxity |

> **Diagnostic rule:** Apprehension + Relocation + Anterior Release triad has been shown to have sensitivity of 0.68 and specificity of 0.99 for anterior instability (Lo et al., 2004). Apprehension alone is the single best test.

## Red Flags

The following warrant urgent onward referral or emergency management — do **not** manage conservatively without senior review:

| Red Flag | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Acute traumatic dislocation with neurovascular compromise (axillary nerve deficit, absent radial pulse) | A&E same day |
| Bony Bankart with >25% glenoid bone loss on imaging | Urgent orthopaedic referral — high recurrence risk, Latarjet procedure indicated |
| Associated proximal humerus fracture (fracture-dislocation) | A&E same day |
| Unreduced glenohumeral dislocation | A&E same day |
| Progressive neurological deficit (brachial plexus neuropraxia not resolving at 6 weeks) | Urgent neurology/orthopaedic referral |
| Recurrent instability with Hill-Sachs engaging on external rotation | Orthopaedic referral — remplissage or Latarjet may be required |
| First-time dislocation in patient <25 years (high recurrence risk, 70–90%) | Expedited orthopaedic discussion re: surgical prevention |
| Suspected malignancy (unexplained wasting, night pain, weight loss, age >50 with insidious onset) | 2-week wait cancer pathway |

## Clinical Frameworks

### SLAP Decision Framework

```
Presentation → Overhead athlete / throwing sport / traction mechanism
     ↓
History: Pain with late cocking phase, clicking, instability, biceps anchor tenderness
     ↓
Special Tests: Biceps Load II + O'Brien's + Crank (cluster ≥2 positive)
     ↓
           Positive Cluster                    Negative Cluster
                ↓                                    ↓
     Request MR Arthrography              Consider alternative diagnosis
     (gold standard; plain MRI            (RC pathology, AC joint,
      misses 26–28% SLAP II)              cervical referral)
           ↓
     SLAP confirmed on imaging
           ↓
     Conservative trial 3–6 months:       Failure of conservative → 
     Scapular control, rotator cuff,      Orthopaedic referral
     posterior capsule stretch,           (arthroscopic labral repair
     sport-specific rehab                  vs. biceps tenodesis age >35)
```

### Bankart / Instability Framework (HSC NI)

```
First-time traumatic anterior dislocation
     ↓
A&E reduction → Immobilisation (2–3 weeks) → Physiotherapy
     ↓
Risk stratification:
  • Age <25 + contact/overhead sport → HIGH recurrence (70–90%)
  • Age >40 + first dislocation → Rotator cuff tear more likely
  • Bone loss >20% glenoid OR engaging Hill-Sachs → SURGICAL
     ↓
Conservative physiotherapy (12–16 weeks):
  • Phase 1 (0–6 wk): Pendulum, scapular control, rotator cuff activation
  • Phase 2 (6–12 wk): Progressive strengthening, proprioception, neuromuscular control
  • Phase 3 (12–16 wk): Functional/sport-specific, return-to-play criteria
     ↓
Recurrence or failure of conservative → Orthopaedic referral
(Arthroscopic Bankart repair; Latarjet if bone loss)
```

### GIRD Management (SLAP prevention/rehab)

- **Sleeper stretch**: Side-lying on affected shoulder, horizontal adduction + IR — target ≥5° gain in IR compared to contralateral
- **Cross-body stretch**: Horizontal adduction in standing — posterior capsule release
- **Goal**: Achieve GIRD <18° or symmetric to contralateral shoulder (Wilk et al., 2011)

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Imaging Hierarchy

| Modality | SLAP Sensitivity | SLAP Specificity | Notes |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|-------|
| **MR Arthrography (MRA)** | 82–92% | 85–98% | Gold standard; intra-articular gadolinium required |
| **Plain MRI (3T)** | 63–82% | 79–94% | Adequate for Bankart; misses 18–26% SLAP II |
| **Plain MRI (1.5T)** | 44–68% | 66–87% | Inferior; request MRA if clinical suspicion high |
| **Ultrasound** | Poor for SLAP/Bankart | — | Not indicated for labral pathology |
| **CT Arthrography** | 78–91% | 91% | Alternative if MRI contraindicated; better for bone loss quantification |
| **Plain X-ray (AP/Axillary/Scapular-Y)** | N/A | N/A | Mandatory first-line to exclude fracture, bone loss, Hill-Sachs |

### Diagnostic Accuracy Summary

**For SLAP lesions:** Biceps Load II is the most accurate standalone test (sens 0.90, spec 0.97). A positive cluster (≥2 of 3: O'Brien's + Crank + Anterior Slide) improves specificity to 0.85. If clinical cluster positive → MR arthrography.

**For Bankart/instability:** History of dislocation is the most powerful diagnostic factor. Apprehension + Relocation + Anterior Release triad achieves specificity of 0.99. Plain X-ray to exclude fracture/bone loss is mandatory first-line.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Differentiating Features | Key Tests | Imaging |
|-----------|--------------------------|-----------|---------|
| **Subacromial Impingement / RC Tear** | Pain arc 60–120°, painful resisted abduction/ER; older age group | Neer, Hawkins-Kennedy, Empty Can, Lift-Off | USS first-line; MRI if full-thickness tear suspected |
| **AC Joint Pathology** | Pain localised to AC joint; positive cross-body adduction; O'Brien's positive with pain at top not deep in shoulder | Cross-body adduction, AC joint palpation | X-ray AC views, USS |
| **Biceps Tendinopathy / Rupture** | Anterior groove tenderness; "Popeye" deformity (rupture); speed's positive | Speed's, Yergason's, palpation | USS bicipital groove |
| **Adhesive Capsulitis** | Global passive ROM restriction (all planes); insidious onset; ER most restricted | Capsular pattern assessment | X-ray to exclude OA; MRI shows capsular thickening |
| **SLAC (Superior Labral Anterior-posterior Cyst)** | Paralabral cyst on imaging; suprascapular nerve compression; spinati wasting | EMG/NCS, suprascapular notch palpation | MRI |
| **Multidirectional Instability (MDI)** | Bilateral laxity; atraumatic onset; sulcus sign; Beighton score | Sulcus sign, load-and-shift all directions, Beighton score | MRI; often normal |
| **Cervical Radiculopathy (C5/C6)** | Dermatomal symptoms; neck movement provokes; reflexes affected | Spurling's, Upper limb tension test | Cervical MRI |
| **Glenohumeral OA** | Older age; end-stage reduced ROM; crepitus; loss of joint space | Crepitus, passive ROM | X-ray (joint space narrowing, osteophytes) |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Pathway

> Aligned with **NICE guidelines** and **BESS (British Elbow & Shoulder Society) consensus** recommendations. Refer to local MSK pathway documentation for specific waiting time targets.

**Step 1: Initial Physiotherapy Assessment (Community/PCMSK)**
- Full subjective and objective assessment
- Red flag screen (see above)
- Baseline outcome measures (WOSI + DASH)
- Imaging: Plain X-ray if not already done (exclude fracture, bone loss)
- Onward imaging request: MRI (or MR arthrography) if labral pathology suspected and clinical cluster positive

**Step 2: Conservative Management (Primary)**
- Indicated for all patients initially (except surgical red flags above)
- Duration: minimum 12–16 weeks structured physiotherapy

**Step 3: Onward Referral Triggers**
- Failure of 12–16 weeks structured physiotherapy
- Recurrent instability (≥2 episodes) despite rehab
- Age <25 + first dislocation + high-demand overhead sport (early surgical discussion)
- Imaging-confirmed SLAP II/III/IV with ongoing functional limitation

### Conservative Management

**Phase 1 — Acute/Pain Control (Weeks 0–4)**
- Activity modification; avoid aggravating overhead/throwing positions
- Pendulum exercises, AROM within comfort
- Scapular retraction/depression activation
- Heat/ice PRN for symptom management
- Patient education: mechanism, prognosis, self-management

**Phase 2 — Strengthening (Weeks 4–12)**
- Rotator cuff strengthening: ER/IR with resistance band (supine → standing progression)
- Scapular stabiliser strengthening: serratus anterior (wall push-up plus), lower trapezius
- Posterior capsule stretching if GIRD present (sleeper stretch, cross-body stretch)
- Proprioception: closed-chain upper extremity exercises, unstable surface work

**Phase 3 — Sport/Function (Weeks 12–16+)**
- Sport-specific progressive loading
- Throwing athletes: interval throwing programme (ITP) — Andrews protocol
- Return-to-play criteria:
  - Full, pain-free ROM
  - ≥90% strength symmetry (isokinetic or HHD)
  - WOSI >85% of maximum score
  - Negative apprehension/biceps provocation tests
  - Functional/sport-specific tasks completed without limitation

### Pharmacological

| Agent | Indication | Evidence | Notes |
|-------|------------|----------|-------|
| **NSAIDs (oral)** | Acute pain, swelling post-dislocation | Moderate | 7–14 day course; gastroprotection if risk factors |
| **Paracetamol** | Background analgesia | Moderate | First-line adjunct; safe long-term |
| **Intra-articular corticosteroid** | Significant synovitis / concurrent impingement | Low-Moderate | Not recommended as primary treatment for labral tears; may mask symptoms |
| **Opioids** | Post-reduction acute pain only | Expert consensus | Short-term (72 hrs); not for ongoing management |

> No pharmacological agent treats the labral tear itself. Medication is adjunctive to physiotherapy.

### Onward Referral Criteria (HSC NI MSK Pathway)

**Routine Orthopaedic Referral:**
- SLAP II–IV confirmed on MR arthrography with failure of ≥12 weeks physiotherapy
- First-time Bankart with failure of 16 weeks structured rehab and ongoing instability

**Expedited Orthopaedic Referral (within 4 weeks):**
- Recurrent anterior dislocations (≥3 episodes)
- Age <25 with first traumatic dislocation + competitive overhead/contact sport
- Imaging showing >15% glenoid bone loss

**Urgent/Same-Day A&E:**
- Unreduced dislocation, fracture-dislocation, neurovascular compromise

**Surgical Options (for clinician awareness):**
- **Arthroscopic Bankart repair**: Gold standard for Bankart without significant bone loss; recurrence rate 3–15%
- **Latarjet procedure**: Coracoid transfer for critical glenoid bone loss (>20%) or failed Bankart repair; recurrence rate <3%
- **SLAP repair vs. biceps tenodesis**: SLAP repair for athletes <35 years; biceps tenodesis preferred in workers >35 years or failed SLAP repair (superior patient satisfaction in some series)
- **Remplissage**: Capsulodesis of posterior capsule into Hill-Sachs defect for engaging lesions
