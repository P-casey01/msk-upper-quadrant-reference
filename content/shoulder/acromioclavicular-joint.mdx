## Overview & Pathophysiology

The acromioclavicular joint (ACJ) is a diarthrodial joint between the distal clavicle and the acromion, stabilised by the acromioclavicular (AC) and coracoclavicular (CC) ligaments (trapezoid and conoid). It transmits forces between the upper limb and axial skeleton and allows approximately 20° of rotation during shoulder elevation.

### Conditions Covered

**ACJ Sprain / Separation**
Traumatic disruption of AC and/or CC ligaments, typically from a direct fall onto the point of the shoulder or onto an outstretched hand (FOOSH). The distal clavicle rides superiorly relative to the acromion proportional to ligamentous damage. Most common in males aged 20–40 and in contact sports (rugby, GAA, cycling).

**ACJ Osteoarthritis**
Degenerative joint disease of the ACJ, often presenting in middle-aged to older adults. Associated with repetitive overhead loading, prior trauma, and distal clavicle osteolysis. Cartilaginous narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, and osteophyte formation reduce the subacromial space and may contribute to secondary impingement.

**Distal Clavicle Osteolysis**
Stress-related resorption of the distal clavicle, classically in weight-lifters and overhead athletes. Histology shows microfractures, vascular proliferation, and osteoclastic resorption without infection. X-ray shows tapering and irregular resorption of the distal 2–3 cm of clavicle.

## Rockwood Classification (ACJ Separation)

| Grade | Structures Damaged | Clavicle Displacement | Management |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------|------------|
| **I** | AC ligament sprain only | None | Conservative |
| **II** | AC ligament torn, CC sprain | < 25% superior | Conservative |
| **III** | AC + CC ligaments torn | 25–100% superior | Controversial (usually conservative) |
| **IV** | AC + CC torn; clavicle displaced posteriorly into trapezius | Posterior | Surgical |
| **V** | AC + CC torn; deltotrapezial fascia stripped | 100–300% superior | Surgical |
| **VI** | Inferior dislocation (rare) | Inferior to coracoid | Surgical |

> **Clinical Note:** Grades I–II = conservative. Grade III = individualise (most evidence supports non-operative). Grades IV–VI = refer for orthopaedic assessment.

## Special Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | −LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|------------|------------|-----|-----|---------------|-------|
| **O'Brien's Active Compression Test** | 0.16–0.41 | 0.79–0.99 | 1.6–41 | 0.60–0.85 | Moderate (Hegedus et al. 2012) | High specificity for ACJ when pain localised to top of shoulder; lower sens for SLAP |
| **Cross-Body Adduction Test** | 0.77 | 0.79 | 3.7 | 0.29 | Moderate (Mazzocca et al. 2004) | Arm adducted across chest in 90° flexion; reproduces ACJ pain |
| **Paxinos Test** | 0.79 | 0.50 | 1.58 | 0.42 | Low–Moderate (Walton et al. 2004) | Examiner's thumb pushes clavicle anteroinferiorly; reproduces ACJ symptoms |
| **Horizontal Adduction Stress Test** | 0.82 | 0.28 | 1.14 | 0.64 | Low (Chronopoulos et al. 2004) | High sensitivity but poor specificity |
| **ACJ Palpation** | 0.96 | 0.10 | 1.07 | 0.40 | Low (Chronopoulos et al. 2004) | Useful for confirming location; poor for ruling in/out pathology |

> **Diagnostic Tip:** O'Brien's is most useful when pain is reproduced at the top of the shoulder AND relieved with palm-up position. Pain deep in the shoulder suggests SLAP lesion rather than ACJ pathology.

### Diagnostic Injection (Gold Standard)
Lidocaine injection into ACJ with ≥50% pain relief is considered the clinical reference standard for ACJ pathology (sensitivity 0.96, specificity 0.84 — Walton et al. 2004). Request via GP or MSK consultant if diagnosis uncertain.

## Red Flags

> ⚠️ **Refer urgently if any of the following are present:**

- **Neurovascular compromise** — paraesthesia, weakness, or absent pulses following trauma
- **Open ACJ dislocation** or skin tenting/threat of skin breakdown (Grade V/VI)
- **Pathological fracture** — no trauma history, known malignancy, unexplained weight loss, night pain out of proportion
- **Suspected septic arthritis** — fever, systemic illness, rapid joint swelling, raised CRP/WBC
- **Cervical myelopathy** — bilateral symptoms, gait disturbance, bladder/bowel dysfunction
- **Polyarthropathy** — multiple joint involvement, morning stiffness >30 min (consider inflammatory arthropathy → rheumatology)
- **Distal clavicle osteolysis with systemic symptoms** — consider PVNS or metabolic bone disease

## Clinical Frameworks

### Diagnostic Algorithm

```
Mechanism of injury?
├─ Trauma (fall onto shoulder/FOOSH)
│   ├─ Check Rockwood grade clinically + X-ray (AP + Zanca view)
│   ├─ Grade I–II → Physiotherapy (see management)
│   ├─ Grade III → Shared decision-making; most → conservative
│   └─ Grade IV–VI → Orthopaedic referral (surgical)
└─ Insidious / overuse
    ├─ ACJ OA or distal clavicle osteolysis
    ├─ Cross-body adduction + Paxinos test
    ├─ X-ray (Zanca view preferred): OA changes, osteolysis, joint space narrowing
    └─ Diagnostic injection if diagnosis unclear
```

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Imaging Guidelines

| Modality | Indication | Findings |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **X-ray (AP + Zanca 10° cephalic tilt)** | First-line; trauma/OA | CC distance (normal 11–13 mm), joint space narrowing, osteolysis, stress views for instability |
| **Ultrasound** | Dynamic assessment, guided injection | Joint effusion, capsular thickening, distal clavicle changes |
| **MRI** | Soft tissue assessment, planned surgery | CC ligament integrity, labral pathology, bone oedema (osteolysis) |
| **CT** | Complex/comminuted fractures, pre-surgical | Clavicle fracture pattern, posterior displacement (Grade IV) |

> **Zanca View:** 10–15° cephalic tilt reduces overlap with spine; reduces beam by 50% for better joint detail. Preferred over standard AP for ACJ assessment.

### Bilateral Stress Views
Weight-bearing X-rays with 10 lb weights hung from wrists: identifies Grade III vs IV by measuring CC distance bilaterally. Now used selectively (not routinely).

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Key Differentiating Features | Investigation |
|-----------|------------------------------|--------------|
| **Subacromial impingement** | Painful arc 60–120°, positive Neer/Hawkins | USS, MRI |
| **Rotator cuff tear** | Weakness on resisted rotation, positive drop arm | USS/MRI |
| **SLAP lesion** | O'Brien's positive with deep shoulder pain, biceps involvement | MRI arthrogram |
| **Sternoclavicular joint pathology** | Pain medial to ACJ, SCJ tenderness, swelling | X-ray, CT |
| **Cervical radiculopathy (C4–C5)** | Dermatomal distribution, neck movement reproduces symptoms | Cervical assessment, MRI |
| **Inflammatory arthropathy** | Bilateral, morning stiffness, systemic features | Blood (CRP, RF, anti-CCP), rheumatology |
| **Thoracic outlet syndrome** | Vascular/neurogenic symptoms with provocation | EAST test, vascular studies |
| **Clavicle stress fracture** | Localised clavicle tenderness, overhead athletes | X-ray, MRI bone oedema |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

> **Physiotherapy First Access:**
> In HSC NI, patients with ACJ disorders can self-refer or be referred via GP to MSK Physiotherapy (First Contact Practitioners at some sites). Onward referral to MSK Consultant / Orthopaedics follows failure of conservative management (typically 6–12 weeks) or specific indications (Grade IV–VI, suspected septic arthritis).

**Pathway:**
1. **GP / Self-referral / FCP** → MSK Physiotherapy assessment
2. **Conservative management** (6–12 weeks)
3. **No improvement** → MSK Consultant / Orthopaedic review → Consider diagnostic injection / imaging
4. **Surgical indications** → Orthopaedic referral (via MSK Consultant)

### Conservative Management (Grades I–III)

**Acute Phase (Week 0–3):**
- Sling for comfort (Grade II–III): typically 1–2 weeks maximum; avoid prolonged immobilisation
- Ice (10–15 min, 3–4× daily), activity modification
- Analgesia (see pharmacological)
- Patient education: expected recovery timeline, activity modification, return-to-sport guidance

**Rehabilitation Phase (Week 2–12):**
- **Range of motion**: pendular exercises → active-assisted → full active ROM
- **Rotator cuff strengthening**: isometric → isotonic (bands → free weights)
- **Periscapular strengthening**: lower trapezius, serratus anterior — critical for ACJ load reduction
- **Proprioception / neuromuscular training**: closed-chain progression
- **Sport-specific rehabilitation**: graded return to impact/contact

**Evidence Base:**
- Mouhsine et al. (2003): Grade I–III — 90% satisfaction with conservative management at 2 years
- Mazzocca et al. (2011): No significant difference in outcomes between surgical and conservative Grade III at 5 years

### ACJ OA / Distal Clavicle Osteolysis
- **Load modification**: reduce overhead pressing, heavy bench press (modify grip width — wider grip reduces ACJ stress)
- **Periscapular strengthening** as above
- **Manual therapy**: ACJ mobilisation (Grade III–IV Maitland); may reduce pain (limited RCT evidence)
- **Distal clavicle osteolysis**: relative rest from causative activity for 3–6 months; most resolve

### Pharmacological Management

| Drug | Dose | Duration | Notes |
|------|------|---------|-------|
| **Paracetamol** | 1 g QDS | PRN | First-line; safest option |
| **NSAIDs (e.g. Naproxen 500 mg BD, Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS)** | Standard | ≤4 weeks | With food; contraindicated in renal impairment, GI history, anticoagulation |
| **Topical NSAIDs (Diclofenac gel)** | Apply 3–4× daily | ≤4 weeks | NICE preferred for local pain; reduced systemic effects |
| **Intra-articular corticosteroid injection** | e.g. Triamcinolone 20 mg + lidocaine | 1–3 per joint/year | Via GP, MSK consultant, or radiology; ≥50% pain relief expected if ACJ is source |

> **NICE Guidance (MSK conditions):** Offer exercise-based therapy first. Corticosteroid injection for short-term relief while commencing rehabilitation. Avoid >3 injections/year to same joint.

### Onward Referral Criteria

| Situation | Referral Destination | Urgency |
|-----------|---------------------|---------|
| Grade IV–VI ACJ separation | Orthopaedics | Urgent (within days if neurovascular compromise) |
| Grade III — failed 3–6 months conservative; high-demand athlete | MSK Consultant / Orthopaedics | Routine |
| ACJ OA — failed 6–12 weeks conservative + failed injection | MSK Consultant / Orthopaedics | Routine |
| Distal clavicle osteolysis — failed 6 months conservative | MSK Consultant | Routine |
| Suspected inflammatory arthropathy | Rheumatology | 2-week wait if systemically unwell |
| Red flags (septic arthritis, tumour) | A&E / Orthopaedics | Same day |

### Surgical Options (for reference)
- **Grade III**: ACCR (acromioclavicular coracoclavicular reconstruction) or Weaver-Dunn procedure
- **ACJ OA / Osteolysis**: Distal clavicle excision (Mumford procedure) — arthroscopic or open; high satisfaction rates (85–92%)
