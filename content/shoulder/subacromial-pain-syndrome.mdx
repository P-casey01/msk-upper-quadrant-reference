## Overview & Pathophysiology

Subacromial Pain Syndrome (SAPS) is the currently preferred umbrella term for pain arising from the subacromial space, replacing the older — and now disputed — term "subacromial impingement syndrome" (SIS). SAPS encompasses pathology of the subacromial bursa, the rotator cuff tendons (particularly supraspinatus), and the coracoacromial arch, without implying a specific structural mechanism.

The terminology shift reflects a fundamental change in understanding: the mechanical impingement model (Neer, 1972) has been largely replaced by a biopsychosocial and load-based framework. SAPS accounts for 44–65% of all shoulder pain presentations.

### Historical Concepts vs Current Understanding

| Model | Historical (Neer, 1972) | Current (Lewis, 2009–2016) |
|-------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Mechanism | Mechanical pinching of supraspinatus under coracoacromial arch | Multifactorial: load, motor control, biopsychosocial |
| Treatment | Acromioplasty | Exercise; surgical benefit not supported |
| Terminology | Subacromial Impingement Syndrome | Rotator Cuff Related Shoulder Pain / SAPS |
| Key evidence | Cadaveric studies | RCT: Beard et al. 2018 (CSAW trial) |

**CSAW Trial (Beard et al., 2018 — JAMA):** Landmark RCT demonstrating that arthroscopic acromioplasty + physiotherapy was NOT superior to physiotherapy alone or sham surgery. This fundamentally challenged the impingement model and shifted treatment toward exercise-based management.

### Pathophysiology

**Primary contributors:**
1. **Subacromial bursal inflammation/thickening:** The bursa (lying between rotator cuff and acromion) is highly innervated — a key pain source
2. **Rotator cuff tendinopathy:** Most commonly supraspinatus — on the continuum from reactive to degenerative
3. **Altered shoulder kinematics:** Scapular dyskinesis; reduced upward rotation; increased internal rotation during elevation
4. **Rotator cuff force couple imbalance:** Weakness/fatigue of external rotators and lower trapezius; relative upper trapezius overactivation

**Extrinsic factors:**
- Acromion morphology (Bigliani Type II — curved; Type III — hooked): associated but not causal
- Acromioclavicular joint osteophytes
- Coracoacromial ligament thickening
- Occupational overhead loading; sport (swimming, throwing, racquet sports)

**Intrinsic factors:**
- Rotator cuff tendon degeneration (age-related)
- Reduced vascularity in supraspinatus critical zone
- Tendon morphology and genetic factors

### Prevalence
- Most common shoulder diagnosis in primary care (44–65%)
- Annual incidence in UK general practice: ~14.7/1000 patients/year (Linsell et al., 2006)
- Peak incidence: 45–64 years; increasing with overhead occupational exposure

## Special Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|------------|------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **Hawkins-Kennedy** | 79% | 59% | 1.93 | 0.36 | High | Best sensitivity — use to rule OUT SAPS; passive shoulder/elbow 90° flexion, IR |
| **Neer Impingement Sign** | 72% | 60% | 1.80 | 0.47 | High | Passive arm elevation with ER blocked; stabilise scapula; watch for pain arc |
| **Painful Arc (60–120°)** | 74% | 81% | 3.89 | 0.32 | High | Best specificity in isolation; pain during arc of elevation |
| **Empty Can (Jobe's)** | 69% | 66% | 2.03 | 0.47 | High | Supraspinatus load test; 90° abduction, 30° horizontal adduction, IR |
| **Full Can** | 74% | 78% | 3.36 | 0.33 | Moderate | Better tolerated than empty can; ER maintained |
| **Neer Impingement Test** | 88% | 44% | 1.57 | 0.27 | Moderate | Relief within 5 min of subacromial lidocaine injection confirms subacromial source |
| **Internal Rotation Resistance Test (IRRT)** | 88% | 96% | 22.0 | 0.13 | Moderate | Differentiates intra-articular from subacromial — IR resisted; if MORE painful than ER resisted = intra-articular |
| **Infraspinatus Muscle Test** | 42% | 90% | 4.20 | 0.64 | Moderate | ER strength deficit in neutral — if positive with Hawkins/Painful Arc, improves cluster accuracy |
| **Horizontal Adduction (Scarf) Test** | 77% | 79% | 3.67 | 0.29 | Moderate | Primarily for ACJ but pain may indicate posterior subacromial origin |

### Diagnostic Cluster — Michener et al. (2009)
**Subacromial Impingement Cluster (3 tests positive):**
- Hawkins-Kennedy + Painful Arc + Infraspinatus Muscle Test
- +LR = **10.56** for SAPS (highly useful rule-in)
- -LR = **0.17** (useful rule-out when all 3 negative)

**Hegedus et al. (2012) systematic review conclusions:**
- No single test is sufficiently accurate for isolated diagnosis
- Cluster testing significantly improves diagnostic accuracy
- The painful arc + full can cluster has best clinical utility for ruling in subacromial pathology

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Age >50, unexplained weight loss, night sweats | Malignancy (Pancoast tumour, bone metastasis) | Urgent referral; CXR |
| Fever, hot joint, systemically unwell | Septic arthritis | Emergency — joint aspiration |
| Acute trauma with immediate severe weakness | RC tear; fracture; dislocation | X-ray; urgent orthopaedic assessment |
| Disproportionate night pain (waking multiple times) | Inflammatory arthritis; malignancy; septic | Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR, WCC) |
| Left shoulder + jaw + chest pain, dyspnoea | Cardiac ischaemia | 999 — Emergency |
| Shoulder/neck pain after abdominal event | Diaphragmatic irritation (C3/C4 referred) | Urgent medical review |
| Rapid muscle wasting (supraspinatus/infraspinatus) | Suprascapular nerve entrapment; space-occupying lesion | MRI; neurology |
| Progressive neurological deficit | Cervical myelopathy; brachial neuritis | Urgent neurology/spinal referral |
| Bony tenderness, bruising after trauma | Fracture | X-ray |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Lewis Shoulder Framework (2009, 2016)
Jeremy Lewis's pragmatic approach to shoulder assessment challenges the impingement model:

1. **Assessment Philosophy:** Pain provocation tests have poor diagnostic accuracy; use clusters not single tests
2. **Treatment Prioritisation:** Shoulder exercise regardless of specific diagnosis; acromioplasty not supported
3. **Staging by behaviour:**
   - Stage 1: Pain only at end range/high load — progressive exercise tolerated
   - Stage 2: Pain through range — modify loading; isometrics may reduce pain
   - Stage 3: Pain at rest — protect and manage; graded return to load
4. **Key principle:** The painful arc itself is not diagnostic — assess what provokes, what reduces, and response to load

### Cools Rehabilitation Model (2016)
**Scapular focus in SAPS rehabilitation:**
- Assess scapular kinematics: upward rotation deficit (SARD) strongly associated with SAPS
- Target serratus anterior and lower trapezius activation
- Correct upper trapezius dominance
- Posterior shoulder stretching for posterior capsule tightness (GIRD — Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit)

**GIRD (Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit):**
- IR deficit >25° compared to contralateral = significant
- Posterior capsule tightness → anterior-superior humeral head migration → subacromial space reduction
- Posterior capsule stretching (sleeper stretch, cross-body stretch) — evidence from throwing athletes

### Biopsychosocial Considerations
- Central sensitisation contributes to chronic SAPS — screen with PainDETECT
- Catastrophising, fear-avoidance, low self-efficacy are predictors of poor outcome
- Address from first consultation; avoid overmedicalising with imaging-driven diagnoses

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### CSAW Trial (Beard et al., 2018 — JAMA)
- Multicentre RCT; n=313; 3 arms: (1) arthroscopic acromioplasty + physio; (2) arthroscopic lavage (sham) + physio; (3) physiotherapy alone
- Primary outcome (Oxford Shoulder Score) at 6 months: **No significant difference between groups**
- Conclusion: Acromioplasty does not add benefit beyond physiotherapy
- Impact: Major shift in surgical practice; acromioplasty rates declining significantly in UK/Ireland

### Imaging Evidence
**USS (Ultrasound):**
- Identifies bursal thickening, rotator cuff changes, calcific deposits
- Dynamic assessment of subacromial space
- First-line investigation (BESS guidelines; NICE NG194)

**MRI:**
- Better for partial-thickness tears, labral pathology, bone marrow changes
- MR arthrogram where structural tear or instability suspected

**X-ray findings in SAPS:**
- Acromion morphology (AP + outlet view)
- Acromioclavicular joint changes
- Calcific deposits
- Greater tuberosity sclerosis/cyst

### Clinical Prediction Rules
- No single validated CPR for SAPS diagnosis equivalent to those in lumbar spine
- Michener cluster (Hawkins + Painful Arc + Infraspinatus) remains most validated (+LR 10.56)
- Response to injection can confirm subacromial source (functional diagnostic injection)

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Features | Differentiating Test | Imaging |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|---------|
| Rotator cuff tendinopathy | Overlaps significantly with SAPS | Load response; muscle tests | USS |
| Full-thickness RC tear | Lag signs positive; significant weakness | Drop arm; ERLS | USS/MRI |
| Adhesive capsulitis | Global ROM restriction; capsular pattern | All directions restricted | USS; X-ray |
| ACJ pathology | Direct AC tenderness; cross-body pain | O'Brien's; AC palpation; injection test | X-ray AC views |
| Calcific tendinitis | Acute severe pain; may mimic infection | X-ray — calcific deposits | X-ray diagnostic |
| Glenohumeral OA | Age; crepitus; restricted; gradual | X-ray: joint space loss | X-ray |
| Cervical radiculopathy | Neck; arm symptoms; neurological signs | Spurling's; ULTT | Cervical MRI |
| SLAP tear | Young; overhead sport; catching/clunking | O'Brien's; Biceps Load II | MR arthrogram |
| Shoulder instability | Young; hyperlaxity; apprehension | Apprehension; Relocation | MR arthrogram |
| Thoracic outlet syndrome | Vascular/neurological; positional | Roos; Adson's; ULTT | Doppler USS; MRI |
| Brachial neuritis | Severe pain → rapid weakness; post-viral | EMG; weakness pattern | MRI neurography |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care / Self-referral:**
- Physio self-referral operational in Belfast, South Eastern, Northern, Western, Southern Trusts
- Initial presentation: conservative management for minimum 6 weeks before imaging or injection in non-urgent cases
- GP arranged USS if: failure to respond to conservative care at 6–12 weeks; suspicion of structural pathology

**MSK Interface Service (HSC NI):**
- MSK First Contact Practitioners (FCPs) in GP practices for triage and direct management
- Community MSK services: USS-guided injection (subacromial), structured rehabilitation prescription, advice
- Image-guided corticosteroid injection: GPwSI/MSK interface pathway; reduces unnecessary orthopaedic referrals
- Orthopaedic waiting times NI 2024–2025: routine 12–26 months; urgent (if SAPS — rare) 4–12 weeks

**Red Flag Escalation:** Emergency (cardiac/septic); urgent 2-week wait (malignancy); urgent orthopaedic (acute traumatic tear with weakness)

### Conservative Management

**Exercise Programme (Evidence Grade A — multiple RCTs, Cochrane):**

| Exercise Type | Example | Dosage | Rationale |
|---------------|---------|--------|-----------|
| Scapular stabilisation | Prone Y/T/W; band retraction; push-up plus | 3×15 reps, daily | Lower trap/serratus activation; correct dyskinesis |
| RC activation | Side-lying ER; ER at 0°; band ER | 3×12–15 reps, daily | Restore ER:IR ratio; reduce superior migration |
| Progressive loading | Scaption; shoulder press; HSR | 3×8–12 reps progressive | Tendon adaptation; pain modulation |
| Posterior stretching | Sleeper stretch; cross-body stretch | 3×30 sec, daily | Correct GIRD; improve IR ROM |
| Thoracic mobility | Thoracic extension; rotation | Daily | Improve scapular platform |

**Manual therapy (Moderate evidence):**
- Thoracic spine manipulation: short-term improvement in pain and function (Bang & Deyle, 2000; Haik et al., 2016)
- Glenohumeral mobilisation (posterior capsule): Grade III–IV for internal rotation deficit
- NOT recommended as standalone; adjunct to exercise

**Education (Essential):**
- Explain SAPS framework — avoid impingement/wear-and-tear language (negative effects on prognosis)
- Activity modification: avoid provocative positions initially; graded return
- Self-management: posture, sleep position, ergonomics
- Prognosis: most SAPS resolves with structured exercise over 3–6 months (80% satisfactory outcome)

### Pharmacological

**NICE NG194 (2021) guidance:**
- **Topical NSAIDs:** First-line analgesic — equivalent to oral NSAIDs with less systemic risk (Level A)
- **Oral NSAIDs:** Lowest effective dose; shortest duration; ibuprofen 400 mg TDS or naproxen 500 mg BD with food; add PPI if >45 years or other risk factors
- **Corticosteroid injection:**
  - Subacromial space: triamcinolone 40 mg + lidocaine; USS-guided superior to landmark (accuracy >95% vs 75%)
  - Short-term benefit (up to 6 weeks): Level I evidence (Cochrane — Koester et al., 2007)
  - Not superior to exercise at 3–6 months
  - Clinical utility: provides pain window to engage with rehabilitation
  - Maximum 3 injections per shoulder per year (NICE; HSC NI guidance)
  - Consider 2-week wait after injection before commencing loading exercises
- **Avoid:** Opioids (no evidence; harm); hyaluronic acid (insufficient evidence — NICE NG194)

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Routine orthopaedic referral:**
- Failed 3–6 months structured conservative management with documented evidence
- Suspected large/full-thickness rotator cuff tear with functional deficit (USS first)
- Recurrent disabling pain despite 2 image-guided injections
- Calcific tendinitis unresponsive to conservative care (consider US-guided needling first)

**Note on acromioplasty (post-CSAW):**
- Acromioplasty should no longer be routinely offered for SAPS without full-thickness tear (NHS England NHS Procedures of Limited Clinical Value, 2018)
- Orthopaedic referral for SAPS alone not indicated unless surgical question exists (e.g., associated tear, failed injection)

**USS/imaging prior to referral:** Required in most HSC NI trusts; arrange before orthopaedic letter
