## Overview & Pathophysiology

Calcific tendinitis (CT) of the shoulder is characterised by the deposition of **calcium hydroxyapatite crystals** within the rotator cuff tendons, most commonly the supraspinatus (80–90% of cases), followed by infraspinatus (~10%) and subscapularis (~5%). It affects 2.7–8% of the adult population, with peak incidence in the 4th–5th decades, and a female predominance (F:M ≈ 2:1).

### Pathophysiology: Uhthoff Model

The most widely accepted pathophysiological model is **Uhthoff & Loehr's (1997) cell-mediated theory** — a fibrocartilaginous transformation of tendon tissue (not a degenerative process), distinct from dystrophic calcification seen in chronic tendinopathy.

**Uhthoff Stages of Calcific Tendinitis:**

| Stage | Phase | Histology | Clinical Features | Duration |
|-------|-------|-----------|-------------------|----------|
| **Pre-calcific** | Fibrocartilaginous transformation | Chondrocytic metaplasia of tendon fibrocytes; no calcium yet | Often asymptomatic; possible mild aching | Months |
| **Calcific — Formative** | Crystal deposition | Calcium crystals deposited in matrix vesicles; chalk-like, soft consistency | Mild–moderate pain; often episodic | Weeks–months |
| **Calcific — Resting** | Quiescent | Calcium consolidates; vascular ingrowth begins | Variable — may be asymptomatic | Variable |
| **Calcific — Resorptive** | ⚠️ Most painful | Phagocytic resorption; toothpaste-like consistency; highly vascular; intense local inflammation | **Severe acute pain**; often the presenting crisis | Days–weeks |
| **Post-calcific** | Remodelling | Fibroblast invasion; collagen remodelling; calcium resolves | Pain subsides; function recovers | Weeks–months |

> **Key clinical implication:** The **resorptive phase** is the most acutely painful (often presenting as apparent 'pseudo-acute' shoulder). Paradoxically, this phase has the best prognosis as calcium is being actively resorbed. Intervention (aspiration/barbotage) is most effective and technically easiest in this phase (fluid/toothpaste consistency).

**Gärtner Radiographic Classification:**

| Type | Appearance | Phase Correlation | Clinical Note |
|------|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Type I** | Dense, homogeneous, well-defined border | Formative/resting | Harder; less likely to resolve spontaneously |
| **Type II** | Dense/translucent, heterogeneous or poorly defined | Transitional | Variable behaviour |
| **Type III** | Translucent, heterogeneous, poorly defined ('fluffy') | Resorptive | Soft; most amenable to barbotage; more likely to resolve spontaneously |

**Why calcium deposits in the critical zone:**
The supraspinatus is most affected in its "critical zone" — 1 cm proximal to the insertion — an area of relative avascularity and high mechanical stress (Rathbun & Macnab, 1970). Hypoxic conditions may trigger fibrocartilaginous metaplasia and initiate the calcification cascade.

**Spontaneous resolution rate:** 27–50% of deposits resolve within 3 years without intervention. Natural history is generally favourable, but 20–30% of patients develop chronic, recalcitrant symptoms.

## Special Tests

> **Clinical Note:** No shoulder special test is specific for calcific tendinitis. Tests below detect subacromial impingement and rotator cuff pathology — calcific tendinitis should be confirmed by imaging. A positive impingement cluster with acute-onset severe pain warrants X-ray.

### Impingement & Rotator Cuff Tests

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Evidence Level | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-----|----------------|-------|
| **Painful Arc** | 0.53–0.74 | 0.81 | 3.7 | 0.57 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | Pain 60–120° abduction; non-specific — impingement, CT, RC tear |
| **Neer Impingement Sign** | 0.72 | 0.66 | 2.1 | 0.43 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | Passive forward flexion with IR; pain = positive |
| **Neer Impingement Test** | 0.83 | 0.54 | 1.80 | 0.31 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | ≥50% pain relief after subacromial lidocaine injection = positive |
| **Hawkins-Kennedy** | 0.72–0.79 | 0.56–0.66 | 1.84–2.32 | 0.34–0.50 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | Forward flexion 90°, forced IR; pain = positive |
| **Empty Can (Jobe)** | 0.69–0.86 | 0.66–0.74 | 2.8 | 0.28 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | Supraspinatus strength; weakness suggests RC involvement |
| **Full Can** | 0.66 | 0.64 | 1.83 | 0.53 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | Less discomfort than Empty Can; equivalent sensitivity |
| **Drop Arm** | 0.21–0.35 | 0.88–0.92 | 2.9 | 0.86 | Moderate (⭐⭐⭐) | High specificity for full-thickness RC tear; low sensitivity |
| **External Rotation Lag Sign** | 0.56–0.77 | 0.98 | 28–38 | 0.28–0.45 | High (⭐⭐⭐⭐) | High specificity for posterosuperior RC tear |

**Impingement Test Cluster (Hegedus et al., 2012):**
Neer + Hawkins-Kennedy + Painful Arc: ≥2 positive tests → sensitivity 0.75, specificity 0.74 for subacromial pathology

> **Calcific tendinitis-specific clinical pattern:** Sudden onset of exquisite shoulder pain (often overnight or following minor activity), severe movement restriction, marked tenderness over supraspinatus insertion, presentation mimicking septic arthritis. X-ray confirms diagnosis.

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Fever + erythema + septic-appearing joint (exclude septic arthritis) | A&E same day — joint aspiration required |
| Severe acute pain with inability to move arm + preceding dental/GI/UTI (reactive arthritis) | Urgent GP/A&E — same day |
| Unexplained weight loss + night pain + age >50 (exclude malignancy) | 2-week wait cancer pathway |
| Acute neurological deficit in upper limb | Urgent neurology/A&E |
| Failure to improve after 3 months conservative treatment | MRI to exclude concurrent full-thickness RC tear; consider escalation |
| Progressive weakness not explained by pain inhibition | Urgent referral — full-thickness tear or neurological cause |
| Bilateral shoulder involvement + systemic symptoms (RA, pseudogout, metabolic disorder) | Rheumatology referral |
| Calcium deposit associated with cortical bone erosion on X-ray | Orthopaedic referral — rare aggressive variant |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Diagnostic Framework

```
Acute/subacute shoulder pain, particularly if severe, sudden onset
     ↓
Screen red flags → if septic arthritis suspected → A&E SAME DAY
     ↓
Physical Examination:
  • Restricted ROM (all planes or primarily in arc 60–120°)
  • Tenderness over supraspinatus insertion (2 cm distal to anterior acromion)
  • Positive impingement signs (Neer, Hawkins-Kennedy)
  • Intact strength (unless pain-inhibited or concurrent RC tear)
     ↓
Plain X-ray (AP + axillary + outlet views) ← FIRST-LINE IMAGING
     ↓
Calcium deposit identified           No calcium identified
Classify: Gärtner I/II/III           → Consider USS/MRI for RC tear,
                                        AC pathology, other diagnosis
     ↓
Determine clinical phase (Uhthoff)
  Resorptive (Gärtner III, severe pain) → USS-guided barbotage ± aspiration
  Formative/Resting (Gärtner I/II) → Conservative physiotherapy → ESWT if failure
```

### Treatment Decision Tree (HSC NI)

```
Diagnosis confirmed (X-ray)
     ↓
Phase 1: Conservative (weeks 0–12)
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs + paracetamol
  • Subacromial corticosteroid injection (acute pain / resorptive phase)
  • Supervised physiotherapy
     ↓
Reassess at 6–12 weeks (X-ray + outcome measures)
     ↓
     Improving                            Not improving / resorptive phase
        ↓                                          ↓
  Continue rehab +               ESWT (1–3 sessions) OR USS-guided
  reassess at 3 months           barbotage/aspiration (if Gärtner III)
                                           ↓
                                Reassess at 3 months post-intervention
                                           ↓
                                     Persistent symptoms
                                     Arthroscopic removal
                                     (rare; <10% of cases)
```

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Imaging Comparison

| Modality | Sensitivity | Specificity | Cost | First-Line? | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------------|------|-------------|-------|
| **Plain X-ray (AP + outlet)** | 66–93% | ~99% | Low | ✅ Yes | Mandatory first-line; identifies deposit, size, morphology (Gärtner classification) |
| **Ultrasound (USS)** | 79–98% | 88–99% | Low-Moderate | ✅ Yes (adjunct) | Dynamic assessment; guides intervention (barbotage); detects concurrent RC tear; operator-dependent |
| **MRI** | 70–82% | 98% | High | No (second-line) | Best for soft tissue; concurrent pathology (RC tear, AC joint); calcium may appear as signal void |
| **CT** | ~100% | ~100% | High/Radiation | No | Rarely needed; useful pre-op planning for large/complex deposits |

**Preferred imaging approach (HSC NI):**
1. **Plain X-ray** — confirm diagnosis, classify deposit (Gärtner I/II/III), measure size
2. **USS** — if intervention planned, or if concurrent RC pathology suspected (complementary to X-ray)
3. **MRI** — if X-ray/USS inconclusive, suspected full-thickness RC tear, or pre-surgical assessment

**Deposit characteristics associated with spontaneous resolution:**
- Gärtner Type III ('fluffy', poorly defined)
- Smaller deposits (<1.5 cm)
- Resorptive phase clinically (acute severe pain)

**Deposit characteristics predicting recalcitrance:**
- Gärtner Type I (dense, homogeneous, well-defined)
- Deposits >2 cm
- Long symptom duration (>12 months)
- Bursal-side deposits

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Differentiating Features | Key Tests | Imaging |
|-----------|--------------------------|-----------|---------|
| **Subacromial Impingement (without calcification)** | Painful arc; similar impingement signs; no sudden onset crisis; gradual onset | Neer, Hawkins-Kennedy, Empty Can | USS/MRI; X-ray normal or minor degenerative changes |
| **Rotator Cuff Tear (full-thickness)** | Weakness > pain (full thickness); Drop Arm positive; ER lag sign; older age | Drop Arm, ER Lag Sign, Belly Press | USS/MRI confirms; X-ray shows superior migration if massive |
| **Adhesive Capsulitis** | Global passive restriction (all planes); ER most restricted; capsular pattern | Capsular pattern ROM testing | MRI (capsular thickening, reduced joint volume); X-ray normal |
| **Septic Arthritis** | Fever, elevated CRP/ESR/WBC; erythema; systemically unwell; rapid onset | FBC/CRP/ESR, joint aspiration | USS shows effusion; X-ray may be normal early |
| **Acromioclavicular Joint Pathology** | Pain at AC joint; cross-body adduction positive; no calcium in supraspinatus | Cross-body adduction, AC palpation, O'Brien's (top pain) | X-ray AC views; USS |
| **Pseudogout (CPPD)** | Often older patient; knee/wrist also affected; synovial fluid analysis confirms | Joint aspiration — positive birefringent crystals | X-ray — chondrocalcinosis in fibrocartilage |
| **Cervical Radiculopathy (C5)** | Dermatomal radiation; neck movement provokes; reduced biceps reflex | Spurling's, ULTT, neurological exam | Cervical MRI |
| **Glenohumeral Osteoarthritis** | Older patient; global ROM loss; crepitus; stiffness > pain; end-stage | Passive ROM, crepitus | X-ray (joint space loss, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis) |
| **Reactive Arthritis** | Preceding infection; other joints affected; systemic features | HLA-B27, ESR/CRP, urine culture | X-ray; MRI |
| **Os Acromiale (unfused secondary ossification centre)** | Young adult; anterior acromial tenderness; acromion mobile; X-ray diagnostic | X-ray (axillary view critical) | CT for surgical planning |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC NI Pathway

> Aligned with **NICE guideline NG233 (Shoulder Pain, 2023)**, **BESS consensus statements**, and SIGN guidelines. All patients should be offered structured physiotherapy before escalation.

**Entry Point:** Community Physiotherapy / PCMSK MSK service

**Step 1 — Assessment & Imaging**
- Full MSK assessment; red flag screen
- Plain X-ray (if not already done) — confirm diagnosis and Gärtner classification
- Baseline outcome measures: SPADI + QuickDASH + NRS pain
- Determine clinical phase (resorptive vs. formative/resting)

**Step 2 — Conservative Management (Weeks 0–12)**
- All patients offered conservative physiotherapy + analgesia
- Review at 6 weeks with repeat outcome measures

**Step 3 — Injection/Intervention (if inadequate response at 6–12 weeks)**
- **Subacromial corticosteroid injection** (acute/resorptive phase — pain control)
- **USS-guided barbotage/aspiration** (resorptive phase, Gärtner III)
- **ESWT referral** (formative/resting phase, Gärtner I/II — conservative failure)

**Step 4 — Orthopaedic Referral**
- Failure of ≥3 months conservative treatment including ESWT/barbotage
- Significant quality of life impairment
- Concurrent full-thickness RC tear requiring surgical management

### Conservative Management

**Physiotherapy Principles:**

| Phase | Timeframe | Goals | Interventions |
|-------|-----------|-------|---------------|
| **Acute (Resorptive)** | Weeks 0–4 | Pain control; maintain ROM; patient education | Pendulum exercises; cryotherapy; gentle passive ROM; avoid provocative overhead loading |
| **Subacute** | Weeks 4–8 | Progressive ROM; begin RC strengthening | Active ROM; scapular stabilisation; isometric RC; posterior capsule stretching |
| **Functional** | Weeks 8–12+ | Full strength; return to function/work | Progressive resistance; proprioception; functional tasks; sport-specific if applicable |

**Manual Therapy:**
- Joint mobilisation (GH and thoracic) has low-level evidence as adjunct to exercise in subacromial pain — included in multimodal approach
- **Not recommended** as primary or standalone treatment

**Exercise:**
- Supervised exercise superior to home exercise alone for subacromial conditions
- Scapular stabilisation and rotator cuff strengthening are cornerstones
- Avoid provocative overhead loading in acute/resorptive phase

### Pharmacological

| Agent | Indication | Evidence | Dosing / Notes |
|-------|------------|----------|----------------|
| **Oral NSAIDs** (e.g., naproxen 500mg BD, ibuprofen 400mg TDS) | Acute/subacute pain control | Moderate | 7–14 day course; gastroprotection (PPI) if risk factors; contraindicated in renal/cardiac disease |
| **Paracetamol** 1g QDS | Background analgesia, adjunct | Low-Moderate | Safe; useful for patients unable to tolerate NSAIDs |
| **Subacromial Corticosteroid Injection** (e.g., methylprednisolone 40mg + local anaesthetic) | Acute pain (resorptive phase); short-term pain control | Moderate | Effective short-term (up to 6 weeks); not disease-modifying; max 2–3 injections/shoulder/year; 48-hr post-injection flare possible |
| **Codeine / Tramadol** | Acute severe pain (resorptive crisis); short-term only | Expert consensus | Maximum 5–7 days; constipation counselling; tramadol — interactions, serotonin syndrome risk |
| **Colchicine** | Acute crystal-induced inflammatory crisis | Limited evidence | Off-label use; some case series support; specialist/GP discretion |

### Interventional: ESWT (Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy)

**Mechanism:** Acoustic pressure waves → mechanical disruption of calcium deposits → stimulate phagocytic resorption + neovascularisation

**Evidence Summary:**

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| **Efficacy vs placebo** | Significant pain reduction and deposit resorption vs sham at 3–12 months (Level 1 evidence — multiple RCTs) |
| **Responders** | Gärtner Type I/II deposits respond better to ESWT than Type III (which is already resorbing) |
| **Protocol** | High-energy ESWT (0.28 mJ/mm²): 1–3 sessions, 1500–3000 impulses per session |
| **Deposit resorption** | Complete resorption in 30–66% at 12 months; partial resorption additional 25–30% |
| **SPADI improvement** | Mean SPADI improvement ~25–35 points vs ~5–10 points placebo |
| **NNT** | NNT for significant improvement ≈ 3–5 |
| **Key RCT** | Gerdesmeyer et al. (2003): High-energy ESWT vs sham; 85% success rate at 2 years |
| **Systematic review** | Louwerens et al. (2014): ESWT superior to placebo for symptom reduction and deposit resorption |
| **HSC NI access** | Via orthopaedic physiotherapy or orthopaedic department; not universally available in primary care |

**ESWT contraindications:** Coagulation disorders/anticoagulants, local infection, malignancy, pregnancy, pacemaker, bony deposits overlying neurovascular structures

### Interventional: USS-Guided Barbotage / Needling & Lavage

**Mechanism:** Direct mechanical disruption of calcium deposit via needle; lavage removes calcium debris; induces local inflammatory response to accelerate resorption.

**Evidence Summary:**

| Parameter | Data |
|-----------|------|
| **Efficacy** | Significant pain reduction; superior to corticosteroid injection alone (de Witte et al., 2012) |
| **Best phase** | Resorptive (Gärtner III) — calcium has toothpaste-like consistency; easier aspiration |
| **Technique** | USS guidance essential; 18G needle; 2-needle technique preferred (in/out); lavage with 0.9% NaCl; attempt aspiration of calcium |
| **Combined with corticosteroid** | Corticosteroid injection after barbotage standard practice for post-procedure flare management |
| **Key RCT** | Randomised trial (de Witte et al., 2012): barbotage + corticosteroid vs corticosteroid alone — ASES score superior in barbotage arm at 12 months |
| **Success rate** | 60–80% significant improvement at 12 months; deposit clearance in 30–70% |
| **HSC NI access** | Musculoskeletal radiologist or orthopaedic consultant; USS required; routine orthopaedic outpatient pathway |

### Surgical Management (Arthroscopic Removal)

**Indication:** Failure of ≥3–6 months conservative treatment (including ESWT and/or barbotage); significant ongoing disability.

**Procedure:** Arthroscopic debridement and removal of calcium deposit ± subacromial decompression (acromioplasty if hook acromion/subacromial narrowing)

**Evidence:**
- Success rate 85–90% at 2 years
- Acromioplasty added in ~70% of cases (Ark et al., 1992)
- Rarely required: <10% of patients with calcific tendinitis require surgery
- Short-stay day-case procedure; physiotherapy essential post-operatively

### Onward Referral Criteria (HSC NI)

| Urgency | Criteria |
|---------|----------|
| **Urgent (same day — A&E)** | Suspected septic arthritis; fever + erythema + hot joint |
| **Urgent (within 2 weeks)** | 2-week wait cancer pathway if malignancy suspected |
| **Expedited (within 4 weeks)** | Concurrent full-thickness RC tear confirmed on USS/MRI |
| **Routine Orthopaedic** | Failure of 12 weeks conservative treatment + 1 intervention (ESWT or barbotage); ongoing severe disability; SPADI remains >50 at 3 months |
| **Rheumatology** | Suspected systemic cause (bilateral CT, CPPD, RA, metabolic disorder) |
