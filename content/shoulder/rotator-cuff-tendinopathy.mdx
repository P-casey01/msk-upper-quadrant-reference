## Overview & Pathophysiology

Rotator cuff tendinopathy is a broad term encompassing pain and dysfunction arising from the rotator cuff tendons — most commonly the supraspinatus, followed by infraspinatus and subscapularis. It represents one of the most prevalent musculoskeletal conditions in clinical practice, accounting for 44–65% of shoulder pain presentations in primary care.

**Prevalence:** Point prevalence of shoulder pain is approximately 7–26% in the general population, with rotator cuff pathology implicated in 65–70% of cases. Prevalence increases sharply with age, particularly after 50 years.

### Continuum Model of Tendinopathy (Cook & Purdam, 2009; revised 2016)

The continuum model — now the dominant explanatory framework — describes tendinopathy as a spectrum rather than a discrete entity:

1. **Reactive tendinopathy:** Acute response to compressive or tensile overload. Non-inflammatory cell-mediated matrix changes. Clinically: sudden onset after unaccustomed loading. Potentially reversible.
2. **Tendon dysrepair:** Failed or incomplete healing response. Disorganised matrix, increased ground substance and vascularity. Clinically: subacute/chronic presentation.
3. **Degenerative tendinopathy:** Cell death, lipid infiltration, calcification, disorganised collagen. High risk of tear. Clinically: chronic, often in older patients.

**Key principle:** Tendinopathy is a failed healing response, not classic inflammation. This drives management decisions — compressive loading and prolonged rest are counterproductive; progressive tensile loading is the evidence-based intervention.

### Relevant Anatomy

- **Supraspinatus:** Arises from the supraspinous fossa, inserts on the greater tuberosity (superior facet). Primary abductor and force-couple stabiliser.
- **Infraspinatus:** External rotator; inserts on greater tuberosity (middle facet).
- **Subscapularis:** Internal rotator; inserts on lesser tuberosity.
- **Teres minor:** External rotator; inserts on greater tuberosity (inferior facet).
- **Critical zone:** The avascular zone 1 cm from the supraspinatus insertion — primary site of degenerative change and tears.
- **Subacromial space:** Normal space 7–14 mm; reduction causes impingement on the bursa and rotator cuff.

### Mechanism

Multifactorial — intrinsic and extrinsic factors:

- **Intrinsic:** Age-related tendon degeneration, vascularity compromise, genetic predisposition (COL5A1 gene variants), tendon morphology.
- **Extrinsic:** Repetitive overhead activity, heavy lifting, acromion morphology (Bigliani Type II/III), scapular dyskinesis, altered glenohumeral kinematics, poor rotator cuff strength/endurance.
- **Compressive loading** at the subacromial space is now recognised as a key perpetuating factor, particularly for supraspinatus tendinopathy.

**Clinical pearl — Cluster testing for rotator cuff tendinopathy:**
Hegedus et al. (2012) systematic review confirms no single test is diagnostic. Use clusters:
- Hawkins-Kennedy + Painful Arc + Infraspinatus muscle test (3-positive cluster): +LR 10.56 for subacromial impingement syndrome (Michener et al., 2009)
- Sensitivity maximised by Hawkins-Kennedy; specificity maximised by Painful Arc and muscle testing

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Possible Diagnosis | Action |
|----------|-------------------|--------|
| Night pain disproportionate to activity, unexplained weight loss, age >50 + new onset | Malignancy (primary or secondary — Pancoast tumour, bone metastasis) | Urgent referral; chest X-ray |
| Fever, hot swollen joint, systemically unwell | Septic arthritis | Emergency assessment; joint aspiration |
| History of significant trauma + severe weakness | Full-thickness rotator cuff tear; fracture | X-ray; urgent orthopaedic review |
| Left shoulder/jaw pain, diaphoresis, dyspnoea | Cardiac event (referred pain) | Emergency — 999 |
| Upper shoulder/neck pain after diaphragmatic irritation (e.g., post-surgical, haemothorax) | Diaphragmatic referred pain (C3/C4) | Urgent medical review |
| Rapidly progressive neurological deficit | Brachial neuritis (Parsonage-Turner syndrome) | Urgent neurology referral |
| Progressive weakness without pain | Cervical myelopathy; neurological cause | Urgent neurology/spinal referral |
| Axillary nerve deficit post-dislocation | Axillary nerve injury | EMG/NCS; monitor 3–6 months |
| Wasting of supraspinatus/infraspinatus fossa | Suprascapular nerve entrapment; space-occupying lesion | MRI; neurology referral |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Lewis Shoulder Framework (2009, updated 2015)
Jeremy Lewis's clinical framework for shoulder assessment emphasises:
1. **Symptom behaviour** — directional preference, aggravating/relieving factors
2. **Movement assessment** — active ROM, painful arc, end-range pain
3. **Load assessment** — rotator cuff strength testing (ER, IR, abduction, flexion)
4. **Differentiation testing** — supine positioning to offload subacromial space
5. **Treatment-based classification** — responds better than diagnosis-based for directing exercise loading

### Cools Rehabilitation Framework (2016)
Ann Cools's evidence-based rehabilitation model for shoulder:
1. **Phase 1 — Pain management:** Activity modification, posture correction, scapular control
2. **Phase 2 — Motor control:** Low-load rotator cuff activation, scapular stabilisers
3. **Phase 3 — Strength:** Progressive loading, ER/IR ratio restoration (target ER:IR ≥ 0.66)
4. **Phase 4 — Function:** Sport/work-specific loading, overhead mechanics

### Cluster Testing Approach (Hegedus et al., 2012)
- Never rely on a single test
- Combine high-sensitivity tests (rule out) with high-specificity tests (rule in)
- Assess response to load — does isometric contraction reproduce/resolve pain?
- Musculoskeletal ultrasound is the recommended first-line imaging in most guidelines

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Imaging Guidelines

**Ultrasound (USS):**
- First-line imaging per BESS (British Elbow and Shoulder Society) and NICE guidelines
- Sensitivity for full-thickness RC tear: 92% (95% CI 82–97%); Specificity: 97% (95% CI 91–99%) — de Jesus et al., 2009
- Sensitivity for partial-thickness tear: 67%; Specificity: 94%
- Operator-dependent; limited assessment of labrum and glenohumeral joint

**MRI:**
- Preferred for labral pathology, bony assessment, surgical planning
- Sensitivity full-thickness tear: 94%; Specificity: 93% — de Jesus et al., 2009
- MR arthrogram for suspected partial-thickness tear, labral tears, instability

**X-ray:**
- Limited for soft tissue; useful for: calcific deposits, acromioclavicular joint, glenohumeral arthritis, acromion morphology, greater tuberosity irregularity
- AP + axillary views minimum

### Key Systematic Reviews
- **Hegedus et al. (2012)** — BJSM: Most comprehensive meta-analysis of shoulder special tests. Conclusion: No single test has sufficient diagnostic accuracy; clusters recommended.
- **Lenza et al. (2013)** — Cochrane: USS and MRI comparable for full-thickness tears; MRI superior for partial tears.
- **Diercks et al. (2014)** — BESS: USS first-line before MRI for suspected RC pathology.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Differentiating Features | Special Test | Imaging |
|-----------|------------------------------|--------------|---------|
| Subacromial pain syndrome | Painful arc 60–120°; pain with overhead activities; negative lag signs | Hawkins + Neer positive; muscle tests variable | USS — bursal thickening ± RC changes |
| Full-thickness RC tear | Significant weakness; may have lag signs | Positive lag signs; Drop Arm | USS/MRI — confirms tear |
| Adhesive capsulitis | Global ROM restriction; capsular pattern (ER > abduction > IR) | All movements restricted | X-ray ± USS |
| Glenohumeral arthritis | Age; morning stiffness; crepitus; gradual onset | Restricted capsular pattern | X-ray — joint space loss |
| Cervical radiculopathy | Arm symptoms; neurological signs; neck movement reproduces arm pain | Spurling's positive; ULTT positive | Cervical MRI |
| Acromioclavicular joint pathology | Superior shoulder pain; direct AC joint tenderness | O'Brien's; Cross-arm adduction | X-ray; USS |
| Biceps tendinopathy/tear | Anterior shoulder pain; Speed's; Yergason's | Speed's + Yergason's | USS — biceps tendon |
| Calcific tendinitis | Severe acute pain; may mimic infection | Often all tests painful | X-ray — calcific deposits |
| Shoulder instability | Age <35; history of trauma or hyperlaxity; apprehension | Apprehension + Relocation; load and shift | MRI arthrogram |
| Thoracic outlet syndrome | Vascular/neurological symptoms; positional | Roos; Adson's; Wright's | Doppler USS; CXR; MRI |
| Brachial neuritis (Parsonage-Turner) | Acute severe pain followed by rapid weakness; post-viral/vaccination | Wasting without impingement | MRI neurography |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care / GP:**
- Physiotherapy self-referral available across most HSC Trusts (Belfast, South Eastern, Northern, Western, Southern)
- First 6 weeks: conservative management in primary care
- Image-guided corticosteroid injection: referral to GP with special interest (GPwSI) in MSK or MSK interface clinic
- MSK interface clinics (e.g., Belfast Trust MSK First Contact Practitioner): triage, USS, injection, physiotherapy — avoiding unnecessary orthopaedic referral

**MSK Interface Services (HSC NI):**
- First Contact Practitioners (FCPs) in GP practices (expanding across all trusts 2023–2026)
- Community MSK services: offer USS-guided injection, exercise prescription, structured rehabilitation
- Waiting times (indicative 2024–2025): Community physio 6–20 weeks; MSK interface 8–16 weeks; orthopaedic outpatient 52+ weeks

**Red Flag / Urgent Pathway:**
- Suspected septic arthritis → Emergency Department
- Suspected malignancy → 2-week wait urgent cancer referral
- Significant trauma with weakness → ED/direct orthopaedic assessment

### Conservative Management

**Evidence-based exercise prescription:**

| Phase | Timeframe | Exercise Focus | Dosage | Evidence |
|-------|-----------|----------------|--------|----------|
| Acute (reactive) | 0–4 weeks | Pain-free isometrics; posture; scapular control | 3×10–15 reps, daily | Moderate (RCT) |
| Sub-acute | 4–8 weeks | Isotonic ER/IR; scapular stabilisers; progressive load | 3×12–15 reps, 3×/week | High (multiple RCTs) |
| Rehabilitation | 8–16 weeks | Functional strengthening; eccentric loading; overhead mechanics | 3×8–12 reps progressive | High (Cochrane) |
| Return to activity | 16–24 weeks | Sport/work-specific; plyometric if relevant | Graded exposure | Moderate |

**Key exercise principles:**
- **Eccentric loading** — most evidence base for patellar tendinopathy; emerging evidence for shoulder (Level II)
- **Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR)** — Beyer et al. (2015) AJSM: HSR comparable to eccentric; better adherence
- **ER:IR strength ratio** target ≥ 0.66 at 60°/s isokinetic testing
- **Scapular stabilisation:** Serratus anterior activation; lower/middle trapezius; avoid upper trapezius dominance

**Manual therapy:**
- Thoracic manipulation: reduces shoulder pain and improves ROM — moderate evidence (Haik et al., 2016)
- Glenohumeral joint mobilisation (posterior capsule): Grade III–IV for capsular restriction
- Soft tissue release: limited high-quality evidence as standalone intervention

**Education:**
- Activity modification (not rest); load management principles; graded return
- Posture correction; ergonomic advice (particularly for occupational factors)

### Pharmacological

Per **NICE guideline NG194 (Shoulder Pain, 2021)**:

- **NSAIDs:** Topical first-line if tolerable; oral NSAIDs — minimum effective dose for shortest duration; gastroprotection with PPI if ≥45 years or risk factors
- **Corticosteroid injection:**
  - Short-term benefit (up to 6 weeks) — Level I evidence (Cochrane: Buchbinder et al., 2003)
  - No benefit over placebo at 12 months
  - Limit to 3 injections per shoulder per year (HSC NI / NICE guidance)
  - USS-guided preferred over landmark-guided (Daniels et al., 2018 — superior accuracy)
  - Subacute presentations respond better than chronic
  - Caution: repeated injections associated with tendon weakening (Nichols, 2005)
- **Analgesics:** Paracetamol — limited evidence for musculoskeletal pain; avoid opioids for non-cancer MSK pain (NICE)
- **Hyaluronic acid:** Not recommended routinely — insufficient evidence (NICE NG194)
- **Platelet-rich plasma (PRP):** Growing evidence base; not yet NICE-recommended for shoulder tendinopathy; commissioning decisions pending NICE review

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Orthopaedic referral indicated:**
- Failed 3–6 months of structured conservative management (documented)
- Significant or progressive rotator cuff weakness suggesting large/massive tear
- Age <40 with acute traumatic onset suggesting structural tear
- Positive lag signs with functional deficit impacting work/quality of life
- Persistent symptoms after 2 USS-guided injections
- Suspected calcific tendinitis unresponsive to conservative care (consider US-guided needling)
- Glenohumeral joint pathology requiring surgical assessment

**Imaging prior to referral (HSC NI):**
- USS arranged by GP or MSK service prior to orthopaedic referral in most trusts
- MRI where USS inconclusive or labral/instability pathology suspected
