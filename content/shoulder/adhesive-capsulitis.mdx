## Overview & Pathophysiology

Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder) is a painful, progressive condition characterised by diffuse fibrosis and contracture of the glenohumeral joint capsule, resulting in significant restriction of active and passive range of motion in a capsular pattern. It is a distinct clinical entity from other shoulder conditions and follows a broadly predictable natural history.

**Prevalence:**
- Lifetime prevalence: 2–5% of the general population
- Peak incidence: 40–60 years (rare <40 or >70)
- Female preponderance: approximately 60–70% female
- Second shoulder affected in 20–30% of patients (usually contralateral; simultaneous bilateral uncommon)

**Associated Conditions:**

| Condition | Relative Risk / Prevalence |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Diabetes mellitus (type 1 and 2) | 3–5× increased risk; prevalence 10–20% in frozen shoulder |
| Thyroid disease (hypo- and hyperthyroid) | 2–3× increased risk |
| Dupuytren's contracture | Associated fibrotic diathesis |
| Parkinson's disease | Increased risk; may be early feature |
| Cardiac disease / post-cardiac surgery | Prolonged immobility |
| Post-stroke ipsilateral shoulder | Hemiplegia + immobility |
| Cervical disc disease | Neurogenic inflammation proposed |
| Post-operative immobilisation | Rotator cuff repair; mastectomy |
| Hyperlipidaemia | Emerging association |

### Pathophysiology

The pathological process involves:

1. **Inflammatory phase:** Synovial inflammation with fibroblast proliferation; increased vascularity; cytokine release (IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6)
2. **Fibrotic phase:** Dense fibrocollagenous tissue formation; capsule thickening (normal 2–4 mm → up to 7–10 mm in contracted phase)
3. **Key site:** Rotator cuff interval (between supraspinatus and subscapularis) — contracture here produces external rotation restriction first
4. **Axillary recess obliteration:** Normal joint volume 28–30 mL reduced to <10 mL; arthrographic finding is diagnostic
5. **Contractile myofibroblasts:** Responsible for progressive capsular shortening; similar mechanism to Dupuytren's

### Clinical Stages (Reeves, 1975 — validated by Hannafin & Chiaia, 2000)

| Stage | Name | Duration | Key Features |
|-------|------|----------|--------------|
| **Stage 1** | Freezing (Painful) | 2–9 months | Gradual onset severe pain; pain at rest and night; ROM begins to restrict |
| **Stage 2** | Frozen (Stiffening) | 4–12 months | Pain decreasing; ROM increasingly restricted; significant disability |
| **Stage 3** | Thawing (Resolution) | 5–26 months | Pain minimal; ROM gradually returning; slow recovery |
| **Total** | Full natural history | 12–42 months | Variable; not all patients fully resolve |

**Prognosis:** 
- 20–50% of patients have persistent symptoms or restriction at 2–3 years without treatment
- Diabetic patients: significantly worse prognosis; longer duration; less complete resolution
- Natural history is broadly positive but prolonged — active management improves speed of recovery

## Red Flags

| Red Flag | Concern | Action |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Onset <40 years without diabetes or trauma | Alternative diagnosis — instability; malignancy | Full assessment; MRI |
| Rapid onset with constitutional symptoms | Septic arthritis; malignancy | Emergency evaluation; bloods |
| Severe unrelenting pain out of proportion | Malignancy; inflammatory arthritis; Reflex Sympathetic Dystrophy | Urgent investigation |
| Associated skin changes, vasomotor changes | Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS) | Specialist referral |
| Progressive neurological deficit | Brachial neuritis (Parsonage-Turner); cervical myelopathy | Urgent neurology |
| Bilateral simultaneous onset | Inflammatory arthritis; systemic condition | Rheumatology referral |
| Failure to progress through stages | Malignancy; missed alternative diagnosis | Re-evaluate; imaging |
| Paget's disease features | Bone pain; deformity; elevated ALP | Bloods; X-ray |

## Clinical Frameworks

### Hannafin & Chiaia Staging System (2000)
Correlates histological findings with clinical stages:
- **Stage 1:** Scattered hypervascular synovium; fibrinous exudate
- **Stage 2:** Hypertrophic synovitis; early fibroplasia
- **Stage 3:** Mild synovitis; dense subsynovial fibrosis; collagen remodelling
- **Stage 4:** Mature collagen; avascular hypocellular tissue; minimal synovitis

### Treatment Decision Framework Based on Stage:

| Stage | Dominant Problem | Priority Intervention | Caution |
|-------|-----------------|----------------------|---------|
| Stage 1 (Painful) | Pain; sleep disruption | Pain management; corticosteroid injection; gentle ROM | Avoid aggressive stretching — worsens inflammation |
| Stage 2 (Frozen) | Stiffness; functional limitation | ROM exercises; joint mobilisation; hydrodilatation | Continue pain management |
| Stage 3 (Thawing) | Persistent restriction | Progressive strengthening; capsular stretching | Normal loading; return to function |

### Biopsychosocial Considerations
- Prolonged pain and disability → psychological impact; depression screening (PHQ-9) in chronic cases
- Impact on work and activities of daily living — occupational advice important
- Fear-avoidance behaviours can slow recovery — address early

## Evidence-Based Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

Frozen shoulder remains a clinical diagnosis based on:
1. Characteristic history (gradual onset pain; progressive stiffness)
2. Capsular pattern restriction on passive examination
3. Exclusion of other pathology

**Arthrography:** Historically gold standard — capsular volume <10–12 mL and loss of axillary recess. Now largely replaced.

**USS:**
- Supports diagnosis by excluding structural pathology (RC tear, calcific tendinitis)
- May show thickened rotator cuff interval; increased blood flow (power Doppler)
- Cannot confirm capsular volume reduction directly

**MRI:**
- Thickened rotator cuff interval capsule; enhancing synovium on gadolinium
- Differentiates from glenohumeral OA; identifies associated pathology
- Not routinely required for clinical diagnosis

**X-ray:** Usually normal in primary frozen shoulder — important to exclude glenohumeral OA, calcific tendinitis, malignancy

### Key Evidence for Management:

**Corticosteroid injection:**
- Buchbinder et al. Cochrane 2003; Carette et al. (2003) NEJM: Short-term benefit (up to 6 weeks) — Level I
- Intra-articular corticosteroid superior to physiotherapy alone at 6 weeks; no difference at 12 months
- Best in Stage 1 (freezing) — reduces inflammatory component and provides window for rehabilitation

**Hydrodilatation (arthrographic distension):**
- Valadie et al. (2000); Buchbinder et al. (2004) BMJ: Significant short-term benefit over placebo
- Cochrane (Maund et al., 2012): Hydrodilatation + steroid superior to steroid alone at 6 weeks
- Performed under fluoroscopic or USS guidance — 30–40 mL saline + corticosteroid + local anaesthetic
- Available via MSK interface services in some HSC NI trusts

**Manipulation Under Anaesthesia (MUA):**
- Evidence: retrospective series; no high-quality RCTs
- Effective for Stage 2/3 — breaks down adhesions; risk of fracture, nerve injury, haemarthrosis
- Post-MUA physiotherapy essential — begin same day

**Arthroscopic capsular release:**
- For refractory cases (>6 months no improvement); excellent outcomes reported (>90% satisfaction)
- Systematic review (Omari & Bunker, 2001): superior to MUA for recovery speed
- Available at NI orthopaedic centres

## Differential Diagnosis

| Diagnosis | Key Differentiating Features | Differentiating Assessment |
|-----------|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Glenohumeral osteoarthritis | Older age; crepitus; X-ray changes; gradual onset | X-ray — joint space loss, osteophytes |
| Rotator cuff tear with secondary stiffness | Lag signs; weakness; USS/MRI confirms tear | USS — full-thickness tear |
| Subacromial pain syndrome | Painful arc; strength variable; NOT globally restricted | ROM testing — not capsular pattern |
| Calcific tendinitis | Acute severe pain; X-ray calcification; moves through | X-ray — calcification |
| Glenohumeral inflammatory arthritis (RA/crystal) | Bilateral; systemic features; inflammatory bloods | Bloods (RF, anti-CCP, urate); X-ray |
| Shoulder impingement + secondary guarding | Painful arc; resisted tests positive; ROM pain-limited not structurally restricted | Passive ROM under distraction/supine |
| Avascular necrosis (AVN) | History steroids, alcohol, haemoglobinopathy; deep aching | MRI — AVN signal |
| Posterior shoulder dislocation (missed) | Trauma; fixed IR; limited ER; BEWARE | X-ray (axillary view essential); CT |
| Complex Regional Pain Syndrome | Vasomotor; trophic changes; allodynia; disproportionate | Specialist assessment |
| Cervical radiculopathy | Neck symptoms; referred pain; neurological signs | Cervical exam; ULTT; Spurling's |

## Management & Treatment

### HSC Northern Ireland Pathway

**Primary Care:**
- GP: clinical diagnosis; X-ray (exclude OA/calcific); blood glucose/HbA1c (exclude/diagnose diabetes)
- Intra-articular corticosteroid injection (landmark or USS-guided): available via GP or GPwSI
- Referral to community physiotherapy for Stage 1 (education, pain management) or Stage 2/3 (ROM exercises, mobilisation)

**MSK Interface (HSC NI):**
- USS-guided injection (intra-articular methylprednisolone/triamcinolone + lidocaine)
- Hydrodilatation: available in some trusts via radiology or MSK service under fluoroscopy/USS guidance
- Belfast Trust: orthopaedic MSK interface; South Eastern Trust: community injection service
- Waiting times: community physio 6–20 weeks; MSK interface 8–16 weeks; orthopaedic 12–26+ months

**Orthopaedic pathway:**
- Refractory cases (>6 months no meaningful progression)
- MUA + post-operative physiotherapy
- Arthroscopic capsular release (Musgrave Park Hospital; Altnagelvin; Antrim)

### Conservative Management

**Stage 1 (Painful/Freezing) — Priority: pain management:**
- Education: condition, stages, natural history, prognosis
- Sleep position advice (pillow under arm; avoid lying on affected side)
- Avoid aggressive stretching — counterproductive in Stage 1
- Gentle pendulum exercises; active-assisted ROM in pain-free range
- Pain-free active exercises (isometric, low-load)
- Early injection referral for pain management

**Stage 2 (Frozen/Stiffening) — Priority: ROM restoration:**

| Technique | Evidence | Dosage |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Passive ROM exercises | Moderate | 3×10 reps, 3×/day within pain tolerance |
| Hold-relax (contract-relax) stretching | Moderate | 5 sec contract; 10 sec passive stretch; 3× each direction |
| Glenohumeral joint mobilisation (Grade III–IV) | Moderate | Posterior capsule; inferior glide |
| Physiotherapy-supervised stretching programme | High | 2–3× per week supervised + home programme |
| Codman's (pendulum) exercises | Low-moderate | Daily |

**Stage 3 (Thawing/Resolution) — Priority: function and strength:**
- Progressive strengthening: rotator cuff; scapular stabilisers
- Return to overhead activities: graded exposure
- Occupational/work activities — graded return
- Prognosis counselling — complete resolution may take 2+ years

### Pharmacological

**NICE NG194 alignment:**
- **NSAIDs:** Topical first-line; oral NSAIDs for acute severe pain — minimum dose/duration; PPI cover
- **Simple analgesia:** Paracetamol for mild-moderate pain
- **Corticosteroid injection (intra-articular):**
  - Most effective intervention for Stage 1 (freezing)
  - Carette et al. (2003) NEJM: intra-articular triamcinolone 40 mg superior to physiotherapy alone at 6 weeks
  - Repeat injection at 6 weeks if partial response — up to 3 per year
  - USS-guided preferred for accuracy
  - **Hydrodilatation:** Combine with corticosteroid for enhanced benefit (Buchbinder et al. 2004)
- **Avoid:** Opioids; gabapentinoids (insufficient evidence for CRPS-mimicking shoulder pain)

### Onward Referral Criteria

**Routine orthopaedic referral:**
- Failed 6 months of structured conservative management including at least 2 injections
- Stage 2 with no meaningful ROM improvement after 3 months of supervised physiotherapy
- Diabetic frozen shoulder (worse prognosis — earlier surgical referral threshold)
- Patient preference for MUA or arthroscopic release after full discussion

**Pre-referral minimum:**
- X-ray (glenohumeral AP + axillary view)
- HbA1c/blood glucose
- Documented physiotherapy trial with ROM measurements
- At least 1 image-guided corticosteroid injection
